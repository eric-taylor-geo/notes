# Volcanism, the atmosphere and climate change through time

## 13.3 Volcanic gas emissions

- Water vapour and CO2 are the most abundant volatile species released during a volcanic eruption
- However, their effect in the short term on atmospheric composition is negligible due to their insignificant relative contribution to their high atmospheric concentrations.
	- On longer timescales, outgassing has been a source
- EMission of halogen species (bromine oxide, hydrogren chloride, hydrogen bromide, hydrogen flurodie) plays an important role in plume chemisty [[von Glasow, 2010]]
- Sulphur dioxid is the sole volatile species that has been observed to alter the radiative balance of the atmosphere through it conversion to volcanic sulfuric acid aerosol
- Sulphur species account for 2-35% of the volume of the gas phase - SO2 and H2S are most abundnat
- They rapidly oxidise in the troposphere and have a short lifetime (hours to days)
- In the stratosphere, the lifetime increases significantly:
- SO2 is removed via dry and wet deposition or oxidation to form the sulfuric acid aerosol (not available in the stratosphere)

## 13.4 Atmospheric and climatic impacts of volcanic aerosol

- Ash doesn't really do much and falls out of the atmosphere within minutes to days
	- much larger eruptions (VEI >=8) may induce short-term climate change through ash impacts
- Volcanic sulfuric acid aerosol particles alter the radiative balance of the Earth on timescales relevant for climate change
- Scatters radiation across the entire solar spectrum due to its optical properties and typical particle radius of 0.5 micrometres
	- There is some absorption in the near-infrared spectrum
- E fold time:
	- 9-12 months for tropical eruptions
	- 2-4 for high-latitude eruptions [[Kravitz and Robock, 2011]]
- Top of stratospheric aerosol cloud absorbs solar radiation in the near-infrared 
- Lower atmosphere absorbs from upward thermal infrared radiation [[Stenchikov et al., 1998]]
- Aerosol cloud also forward scatters some incoming solar radiation, enhancing downward diffuse radiation, increasing the sky's brightness, which can enhance vegetation growth and CO2 sink
- Tropical eruptions enhance pole-to-equator heating gradient in the lower stratosphere - creates a stronger polar vertex and a positive mode of the Arctic oscillation in tropospheric circulation [[Kirchner et al., 1999]]
	- Results in warming over NA, NE, and Russia and cooling over the ME
- Indirect advective effect on temperature is stronger than the direct effect that dominates at the lower latitudes and during summer
- Therefore, the season of eruption is important for assessing the magnitude of the climatic response - especially for high latitude eruptions [[Schmidt et al., 2010]]
- Aerosol indirect effects:
	- Can act as cloud nuclei (ice) in the stratosphere or genereal CCN in the troposhere which alters cloud amount and albedo - thereby modifying the optical properties and lifetimes of clouds

- The latitude and season of high magnitude eruptions influences the magnitude of an eruption's impact on the Earth's climate.
- In any case, simply extrapolating the magnitude of an eruption to the impact on the climate is flawed due to the non-linear relationship between magnitude and climatic effect.
