## Atmospheric Stability

- Air is stable when, after being lifted or lowered, it returns to its original position.
- Air that is in unstable equilibrium will, when given a little push, move further away from its original position

**Rising Parcel of air**

- Begins at the Earth's surface where it has the same temperature and air pressure as the air surrounding it
- As it rises, air pressure decreases; therefore, the parcel expands
- The expansion results in a loss of energy, lowering the temperature.
- If there is no interchange of heat with its surroundings, the situation is called an **adiabatic** process
- So long as the air in the parcel is unsaturated (RH < 100%), the rate of adiabatic cooling or warming remains constant
- When air temperature reaches the dew point, condensation occurs, releasing latent heat which offsets some of the cooling due to expansion. Therefore, the air no longer cools at the dry adiabatic lapse rate but at a lesser rate - the moist adiabatic lapse rate
- This lapse rate is not constant and varies greatly with temperature

**Determining stability**

- If rising air is denser than its environment then it will sink back to its original level - the air is stable
- If the rising air is less dense then it will continue to rise until it reaches the same density as its environment - the air is unstable
- Therefore, to determine air's stability, it requires the measurement of both the temperature of the rising air as well as the temperature gradient of its environment
**Stable**
- When the environmental lapse rate is less than the moist adiabatic lapse rate, the atmosphere is **absolutely stable**
- If air is forced to rise in an absolutely stable atmosphere, it will tend to spread out horizontally. If clouds form in this rising air, they will also spread out horizontally into relatively thin layers and have flat tops and bases - e.g., stratiform clouds -> cirrostratus, altostratus, nimbostratus
- If we consider the environmental lapse rate as a simple gradient between surface air and aloft air, then stable conditions can be created when the difference in temperature between the two is relatively small which can occur when:
	- Aloft air warms or is replaced by warm air or surface air cools or is replaced by cooler air
- Cooling of surface air can be due to:
	- Nighttime radiative cooling of the surface
	- An influx of cold surface air brought by cold advection
	- Air moving over a cold surface
- Therefore, the atmosphere is usually most stable in the early morning around sunrise when the lowest surface air temperatures are recorded.
- If surface air becomes saturated in a stable atmosphere, a layer of haze or fog can form
- The atmosphere can also become stable when an entire layer of air sinks, causing the sinking layer to warm adiabatically through compression
- As the layer subsides, it becomes compressed by the weight of the atmosphere and shrinks vertically
- As a result of this shrinkage, the upper layer sinks further and warms more - after this subsidence, the top layer can become warmer than the bottom, resulting in inversion. The atmosphere therefore becomes stable
- Inversions therefore act as a lid on vertical air motion, keeping systems near the ground.
- When the environmental lapse rate is equal to the moist adiabatic lapse rate, the atmosphere is neutrally stable.

**Unstable**
- Absolute instability occurs when the environmental lapse rate is greater than the dry adiabatic lapse rate
- Conditional instability occurs when the environmental lapse rate is between the moist adiabatic rate and the dry adiabatic rate
- Instability occurs when the environmental lapse rate steepens which can occur by the air aloft becoming colder or the surface air becoming warmer
- Cooling of the air aloft can occur when:
	- Winds bring in colder air through cold advection
	- Clouds cool radiatively
- Warming of the air surface through:
	- Daytime solar heating of the surface
	- Influx of warm air through warm advection
	- Air moving over a warm surface
- From these we can see that the atmosphere's stability fluctuates throughout the day
- Maximum instability typically occurs during the hottest part of the day
- Air can also become unstable through mixing or lifting.
- Mixing through convection or wind-induced turbulent eddies results in the cooling and heating of rising air. Up and down motion redistributes the air resulting in the temperature at the top decreasing and the temperature at the base increasing - this steepens the environmental lapse rate, increasing instability
- If mixing continues for some time and the air remains unsaturated, the vertical temperature gradient will become the dry adiabatic lapse rate
- ![[Pasted image 20250209220144.png]]
- Just as the lowering of an entire layer of air makes it more stable, instability occurs when the reverse occurs
- A stable air layer may be converted into an absolutely unstable layer when the lower portion of a layer is moist and the upper portion is dry. 
- If forced to rise, the top will cool at the dry adiabatic lapse rate and the bottom will cool at the moist adiabatic lapse rate -> results in a temperature inversion creating absolute instability

- The potential instability brought about by the lifting of a stable layer whos surface face is humid and top is dry is called convective instability which is associated with the development of severe storms

## Cloud Development

- Rising can occur through:
	- Surface heating and free convection
	- Uplift along topography
	- Widespread ascent due to the convergence of surface air
	- Uplift along weather fronts

**Convection and Clouds**

- Some areas of the Earth's surface are better absorbers of sunlight than others, and therefore, heat up more quickly
- The air over these hotspots becomes warmer and therefore rises.
- As it rises it mixes with the cooler air around it and it gradually loses it identity, slowing its upward movement
- If the rising air cools to its saturation point, the moisture will condense, and the thermal becomes visible as a cumulus cloud
- Downward motions are in part caused by the evaporation of the outer edge of the cloud which absorbs latent heat, cooling it.
- Therefore this results in a convective system which creates small, separated, cumulus clouds
- As clouds grow, they shade the ground from the sun which cuts off surface heating and upwards convection. Therefore, without the continual supply of rising air, the cloud erodes as its droplets evaporate.
- As the cloud dissipates or is advected, the surface heating begins and regenerates another thermal, causing a new cloud to form
- This is why cumulus clouds often form, gradually disappear and then reform in the same spot - yooo this is so obvious but never thought of!!

- Air motions of cloud formation are complex, several assumptions made:
	1. No mixing takes place between the rising air and its surroundings
	2. Only a single thermal produces a cumulus cloud
	3. The cloud forms when the RH is 100%
	4. The rising air in the cloud remains saturated

**Changing Cloud Forms**

- Clouds are good absorbers and emitters of infrared radiation.
- The tops of clouds will often cool - emitting more radiation than they recieve
- The bottoms of clouds will often warm as they absorb emitted longwave radiation from the surface
- This alters the environmental lapse rate and makes the cloud layer conditionally unstable, which causes convection cells to occur within the cloud itself - creates globular cloud elements
- If the wind is uniform throughout the cloud layer, these elements will typically be evenly distributed
- Else, if the wind changes with height, the horizontal axes of the convection cells align with the average direction of the wind which creates arranged rows of clouds.
