- Sun-synchronous
- Equator crossing time at 9:37 am mean local time
- Mean orbital altitude: 695 km
- Revisit time of 16 days

https://terra.nasa.gov/about/terras-orbit-changes

- Prior: equator crossing time of 10:30 am
- Since 2020, Terra has been drifting to an earlier equator crossing time, and in October 2022, it was lowed by 5 km in altitude to make room for new LEO missions.
- Changes to orbit did not reduce the data quality and only resulted in minor changes to orbital repeat time and swath width

## Instruments:

**CERES**
https://terra.nasa.gov/about/terra-instruments/ceres
- Clouds and the Earth's Radiant Energy System
- two identical CERES instruments
- One operates in a cross-track scan mode and the other in a biaxial scane mode

**MISR**
https://terra.nasa.gov/about/terra-instruments/misr
- Multi-angle Imaging SectroRadiometer
- Camera not only toward the nadir but others to produce various viewing angles of 26.1°, 45.6°, 60.0°, and 70.5 
- Four wavelengths: RBG and Near IR
- Various angles improve understanding of how sunlight is scattered in different directions
- MISR can also distinguish between different types of clouds, aerosols, and surfaces. 
- Can monitor long-term trends in:
	- Amount and type of atmospheric aerosol particles, natural and anthropogenic
	- Amount, types, and heights of clouds
	- Distribution of land surface cover, including vegetation canopy structure

**MOPITT**
https://terra.nasa.gov/about/terra-instruments/mopitt
- Measurement of Pollution in the Troposphere
- Focuses on the distribution, transport, sources, and sinks of CO in the troposphere.
- CO hinders the atmosphere's natural ability to rid itself of pollutants, therefore it can be used as a proxy
- Spatial resolution of 22km at nadir. Swath of 640 km
- It can measure concentrations of CO in 5 km layers through the atmosphere's vertical column
