Flow development is dependent on the advection of flow itself, the coriolis force, the pressure gradient force, the gravitational force, and frictional force. Such flow is called a gradient wind (Lynch and Cassano, 2006). Because the flow development is dependent on the flow itself, flow development is non-linear, allowing for the fast evolution of slow even with a small perturbation. Cyclonic and anti-cyclonic display different types of flow development due to the balances of forces within each. This essay will evaluate these differences using the concept of cyclostrophic balance. To ease the attribution of the direction of the Coriolis force, this essay will consider forces in the Northern hemisphere.

Cyclostrophic balance considers the centrifugal force in addition to the pressure gradient and coriolis forces when considering a rotating frame of reference around a low or high pressure system. These forces balance to result in no net force when friction is ignored. The centrifugal force always acts radially away from the centre of curvature and is a function of the wind speed and the radius of curvature. Depending on the flow direction, the Coriolis and centrifugal forces act either in the same direction or oppose eachother, which leads to flow differences between cyclonic and anticyclonic systems.

In an anticyclonic, or high pressure system, rotation is clockwise. Therefore, the centrifugal force acts to the left. The pressure gradient force acts away from the high pressure system and hence, also acts to the left. The Coriolis force meanwhile, acts to the right. Therefore, the force balance is (Lynch and Cassano, 2006):

$$
\text{Centrifugal + PGF (outwards) = Coriolis (inwards)}
$$
Therefore, the weak Coriolis force must balance both the centrifugal and pressure gradient forces. In pure geostrophic flow, the Coriolis force balances with the pressure gradient force. Therefore, in a rotational setting within an anticyclonic system, the Coriolis force must increase to balance the two other forces. Consequently, the wind velocity increases because the Coriolis force increases proportionally to the wind velocity:
$$
F_{Cor} \propto V
$$
However, the centrifugal force is also a function of the velocity, but rather than increasing linearly with velocity it increases by the square of the velocity:

$$
F_{CF} \propto \frac{V^2}{r}
$$
Therefore, as the wind velocity increases, the centrifugal force increases at a greater rate than the coriolis force. This places a dynamic constraint on the intensity of anticyclones because as velocity increases, for instance because an anticyclone moves poleward due to the conservation of absolute vorticity under barotropic non-divergent conditions, the centrifugal force's greater increase will result in the coriolis force no longer being able to keep up, resulting in an imbalance, placing an upper limit on anticyclone intensity. Under such conditions, there are no real solutions to the force balance (Lynch and Cassano, 2006). Therefore, the radius of the anticyclone must increase because the centrifugal force is inversely proportional to the radius of the curvature. Consequently, divergence occurs, resulting in anticyclones typically being broad and low in intensity. Divergence also causes the sinking of air, hence why anticyclones are associated with clear skies and dry conditions. Near the surface, friction disrupts the balance by reducing wind speed and weakening the Coriolis force. This causes a component of the wind to flow from high to low pressure, reinforcing divergence at the surface. The resulting subsidence enhances the clear, dry conditions typical of anticyclones

Meanwhile, cyclones do not have this dynamic constraint. In a cyclone, the centrifugal and coriolis forces both act in the same direction, opposing the pressure gradient force:

$$
\text{Centrifugal + Coriolis (outwards) = PGF (inwards)}
$$
This means that cyclones rotate slower than geostrophic flow. Furthermore, because the centrifugal and coriolis forces are acting in the same direction, the greater increase in centrifugal force does not cause an imbalance. Therefore, for a given pressure gradient force, there is always a velocity solution that exists. Therefore, cyclones can have high velocities and low radii, enabling high intensities. The shrinking of radii results in convergence, and hence upward expansion of the air column, resulting in rising air, and thus, cloud development. This convergence is further enhanced near the surface by friction, which weakens the Coriolis force and allows air to spiral more directly toward the low-pressure centre

To conclude, the differences in atmospheric flow development between cyclones and anticyclones stems from the Coriolis force and centrifugal force acting in opposing or the same direction. When opposing, in anticyclones, their different relationships with velocity (square for centrifugal and linear for coriolis) create a dynamic constraint on the intensity that anticyclones can reach. Anticylcones respond to this dynamic constraint by becoming broader, thereby increase the radius of spin, which lowers the centrifugal force. Divergence also produces downward movement, producing dry and clear conditions. Conversely, these two forces act in the same direction in cyclones (acting outward), therefore no imbalance occurs and cyclones can intensify with no dynamic constraint. This permits the convergence of air, producing rising air and thus, cloud formation and the release of latent heat which is a key part of cyclogenesis. The role of friction is also different in flow development between cyclonic and anticyconlic systems. It enhances divergence in anticylclones whilst enhancing convergence in cyclones.
