# Daytime Variability of Cloud Fraction from DSCOVR/EPIC Observations

- Earth Polychromatic Imaging Camera (EPIC) on DSCOVR captures the sunlit side of the Earth with a frequency between 1-2 hours
- Differs from geostationary satellites which image the same surface
- Observes the state of the atmosphere and surface from sunrise to sunset in the same image 
- Global daytime cloud variability can be analysed using a single sensor, rather than relying on multisensor composite datasets - therefore using EPIC data helps to avoid cross-sensor calibration and consistency issues - therefore, more robust results are expected
- Daytime cloudiness affects the Earth's planetary albedo
- More beneficial than sun-synchronised orbits such as MODIS which provide a single daytime image of most locations at a specific time of day - insufficient for analysing clouds' daily behaviour [[Platnick et al., 2017]]
- Offset between satellites and changes in local time need to be considered when data is combined to estimate the diurnal cycle [[Levy et al., 2018]]
- EPIC instrument does not have thermal infrared sensors, and therefore, relevant spectral tests, such as temperature contrast for cloud detection, cannot be performed.
- Other methodologies have been developed to determine the cloud mask [[Yang et al., 2019]]
- Similar to GEO orbits, the best resolution of the image is at nadir where the viewing zenith angle is close to 0. EPIC has a pixel size of ~8km here.
- As the viewing zenith angle increases, the resolution coarsens with the longer axis of each pixel increasing
