## 12.2 Atmospheric cycle of stratospheric aerosols

**12.2.1**

- Transport of air form the troposhere to the stratosphere occurs almost exclusively at the tropics
- Air is generally very cold and dry because it has gone through the topical tropopause region where it is dehydrated
- Resultantly, it generally contains very few aerosols of tropospheric origin because aerosols will have been scavenged
- The quantity of tropospheric aerosols entering the stratosphere is not known with precision.
- Convection in the troposphere can result in overshooting into the stratosphere [[Nielsen et al., 2007]]
- -However, overall, the contribution is thought to be small.
- Some aerosol precursor gases are not scavenged effectively in the troposphere due to being insufficiently soluble
	- e.g., dimethyl sulphide, hydrogen sulphide, sulfur dioxide
	- Flux is still small
- Main gaseous precursor for stratospheric aerosols during nonvolcanic periods is carbonyl sulphide [[Chin and Davis, 1995]]
- COS is relatively stable in the atmosphere and therefore well-mixed and can be transported into the stratosphere [[Barkley et al., 2008]]
- Sources are natural: oceans, soils, wetlands, oxidation with DMS and CS2
- Small anthropogenic source [[Watts, 2000]]
- Carbonyl sulphide is photo dissociated into SO2 in the stratosphere - radiation is sufficient in the atmosphere, where it represents the dominant nonvolcanic source of sulphur gases in this region.
- End product is H2SO4

- Small volcanic eruptions emit in the troposphere but have a limited impact on the climate in the light of larger anthropogenic sulphur sources at the Earth's surface [[Andres and Kasgnoc, 1998]]
- Sulphur emitted during the largest eruptions can reach the stratosphere when the volcanic plume has enough buoyancy to go through the tropopause.
- Ash particles emitted in the atmosphere have a short (hours to several weeks) residence time in the atmosphere.
- Therefore, it is the emissions of sulphur gases that control the climate response to a volcanic eruption.
- The VEI index is typically not indicative of an eruption's impact on the climate.
- Small but sulphur rich eruptions can have a larger impact than large, but sulphur-poor eruptions E.g., 1980 Mt St Helents had a large VEI of 5 but had no impact on climate due to being sulphur-poor [[Robock, 1981]]
- Tropical eruptions are more effective than tropical-eruptions at perturbating the climate because gases and aerosols have longer residence times in the stratosphere when they enter through the tropics

**12.2.2** Transport

- Vertical motions are important in the troposphere
- Meanwhile, the stratosphere has an inverse vertical temperature gradient
- The tropopause separates the two
- Circulation in the stratosphere is very zonal and winds can be strong
- The meridional (latitudinal) component of the stratospheric circulation is the Brewer-Dobson circulation which originates in the tropical tropopause where air masses associated with deep convection penetrate into the stratosphere
- Circulation rises from the topics, and is advected towards the mid latitudes before sinking and returning in the lower stratosphere and can also reenter the troposphere at the high latitudes - the time is slow and takes several years [[Diallo et a., 2012]]
- Mixing is much faster in the longitudinal direction - therefore volcanic material injected a a given latitude tends to be rapidly transported in the zonal band, resulting in a fairly homogenous concentration within a latitude band
- A volcanic eruption in the mid- or high-latitudes increases aerosol concentrations in these regions and is followed by poleward transport but little transport to the equator [[Kravitz and Robock, 2011]] [[Jégou et al., 2013]]
- Eruptions in the tropics increase concentrations at all latitudes [[Trepte et al., 1993]]
- Why? At the tropics heating is more resulting in a stronger temperature gradient [[Kroll et al., 2011]]

- Quasi-biennial oscillation (QBO) - zonal wind in the equatorial stratosphere which switches from easterlies to westerlies within a period of about two years
	- Oscillation originates in the upper stratosphere and propagates downwards at a speed of ~1km per month until it reaches the tropopause.
	- Wind speeds are stronger in the easterly phase than in the westerly phase
	- The phase has a direct impact on the way and the speed with which chemical species and aerosols injected in the tropical slower stratosphere are transported vertically and dispersed towards the mid-latitudes
## 12.3 Physics and Chemistry of stratospheric aerosols

- Processes are different to the lower atmosphere - there is no wet scavenging or dry deposition
- The only way to remove aerosols in the stratosphere is through transport through its lower boundary
- Coagulation and sedimentation are both important processes.
- Several processes which contribute to aerosol sinks:
	- slow but permanent sedimentation of aerosols down to the troposphere
	- downward advective transport through the tropopause
	- Turbulent vertical diffusion in the lower stratosphere
- Main precursors to atmospheric aerosols are sulphur gases, therefore stratospheric aerosols are mostly composed of sulphuric acid and water
- Sulfuric acid aerosols are liquid in the cold, lower stratosphere. Above 30-35km, aerosols evaporate due to higher temperatures (remember, stratosphere has inverse temperature gradient) which defines the upper limit of the stratospheric aerosol layer which is referred to as the Junge Layer [[Junge et al., 1961]]
- Condensation nuclei for polar stratospheric clouds

- size distribution of stratospheric aerosols varies spatially and temporally according to seasonal variations in circulation and microphysical processes
- Interannual fluctuations due to variations in sources, especially wrt major volcanic eruptions.
- SO2 is oxidised into H2SO4 and can nucleate and form new particles or condense on pre existing aerosols.
- Aerosols grow progressively under coagulation, as they grow the size distribution shifts to larger sizes
- The size distribution is monomodal in nonvolcanic periods and becomes bimodal in the months folowing a volcanic period
- This bimodal distribution can be observed in the months following large sulphur-rich eruptions

## 12.4 Volcanic Aerosol Records

- Because aerosols in the stratosphere are well mixed longitudinally, a climatology can be constructed from well-distributed latitude and altitude measurements.
- The recent period is well covered by satellite measurements and ground-based measurements.
- Volcanic measurements from more distant past eruptions have to be inferred from indirect measurements of activity
	- E.g., ice cores
- Relaxation towards the normal background aerosol layer occurs at an exponential decrease rate 
	- e-folding time is ~ 1 year for a tropical injection - therefore, it can take several years to recover to a background stratosphere after a major eruption.
	- In the absence of any major volcanic eruptions after 1991 Pinatubo, the stratosphere likely reached background aerosol levels in the early 2000s
- Smaller eruptions and those which occur outside the topics can have much shorter e-folding times [[Kravitz and Robock, 2011]] [[Jégou et al., 2013]]

**12.4.2** Recent climate-relavent eruptions

**Salams Volcano, Indonesia**
- Ice cores show a signature of a very large eruption that took place in 1257 or 1258 [[Oppenheimer, 2003]]
- Debate over location but Salamas Volcano, Indonesia believed - [[Lavigne et al., 2013]]
- However, the proxy data shows that surface cooling was limited which suggests hat the radiative forcing associated to volcanic SO2 emissions does not scale linearly with emissions and sulphate loading because of changes in their size distribution [[Timmreck et al., 2009]]
**Laki, Iceland**
- June 1783 until February 1784
- Injected 120 Mt of SO2 and 8 Mt of HF - a little less than present day anthropogenic emissions of SO2 in a year [[Oman et al., 2006]]
- Advected acidic fog over Europe, resulting in a significant increase in deaths - partly attributable to a decrease in air quality
- Could have caused famines due to a failure in monsoons 
- Very cold 1783-84 winter over Europe and NA
**Tambora**
- April 1815
- Cooling of 1 K in the NH in 1816 - year without a summer [[Cole-Dai et al., 2009]]
**Pinatubo, Philippines**
- June 15 1991
- Not largest eruption in 20th century but the most observed and studied wrt its impact on the atmosphere and climate system
- Estimated to have released 10 Mt of SO2 into the atmosphere
- Cooling of 0.6 K in the NH and 0.4 K in the global mean

## 12.5 Role of stratospheric aerosols on the climate system

- Stratospheric aerosols have a climatic impact which occurs primarily through aerosol-radiation interactions as there are no water clouds in the stratosphere (except for polar stratospheric clouds)
- Aeorosl-radiation interactions result in a negative shortwave forcing because aerosols are primarily scattering
- For Pinatubo, the anomaly at the TOA was -4 to -5 W m-2
- Also exert a green-house effect because they absorb and emit longwave radiation at a temperature that is generally colder than the emission temperature of longwave radiation they absorb
	- Offsets shortwave effect by ~30%
	- Degree of compensation between GH effect and solar albedo effects depends on the aerosol size distribution and is therefore a function of time after a major volcanic eruption (due to coagulation affecting size distribution)
- The radiative forcing effect causes temporary cooling and a decrease of water vapour in the troposphere
- Radiative forcing is relatively homogenous spatially, the associated cooling is not:
	- difficult to disentangle the climate response to the volcanic eruption from natural variability
- Pinatubo was followed by winter warming - aerosol layer initially heats the topical stratosphere before the temperature anomaly advects to the middle latitudes, increasing the temperature gradient between the midlatitudes and the North Pole which results in the strengthing of zonal circulation in the stratosphere
- Aerosols can increase the amount of diffuse solar radiation at the Earth's surface, and increase carbon sinks in terrestrial ecosystems [[Gu et al., 2003]]
