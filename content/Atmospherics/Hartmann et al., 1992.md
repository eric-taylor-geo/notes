# The Effect of Cloud Type on Earth's Energy Balance: Global Analysis

- Cloud types can be categorised into 5 types of cloud to define day-to-day variations in radiation budget quantities
	- Low, Middle (Thin and Thick), and High (Thin and Thick)
	- Low clouds are not given a thin or thick classification because this isn't useful in the paper's regression analysis and most low cloud is of the thin variety
- ![[Pasted image 20250212204737.png|450]]
- Classifying to 5 simple cloud types enables an easier description of the relationship between cloud type and radiation balance
- Data is restricted to 60 degrees N during the winter due to relying on visible optical imagery
- High-cloud types occur preferentially where convection occurs in the tropics and midlatitude storm tracks
- High thick clouds occur in a smaller fraction of the globe than high thin clouds - typically in areas where convection is strongest
- Low clouds are mainly oceanic and most abundant in the subtropical eastern oceans where SST is comparatively low and where mean air motion is downward
- Total cloud cover is highest over the high-latitude oceans - stratus regimes are best developed here as well as over areas of intense tropical convection (e.g., Bay of Bengal in JJA and Amazon and Indonesia in DJF)
- Middle thin clouds are distributed diffusely throughout the convective regions of the Earth 
- Thick middle clouds are mostly in the high latitudes

## Results

- Use stepwise linear regression to express the radiation budget (from Earth Radiation Budget Experiment) in terms of cloud abundance.
- Regression for outgoing longwave radiation and albedo explains 80% of the variance
- Best in for example the mid and high latitudes of the Southern Hemisphere and the oceanic storm tracks of the Northern Hemisphere
- Regression is locally weaker in regions where the reflective effect negates the warming effect of clouds
- Cloud-type information explains twice as much variance as a regression on total cloud cover along for albedo and net radiation.
- Five-type cloud description explains 80% of the variance in OLR and albedo for most regions but this drops to 40% for net radiation.
- Net radiation is less uniquely related to the cloud distribution than either OLR or albedo, owing to the compensation between OLR and the albedo effect of clouds.
- Clear-sky estimates and total cloud-forcing estimates are in good agreement with those derived from ERBE clear-sky and average composites
	- Except in regions where clouds are so persistent that there is no observational data of clear skies
- Regression can also produce unrealistic estimates of cloud forcing - requires care when interpreting results
- For example, the regression overestimates clear-sky outgoing longwave radiation in tropical regions where deep convection moistens the troposphere - difficult to separate the longwave effect of cloud liquid water and ice from the longwave effect fo the enhancement of upper-tropospheric water vapour which accompanies deep convection in the tropics
	- Overestimates by as much as 20 W m^-2
- Regression if ERBE data on ISCCP cloud types provides an estimate of the contributions of individual cloud types on the earth's energy balance.
- Low-clouds provide -16 W m^-2 (60% of total net cloud forcing)
- High- and midlevel clouds contribute -7 W m-2
- Per unit area, high thick clouds make a larger contribution than low level clouds but are spatially smaller
- High and mid-level thin clouds make a small positive contribution to global net cloud forcing
