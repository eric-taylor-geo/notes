## 2.1 Definitions

- Aerosol is a collection of solid or liquid particles in suspension in a gas - aerosol actually refers to both the particles and the suspending gas however in atmospheric sciences this is typically used just for the partical
- Primary Aerosols: emitted into the atmosphere as particles
- Secondary Aerosols: not emitted directly in the particulate phase but come instead from condensation of atmospheric gas-phase species. The gas-phase species are called aerosol precursors
- Chemical composition can provide clues as to whether an aerosol is secondary or primary

**2.2.3** Volcanic Aerosols

- As well as ash, volcanoes emit SO2 and H2S that get oxidised in the atmosphere to form submicronic sulphate aerosols.
- Residence time in the troposphere is short lived - typically a few weeks
- Residence time in the stratosphere is much longer - months to a year depending on the region and altitude of injection

## 2.3 Spatial and Temporal Aerosol Distributions

- Once in the atmosphere, aerosols are transported by winds but are also subject to removal pathways - atmospheric sinks
- Different particles have different residence times int he atmosphere
- Residence time is the average amount of time an aerosol spends in the atmosphere and is defined as:
$$
\tau^* = \frac{B}{S}=\frac{B}{R}
$$
- where B is the vertically-integrated aerosol concentration (aerosol burden or column), S is the source flux, and R is the removal or sink flux
- Quantities are averaged over the globe
- The spatial and temporal distribution of aerosols thus strongly depends on the distribution of sources and sinks and how these interact with transport 
- Primary sinks are **dry deposition** at the surface and **wet deposition** from precipitations
- Aerosol properties evolve during transport
- Concentrations and properties vary in the vertical - most sources are located at the Earth's surface therefore we would expect aerosol concentrations to decrease with height - concentrations are typically higher in the atmospheric boundary layer than in the free troposphere
- However, plumes can lift aerosols into the free troposphere

## 2.4 Aerosol-Cloud-Radiation Interactions

- Aerosols interact with electromagnetic radiation that propagates in the atmosphere in serval ways
- Shortwave radiation is:
	- scattered -  deflected in all directions but anisotropically
	- Absorbed - transfering to heat
	- Reflected
- Aerosols influence microphysical properties of liquid clouds - in particular through their role as CCN - affects the size and population of cloud water droplets
- Some aerosols favour the formation of ice and act as ice nuclei 
- Clouds themselves also influence aerosol populations - precipitating clouds act as a sink
	- In cloud scavenging: aerosols enter water droplets within the clouds before precipitation
	- Below cloud scavenging: aeorols are captured by raindrops themselves
- Clouds therefore act as a wet deposition sink
- Non-precipitating clouds will eventually evaporate and release cloud droplet residies back into the atmosphere and can act as future CCN

- SO2 is readily oxidized in cloud droplets - affecting the chemical and physical properties of an aerosol population
- Cloud processing effect is important in convective clouds which can activate small aerosols and release larger ones which can act as CCN in other clouds
- Radiation: reflectance, absorption, emittance
- Heating modifies the temperature profile of the cloud and thus its stability

## 2.5 Climate Effects of Aerosols

- Scatter and absorb radiation - backscatering towards space reduces incoming solar radiation and a loss of energy
	- Absorption is accompanied by heating within the aerosol layer which is then radiated outwards but this also reduces the radiation at the Earth's surface (impact of this on emitted radiation governed by stefan-boltzmann)
	- Termed aerosol-radiation interactions
- Absorption of solar aradiaiton modifies the vertical temperature profile - impacting relative humidity, atmospheric stability, and therefore cloud formation
	- Aerosol semi-direct effect
	- Rapid adjustment of atmospheric state following aerosol-radiation interactions
- Act as CCN - results in less solar energy being absorbed
	- Aerosol-cloud interactions]
- Modification of cloud microphysical properties is expected to have an impact on cloud evolution
- Ice CCN too
- Absorbing aerosols may deposit onto snow and ice surfaces, making the surfaces less reflective - warms the climate susyem
	- 'aerosol on snow effect' 
	- 'aerosol-surface interactions'
- Interact with vegetation through changes in incoming solar radiation
