# Observing polar regions from space: advantages of a satellite system on a highly elliptical orbit versus a constellation of low Earth polar orbiters

- Compares a realistic two-satellite HEO system with currently operating two-, four-, and seven-satellite LEO constellations in terms of spatio-temporal coverage and the capacity to provide sequences of single, dual, and triplet images at a required temporal resolution which is required to properly monitor dynamic events
	- E.g., volcanic ash transport, frontal systems, wild fire smoke, cloud motion and derivations of wind vectors
- A two-satellite HEO system is 5-10 times more efficient for monitoring dynamic events from image pairs above 70ºN than a standard four-satellite morning-afternoon sun synchronous LEO constellation
- A LEO constellation to provide a coverage with 15 minute refresh rate would need 23 satellites

## Introduction

- Despite LEO imaging systems having a better spatial resolution than GEO - the difference is not seen as a critical factor for most applications.
- GEO satellites can observe the Earth at any point with a high refresh rate (1-15 mins) within their area coverage but are unable to observe the poles
- LEO satellites cannot do this - not enough polar sun synchronous in orbit
- Even if there were, compositing mosaics from multiple satellites is a large challenge and mosaic compositions have their own issues for monitoring dynamic phenomena in the atmosphere:
	- atmospheric motion vectors, volcanic ash wildfire smoke, ice motion - requires the analysis of image triplets or at least image pairs properly synchronized in time [[Nieman et al., 1997] [[Velden et al., 2005]]

- A potential solution is to use a satellite constellation of HEOs [[Trishchenko et al., 2011]] [[Kidder and Vonder Haar, 1990]]
- The HEO concept was endorsed by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) in its strategic document “Vision for the Global Observing System in 2025” adopted by the 61st session of the WMO Executive Council [[WMO, 2009]]

## HEO system definition

- Molniya orbits, designed by the soviet union for high-latitude communication and monitoring, have a stable location of the apogee, using a critical inclination of 63.4º - produces four apogees:
	- ![[Pasted image 20250122202645.png]]
- Allows for quasi-geostationary monitoring
- In a two-satellite system, when one satellite is at the apogee, the other is at the perigee
- Molniya orbit is limited by its exposure to a harsh ionising radiation environment from the presence of high-energy protons
- [[Trishchenko et al., 2011]] suggest a different orbit with three apogees which addresses this problem.
- ![[Pasted image 20250122202914.png]]
- Therefore, HEO and GEO observing systems can compement eachother - GEO covers the tropics and mid-latitudes up to ~60º and HEO provides continuous coverage of the polar regions
- Two pairs of two-satellite constellations are required to cover both hemispheres
