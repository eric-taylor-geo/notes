[[Atmospherics - Clouds]]
- Dew occurs when air at the surface is cooled by conduction from cold surfaces beneath. Upon reaching the dew point, water condenses
- Therefore often requires calm, clear nights.
	- Clean night: allows for cooling via emission of infrared radiation
	- Calm: little mixing at the surface, allowing for cold air to remain near the surface
- When the dew point is at or below freezing, water vapour can directly become ice without being a liquid first - a process called deposition.
- Creates frost - which has a branching, crystalline structure which differs from spherical beads of frozen dew

## Condensation Nuclei

- Without CCN, RH of 100s of percent would be needed for condensation to spontaneously occur
- Different CCN will result in condensation at different degrees of RH - some lower than 100% (on **hygroscopic** nuclei), some higher

## Clouds

- A cloud is a "visible aggregate of tiny water droplets or ice cyrstals suspended in the air" (p. 124)

### High Clouds

- Typically >5,000 m - air is cold and 'dry'
- Only ice phase
**Cirrus**
- Thin, whispy clouds blown by high winds in the upper atmosphere int long streamers
- Typically move from west to east, indicative of the prevailing wind at their elevation.
- Occur generally during periods of fair, pleasant weather
**Cirrocumulus**
- small, rounded, white puffy clouds which may occur individually or in long rows
- Small ripples present in the clouds - which cover large proportions of the sky
**Cirrostratus**
- Thin, sheetline high clouds which cover the entire sky.
- Very thin
- Ice crystals present scatter light, creating a halo which encircles the sun and moon
- Clouds can be sufficiently thin that the halo is the only visible indicator
- Thick cirrostratus clouds give the sky a white appearance and frequently form ahead of an advancing cyclonic storm - therefore they can be used to predict rain or snow within 12-24 hrs

### Middle Clouds
- 2,000-7,000m
- Mixed phase
- Composed mainly of water droplets with ice crystals if temperatures are sufficiently low
**Altocumulus**
- Less than 1 km thick
- Appear as gray, puffy masses, sometimes followed out in parallel waves or bands
- Waves result in thicker and lighter cloud elements when seen from below
- When appearing on warm, humid summer warmings, it can be a precursor of thunderstorms in the late afternoon
**Altostratus**
- gray or blue-gray clouds composed of ice crystals and water droplets
- Often cover the entire sky and extend many hundreds of square km
- If precipitation falls from altostratus clouds, the cloud base usually lowers and precipitation is steady rather than showery
- If preciptation reaches the ground, the cloud is classified as nimbostratus

### Low clouds
- Bases <2,000 m
- Mostly always composed of water droplets but may contain ice particles in cold conditions
- Liquid phase
**Nimbostratus**:
- Cloud layers associated with more or less continuously falling rain or snow
- Intensity of precipitation is typicaly light or moderate - never showery
- Precipitation makes identifying the cloud base difficult.
- If air beneath becomes saturated by falling precipitation, a lower layer of clouds or fog can occur.
**Stratocumulus**
- Low, lumpy clouds that appear in rows or patches with blue sky visible between individual cloud elements
- Often occur near sunset as the spreading remains of former cumulus clouds
- Differs from altocumulus in that they have a lower base and larger individual cloud elements.
**Stratus**
- Uniform grayish cloud that often covers the entire sky
- Often have a more uniform cloud base than nimbostratus clouds

### Clouds with vertical development
**Cumulus**
- Tops of clouds denote the limit of rising air
- Differ from stratocumulus for clouds are much more detached whilst stratocumulus occur in patches and groups
- Develop on warm summer mornings and become larger throughout the day as air rises
- Become cumulus congestus upon sufficient growth - often is a single large cloud but they can grow into eachother
- Precipitation that falls is always showery with frequent changes in its intensity
- Upon further vertical growth, they develop into cumulonimbus clouds (thunderstorm clouds)
**Cumulonimbus**
- Extend from 600 m up to 12,000 m
- The condensation of water vapour within them releases lots of energy and creates significant updrafts and downdrafts with wind speeds of up to 70 knots in the vertical direction
- Fast winds at higher altitudes can reshape the upper portion, turning the cloud into a flattened anbil
