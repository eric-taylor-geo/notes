## Atmospheric Stability

- In a stable atmosphere, a parcel of air will return to its original elevation after its lifting / downward force is removed. Therefore, it is difficult for energy and moisture to move vertically from the surface.
- Degree of atmospheric stability depends on the lapse rate - the rate at which the temperature changes with height
- If a parcel of air rises or sinks quickly enough that it does not have enough time to exchange any energy with the surrounding air, it is said to have adiabatic motion
- As it rises, surrounding air pressure decreases, causing the air parcel to expand. 
- Work has to be done to expand its volume, therefore energy is used which decreases its temperature
- Conversely, if it sinks adiabatically, it becomes compressed by the increasing air column above, doing work from above, increasing its temperature
- The rate of cooling and warming is equal - first law of thermodynamics
- Unsaturated adiabatic lapse rate is 9.8 K per KM
- Upon becoming saturated, water begins to condense, releasing latent heat, thereby warming the air parcel. This release of energy prevents the parcel from cooling as rapidly under unsaturated conditions. Therefore, the saturated adiabatic lapse rate is always less.
- The saturated adiabatic lapse rate is also not a constant value and instead varies according to the moisture content of the air.
- Adiabatic assumption is never completely valid - however the assumption has to be made because there is no method of monitoring the temperature change of every parcel of air that rises or sinks at all times across the Earth.
	- Heat exchange between air parcels is often slow compared to the timescale of vertical motion in the atmosphere
	- Allows the isolation of pressure changes on temperature without considering external heat transfer
- Environmental lapse rate varies daily and across space.
	- Global average of 6.5 K / km
- The stability of the atmosphere is therefore dependent on the different between the unsaturated and saturated adiabatic lapse rate and the specific environmental lapse rate at a given point in time and space
- Conditional stability occurs when $\Gamma_s < \gamma < \Gamma_u$ 
	- The atmosphere's stability is therefore conditional on whether or not the air is saturated.

- LCL: lifting condensation level - level at which the air becomes saturated and the air parcel no longer cools at the UALR but rather at the SALR
- **Potential Temperature**: temperature a parcel of air would have if moved dry adiabatically from its height (z) in the atmosphere to the 1000-mb level.
	- $\theta = T + \Gamma_u(z-z_{\text{1000 mb}})$
	- Unstable: $\frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta z}<0$ 
	- Neutral: $\frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta z} = 0$
	- Stable: $\frac{\Delta \theta}{\Delta z} > 0$
