Precipitation in the Earth's atmosphere arises from the growth of cloud particles, whether in the liquid or ice phase, into sizes sufficient to overcome atmospheric drag and sediment, reaching the surface as precipitation. The precipitation process has two primary pathways: the warm rain pathway, involving condensational growth and coagulation via collision and coalescence; and the cold wain pathway, involving depositional growth and either accretion or aggregation. The efficiency of these processes controls the timing between initial cloud formation and precipitation. This essay will first establish the processes at which water droplets reach precipitation sizes, followed by ice crystals. It will then compare and contrast their processes.

The near-field of a droplet is in an instantaneous equilibrium with its surrounding environment and the water vapour concentration is given by the saturation vapour pressure of the specific droplet. The growth of water droplets is governed by Koehler Theory (Köhler, 1936) which combines the effects of the Kelvin, or curvature effect, and the Raoult, or solution effect, to describe the initial growth and activation of cloud droplets. The Raoult effect describes the lowering of the equilibrium vapour pressure due to the presence of hygroscopic solutes where the saturation vapour pressure over a solution is proportional to the mole fraction of water in solution. Hygroscopic solutes (e.g., a dissolved NaCl cloud condensation nucleus) attract water molecules, and most importantly, the strength of their attraction is greater than water molecules' attraction to eachother. Therefore, it is harder for water molecules to evaporate. Consequently, the saturation vapour pressure is lower than with respect to a flat surface of pure water, enabling growth. As a water droplet grows, the mole fraction of water increases, meaning the dissolved solute is less concentrated. Consequently, its effect decreases and the saturation vapour pressure increases, making further growth more difficult. On the other hand, the Kelvin effect describes the relation between a droplet's radius and the vapour pressure at the liquid-vapour interface. At very small radii the droplet is very curved, meaning molecules at the surface have fewer neighbouring water molecules attracting eachother, making evaporation easier. Consequently, the saturation vapour pressure is higher than that of a flat surface. As a droplet grows, its curvature decreases - it becomes 'flatter' - thereby decreasing the saturation vapour pressure, which makes further growth easier. Thus, the Raoult and Kelvin effects oppose eachother, the Raoult effect permitting initial growth but making growth more difficult as droplets grow, and the Kelvin effect which opposes initial growth but facilitates further growth. The two effects when combined produce the Koehler Curve which has a critical radius which features a turning point, beyond which any droplet growth lowers the saturation vapour pressure (due to the Kelvin effect), permitting continuous growth so long as conditions remain super-saturated and vapour molecules can diffuse from the far-field into the near-field and that there is a sufficient flux of latent heat (released during condensation) to the far-field from the near-field. The exact value of the critical radius varies depending on the CCN and the environment (e.g., temperature), but it is typically around 0.05 to 0.5 micrometres (Ackerman and Knox, 2011). After surpassing the critical radius, the droplet is considered to be 'activated'. However, whilst these two processes enable the initial growth of water droplets, the continued growth of droplets into precipitation droplets becomes very slow because the Koehler Curve flattens and the saturation vapour pressure approaches that of a flat surface of pure water, reducing the growth rate. Growth rate is inversely proportional to the droplet radius (flux depends on surface area $\propto r^2$ but growth is dependent on volume $\propto r^3$) and it would take ~40,000 seconds for precipitation sized droplets to form (5-7 mm) (Rogers and Yau 1989). Thus, other processes are critical for precipitation efficiency and must dominate droplet growth once activated in order to produce precipitation efficiently within the timescales observed in the atmosphere upon cloud formation  (~30 minutes) (Rogers and Yau, 1989).

Cloud droplets will collide as long as their velocities are different (speed and direction), which can be caused by brownian motion, turbulent sheer, and differential sedimentation rates. Differential sedimentation rates are the dominant cause of collisions for the size of typical cloud droplets (~10 micrometers). The sedimentation  velocity is dependent on droplet size due to the drag of the atmosphere. Therefore, so long as there is a sufficient distribution of droplet sizes, there will be a high collision efficiency (Berry and Reinhardt, 1974). However, droplets must be of a sufficient size to have sufficient intertia to collide with others - when the inertia is too small, droplets will follow the direction of air flow which moves around droplets. Hocking (1959) demonstrated that collector droplets must have sizes greater than 18 micrometers. Such sized cloud droplets are rare (typically 1 in 100,000) but can form due to different CCN and environmental conditions which are continuously changing as mixing occurs within a cloud. A rain droplet typically requires 100,000 cloud droplets to coagulate to form. Thus, given this size distribution, collisions and subsequent coalescence can occur to form rain droplets. The total coagulation equation is the probability of a droplet of size R coagulating (colliding and then coalescing) with a droplet of size r, multiplied by the number of droplets of each size, and integrated over all possible droplet sizes. Berry and Reinbardt (1974) solved this numerically, showing that when the distribution of initial rain droplets is sufficiently broad (has enough droplets >18 micrometers; Hocking, 1959), rainfall sized droplets form efficiently within 30 minutes, aligning with atmospheric observations of rainfall efficiency (Rogers and Yau, 1989). Thus, collisions and coalescence, driven by the distribution of droplet sizes having different sedimentation velocities, are critical for precipitation efficiency.

 Unlike Koehler theory for water droplets, there is no single closed theoretical description for the freezing of ice crystals. This is because freezing is a stochastic process requiring the random arrangement of water molecules into an initial ice embryo to initiate freezing. Thus, freezing does not occur at 0 degrees C, and water droplets become supercooled. The probability of this occurring is greater for larger water droplets due to the increased likelihood of molecules arranging themselves. Ice crystals can form homogenous or heterogenous processes. Homogenous freezing occurs when pure water droplets freeze without an ice nuclei - this occurs at -38 degrees C. Homogenous deposition does not occur in the atmosphere because it requires an embryo to form from the chance collision of vapour molecules which is impossible under atmospheric conditions. Ice nuclei enable either heterogenous deposition or the freezing of supercooled water droplets at warmer temperatures - typically around - 15 degrees C. Though some are more effective, for example, Silver Iodide permits freezing at -4 degrees C (Rogers and Yau, 1989). Once frozen, crystals grow very effectively via diffusional growth due to the Wegener-Bergeron-Findeisen Process. The saturation vapour pressure at a given temperature is lower over ice than water, with the maximum difference occurring at -12 degrees. Consequently, in a cold environment prior to ice formation. there will be many supercooled liquid cloud droplets which are in equilibrium with their environment. However, upon freezing, the local saturation vapour pressure over ice crystals is lower, causing ice crystals to be sub-saturated with respect to the environment. Consequently, water molecules deposit on them, causing ice crystals to grow. This then removes vapour from the environment, causing the water droplets become over-saturated with respect to the environment, causing evaportion, which provides more vapour for crystal growth. Therefore, due to the Wegener-Bergeron-Findeisen, few ice crystals will grow at the expense of many water droplets, enabling very efficient crystal growth into precipitation size crystals within less than an hour, occuring faster than the warm rain process (Rogers and Yau, 1989). This is most efficient at -15 degrees C, slightly cooler than the maximum difference at -12 C because the latent heat released during deposition needs to be mitigated. Therefore, the cold rain process contrasts with the warm rain process because the cold rain process is highly efficient at producing precipitation sized crystals only through diffusional growth, whilst the warm rain process requires coagulation.

The processes of accretion and aggregation further enhance the precipitation efficiency of ice crystals. Accretion, whereby crystals collide with supercooled droplets resulting in freezing (Riming), is a very efficient process - the collection coefficient is almost unity (Rogers and Yau, 1989). Furthermore, aggregation occurs when two crystals collide. However, it requires temperatures warmer than -10 C, else crystals are too dry and hard for bonding to occur. The process of rime-splintering or the Hallett and Mossop Process (Hallett and Mossop, 1974) occurs when graupels collide with water droplets. When temperautres are between -8 and -3 degrees C and the droplet is >25 micromters in diameter, the freezing of the droplet results in splintering, creating multiple ice particles, enhancing the number of ice crystals in a cloud, which further increases the efficiency of precipitation. The relatively warm conditions for rime splintering are not typically conducive to the formation of ice in the first place. Graupel particles that induce rime splintering typically originate from higher and colder parts of the cloud through mixing. Thus, the mixing within mixed-phase clouds is critical for such processes, enabling precipitation efficiency. Therefore, whilst the diffusional growth of ice crystals is sufficient for efficient precipitation generation (Rogers and Yau, 2006),  accretion and aggregation further enhance the efficiency.

To conclude and summarise, both water droplets (warm process) and ice crystals (cold process) grow via diffusional growth and the collision and coalescence and aggregation/accretion. The effiency of such processes differ for each rain process. For warm rain, diffusional growth is captured by Koehler Theory and occurs too slowly for efficient precipitation. Therefore, coagulation is a critical process for precipitation efficiency. Meanwhile, ice crystals grow very efficiently via diffusional growth because of the Wegener-Bergeron-Findeisen process, which by itself can produce precipitation efficiently (Rogers and Yau, 1989). The processes of accretion and aggregation further enhance the precipitation efficiency.
