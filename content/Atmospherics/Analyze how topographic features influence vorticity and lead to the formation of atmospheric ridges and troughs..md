Topographic features cause the squeezing and stretching of the air column as air moves over them. Due to the conservation of isentropic potential vorticity between two isentropic surfaces (surfaces of constant potential temperature), the absolute and planetary vorticity of an air mass is affected as the column is stretched and squeezed. In turn, this produces a stationary Rossby wave downwind of a topographic feature, producing atmospheric ridges and troughs. This essay will first outline how a topographic feature influences vorticity by using the concept of the conservation of isentropic potential vorticity, followed by how this forms atmospheric ridges and troughs due to the formation of stationary Rossby waves.

Isentropic surfaces are surfaces of constant potential temperature (Lynch and Cassano, 2006). Potential temperature is the temperature at which an air parcel will be if moved adiabatically to a reference elevation, typically sea level. Topographic features cause isentropic surfaces to rise over them. Isentropic surfaces closer to the surface align more closely with the topographic feature, whilst isentropic surfaces aloft are less so (See Figure 1).

In baratropic, inviscid flow, the isentropic potential vorticity of an air column bounded by two isentropic surfaces is conserved. Isentropic potential vorticity is defined as:
$$
\text{IPV} = -g\frac{\delta \theta}{\delta p}(\xi + f) = \text{Constant}
$$
where g is gravity, delta theta theta and p are the differences in potential temperature and pressure between the top and bottom of the air column, respectively. Xi and f are the relative and planetary vorticities, respectively. Because the air column is bound between two isentropic surfaces, delta theta remains constant. However, because the two isentropic surfaces are affected by the topographic feature differently, the pressure difference in the atmosphere between the two surfaces does change. 
Consider an air column moving left to right (west to east) in Figure 1 with zero relative vorticity. Upon approaching the feature, the upper isentropic surface begins to rise first, thereby stretching the air column. Consequently, delta p increases, lowering the potential temperature gradient. The absolute vorticity (xi + f) must increase to conserve IPV. Therefore, this induces anticlockwise (cyclonic) rotation and poleward meridional displacement, which increases the relative and planetary vorticity. As the air column encroaches the slope of the topographic barrier, it is rapidly squeezed, thereby decreasing delta p and increasing the potential temperature gradient. To conserve IPV, the relative vorticity and planetary vorticity must decrease. Hence, clockwise rotation (anticyclonic) is induced, and the air parcel is displaced equatorward, moving back to its original line of latitude and continuing equatorward. Upon moving over the crest of the topographic feature, the air column is stretched again, which induces poleward motion back to its original line of latitude and cyclonic rotation. Thus, using the concept of the conservation of isentropic potential vorticity in baratropic, inviscid conditions, the influence of topographic features on vorticity has been demonstrated. However, the air parcel does not simply return to its original line of latitude because despite now having returned to having zero relative vorticity, it has poleward meridional momentum, which initiates the formation of a Rossby wave which will now be discussed.

A Rossby wave conserves absolute vorticity, which is conserved in baratropic, inviscid, and divergence-free conditions. Because the air column has now passed over the topographic barrier, the height of the air column is treated as constant. As such, there is now squeezing (divergence), or stretching (convergence). Thus, the absolute vorticity equation is employed:
$$
\frac{d}{dt}(\xi + f) = 0
$$
Returning to the air parcel: the air parcel overshoots its original latitude of zero vorticity due to its poleward momentum. Consequently, its planetary vorticity increases. To conserve absolute vorticity, the relative vorticity must decrease, thereby inducing clockwise (anticyclonic) rotation. The clockwise rotation acts to divert the air parcel back equatorwards. As it moves equatorwards, planetary vorticity decreases and hence, absolute vorticity increases, reducing clockwise rotation, which becomes anticlockwise rotation upon overshooting the original line of latitude, turning the parcel back polewards. This process repeats, creating oscillatory motion around the air parcel's original line of latitude. The restoring force is as a consequence of the change in the Coriolis parameter with latitude. The continued air movement produces a stationary Rossby wave with anticyclonic and cyclonic rotation above and below the line of original latitude, respectively. Regions with anticyclonic motion form atmospheric ridges and regions with cyclonic motion form atmospheric troughs.
