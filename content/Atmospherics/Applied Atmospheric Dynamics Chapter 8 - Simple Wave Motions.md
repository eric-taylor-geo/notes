# 8.1 Properties of waves

- In the atmosphere, wave motions are oscillations in variables such as velocity and pressure.
- Waves travel through a medium; therefore, their position needs to be considered in the wave equation.
$$
\theta = \theta_0 \cos{(kx + ly + mz - \nu t - \alpha)}
$$
- Visualise:
![[atmos.py]]
- $\nu$ is the angular frequency of the wave  
- $k, l, m$ are the wave numbers which are related to the wavelength by:
$$
|\vec{k}| = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda}
$$
- Phase speed is the rate at which the phase of the wave propagates in each of the three spatial dimensions, and is given by:

$$
\phi = kx + ly + mz - \nu t - \alpha
$$
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\phi\right) = \frac{d}{dt}\left(kx + ly + mz - \nu t - \alpha \right)
$$
- For a given wave crest, the phase remains constant, therefore $d \Phi / dt = 0$ 
$$
0 = k\frac{dx}{dt} + l\frac{dy}{dt} + m\frac{dz}{dt}- \nu
$$
- Solving for each of the three spatial dimensions:
$$
c_x = \frac{\nu}{k} \quad c_y = \frac{\nu}{l} \quad c_z = \frac{\nu}{m}
$$
$$
c = \frac{\nu}{\left|\vec{k}\right|}
$$
- Phase speed is dependent on the wavenumber - therefore, waves of different wavelengths which originate from the space location will propagate at different rates
- The rate at which the envelope of observable disturbance travels is called the group velocity

$$
c_x - \bar{u_g} = -\frac{\beta}{\left|\vec{k}\right|^2}
$$
- Rossby's forumula above
- Longer waves (smaller wave number) travel faster than shorter waves
- Ignoring the contribution of the zonal wind, propagation is toward the west (negative x)
# 8.3 Planetary Waves

- Assumption of non-divergence (equivilant to assuming barotropic flow) is only justifiable at scales of motion in the atmosphere that are much larger than mid-latitude synoptic systems.
- At large scales, disturbances in the atmosphere are 2-3 orders of magnitude broader than they are deep, meaning our assumption is typically unproblematic
	- Rossby Waves circle the globe at the mid-latitudes

## 8.3.1 Forcing of planetary waves

- There is a tendency for longer waves to occur in preferred geographic locations
- The preferred locations of peaks and troughs of are associated with meridionaly orientated mountain ranges
- For example in the NH winter, a four-wave pattern is apparent with troughs located in the North Atlantic, Aleutian region of the north Pacific, Rocky Mountains, and Northern Scandinavia southward into the Mediterranean Sea.
- Their persistence suggests that the waves are quasi-stationary.
- Troughs are located preferentially on the lee side (east in this case) of mountain ranges
- Forced waves are generated by three principle mechanisms:
	1. Topographic forcing
	2. Thermal forcing due to longitudinal heating differences associated with the distribution of oceans and continents.
	3. Forcing due to nonlinear interactions with smaller scale disturbances such as extra-tropical cyclones
