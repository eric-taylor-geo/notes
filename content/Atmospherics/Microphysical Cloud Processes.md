# Liquid Microphysical Processes

Cloud droplets grow via two mechanisms; the diffusion and condensation of excess moisture in the cloud onto cloud droplets, and via the collision and coalescence of cloud droplets. The latter is dominant for drops with radii greater than 10 micrometers (Davis and Sartor, 1967). Below 10 micrometers, droplet rapidly grow following their activation, which is described by Koehler Theory.
## Koehler Theory:

Koehler Theory describes the activation of a cloud condensation nuclei to become a cloud droplet (Köhler, 1936). It is described the the Koehler curve which gives the general equilibrium relation between an aqueous salt solution droplet, for instance sodium chloride from sea spray, and water vapour (McFiggans et al., 2006).

The Koehler Curve considers two competing effects which are dictated by the size of a droplet, its solute contents, and environmental conditions:
1. The Raoult, or solute, effect,
2. The Kelvin, or curvature, effect.

The near-field of a droplet can be assumed to be in an instantaneous equilibrium with its surrounding environment and the water vapour concentration in the near-field is given by the saturation vapour pressure of the specific droplet. 

### Raoult Effect

The Raoult, or solute effect, describes the relationship between the vapour pressure and the mole fraction of a hygroscopic solute in a solution.

In the context of a cloud droplet, the cloud condensation nucleus (CCN) is the solute dissolved in a water solution - the droplet. The hygroscopic nature attracts water molecules, and most importantly, their attraction of water molecules is greater than the individual attraction of other water molecules. Consequently, hygroscopic CCNs allow for condensation to occur at a lower vapour pressure than required for homogenous nucleation and permit condensation at relative humidities less than 100% relative to a flat surface of pure water. As the water droplet grows, the solute becomes more dilute and therefore the hygroscopic attraction of molecules decreases, thereby increasing the saturation vapour pressure.

Under ideal conditions, the saturation vapour pressure increases proportionally to the mole fraction of water within a solution.

The solute effect therefore permits the formation of small droplets by condensation due to lowering the saturation vapour pressure relative to a flat surface of pure water. However, it is also opposed by the highly curved nature of small cloud droplets, which is captured by the Kelvin Effect

### Kelvin Effect

The Kelvin, or curvature effect, describes the relationship between a cloud droplet's radius and the vapour pressure at the liquid-vapour interface. At very small radii, a droplet's surface is highly curved, and thus water molecules at the surface have fewer neighbouring water droplets attracting each other compared to a flat surface, thereby making evaporation easier. Thus, the saturation vapour pressure increases. Conversley, larger water droplets' surfaces are much 'flatter' (in the near-field. A truly flat water droplet would require an infinite radius), and thus have more neighbouring water molecule to attract eachother. Consequently, evaporation is more difficult and the saturation vapour pressure is lower. Thus, as a droplet grows in size, the saturation pressure owing to the Kelvin effect decreases.

### Combining the two

Both effects compete with each other and the Koehler Curve combines the two. At small cloud droplet radii, the Kelvin effect dominates, and it is difficult for cloud droplets to initially grow. Any growth increases the saturation vapour pressure owing to the Raoult effect. However, the Koehler Curve has a turning point, whereby after this turning point, which occurs at a critical droplet radius, droplet growth results in a lower saturation vapour pressure due to the Kelvin effect. The exact critical radius is dependent on the properties of the CCN and the environment (e.g., temperature), but it is typically around 0.05-0.5 micrometers (Ackerman and Knox, 2011). 

Upon surpassing the critical radius, the cloud droplet is said to be activated, and any further growth reduces the saturation vapour pressure, enabling more condensation, which further reduces the saturation vapour pressure. Thus, a positive feedback loop occurs, and the droplet grows continuously. However, there are two main limits to this growth.

![[Pasted image 20250603144004.png|Koehler Curve showing the competing Raoult and Kelvin effects relative to a flat surface]]

Water vapour is required for condensation. Therefore, continuous growth requires the presence of water vapour molecules in the far-field which can move to the near-field and condense on the water droplet. Therefore, there must be a concentration gradient in vapour between the near- and far-fields which enables water molecules to move down a concentration gradient toward droplets. Thus, the growth rate is dependent on the rate at which water molecules can move from the far-field to the near-field. The speed of which is dependent on the pressure and temperature - higher temperatures and pressures result in faster movement and a higher concentration of water molecules, respectively.

Temperature plays a secondary role. The Koehler Curve assumes thermodynamic equilibrium. However, the condensation of water droplets on a cloud droplet will release latent heat which increases the temperature of the near-field. Consequently, the saturation vapour pressure increases, thereby negating some of the decrease int he Kelvin effect owing to droplet growth. If the release of latent heat is not dissipated from the near-field, then growth will eventually stop due to the saturation vapour pressure becoming too high. 

Thus, overall, condensational growth is reliant on a water vapour concentration gradient which moves water molecules from the far-field to the near-field and a thermal gradient which dissipates the release of latent heat from condensation from the near-field to the far-field.

As the droplet radius increases, the combined effects of Raoult and Kelvin terms become negligible, and the saturation vapour pressure approaches that over a flat surface of pure water - further growth beyond this is no longer as influenced by the Koehler Curve. Towards droplets 10 micrometers in size, the net effects of the Raoult and Kelvin effects are very little. However, raindrops in the atmosphere are observed to be several millimeters in size, and thus other processes play a role in allowing droplets to grow to such sizes. Therefore, Koehler theory is responsible for explaining the initial activation of cloud droplets and their initial growth. Condensational growth governed by Koehler theory is too slow to reproduce the rate at which precipitation occurs following cloud formation - 40,000 seconds compared to the 30 minutes observed (Rogers and Yau, 1989). Therefore, other processes, such as coagulation, must become more dominate post-activation to explain the rate of precipitation formation.

## Raindrop formation

Fast precipitation formation can be explained via the process of coagulation which consists of firstly a collision between two or more droplets, and their coalescence to form one single new particle.

### Collisions

For collisions to occur, particles need to be moving in different velocities and/or directions so that the relative motion between them allows for a collision. Collisions occur through several processes:
- Brownian motion
- Turbulent shear
- Sedimentation

The larger of the two colliding drops is referred to as the collector drop and is considered to be drop that gains the mass at the expense of the smaller drop in the collision. This smaller drop is usually called the collected drop.

**Brownian Motion** 

Gas molecules and particles suspended in a gas move around randomly due to the temperature of the gas which is a measure of the kinetic energy of the gas motion. This thermal motion is random and dominates for small particles (<1 micrometer). Cloud droplets grow quickly beyond the one micrometer size via condensational growth. Therefore Brownian motion is a les dominant process for cloud droplets larger than the one micrometer range.

**Turbulent Shear**

Turbulent motion, often occurring in the planetary boundary layer, produces eddies with length scales from several 100 meters to the sub-millimetre range, where turbulent eddies dissipate into Brownian motion or heat. Turbulent shear occurs on scales that are larger than those for Brownian motion. Therefore, turbulence can lead to more collisions of larger particles where Brownian motion is less efficient. With respect to Brownian motion and sedimentation, turbulence is never the dominant process and is only important when sedimentation and brownian motion are ineffective - between particles of similar size above the size at which Brownian range is efficient.

**Sedimentation**

Sedimentation is the gravitational settling of particles in the atmosphere. Particle smoving with different fall velocities will collide if they are moving along the same track. Fall velocities increase with particle size, therefore sedimentation becomes dominant for collisions with larger particles of difference sizes. For particles of the same size, sedimentation becomes inefficient due to having similar velocities. Sedimentation via differential fall velocities plays the most dominant role for collisions between cloud droplets.

| Particle Size Category | Flow Regime  | Drag Force Dependence                        | Fall Velocity Dependence                 |
| ---------------------- | ------------ | -------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| Small (r < 30 µm)      | Laminar      | Linearly dependent on velocity & radius      | Increases **quadratically** with radius  |
| Intermediate sized     | Transitional | —                                            | Increases **linearly** with radius       |
| Large (r > 500 µm)     | Turbulent    | Quadratically dependent on velocity & radius | Increases with **square root** of radius |

For differential fall velocities to play a role, clearly, the cloud droplets must have different fall speeds. Therefore, there needs to be a sufficiently broad size distribution. Cloud droplets are not monodisperse due to the the differences in the properties of CCNs and environmental conditions. Different CCNs have different sizes and chemical compositions, even in relatively homogenous environments, hence, the each's Koehler Curve is different, affecting the growth rate of different cloud droplets, thereby creating a size distribution. Furthermore, fluctuations in the atmospheric environment lead to small variations in temperature and moisture in space. Therefore, even for the exact same CCN, cloud droplets will form and grow differently depending on their situation in space.

However, the inertia of droplets with respect to the background air flow must also be considered. The background air flow moves around droplets. Thus, droplets with insufficient inertia will follow the trajectories of the air flow and avoid collisions, even if their sizes are different. Hocking (1959) showed that the collector droplet must have radii greater than 18 micrometers for collisions to occur. 

Cloud droplets are typically 10 micrometers in radius and are therefore below Hocking's (1959) 18 micrometer threshold. However, the constant mixing and exchange of heat and moisture within a cloud and its environment produce a broad size distribution of cloud droplets, producing a small fraction with radii greater than the 18 micrometer threshold, thereby enabling the initiation of collisions.

### Coalescence

Not only must droplets collide, but they must also coalesce in order to produce larger cloud droplets. There are three scenarios which may occur upon colliding.
1. The two droplets bounce apart and preserve their original size and properties.
2. The droplets coalesce to form a single new droplet but break apart after a short period of time.
3. The droplets coalesce to form a single new droplet and remain together.

Small cloud droplets are more likely to coalesce than not. Cloud droplets rarely will simply bounce apart and will rarely break apart because the surface tension of the newly coalesced droplet is sufficiently strong to prevent break up. This is less applicable for rain droplets which are larger and thus have larger kinetic energies that need to be absorbed by the new drop. The increased kinetic energy may surpass the surface tension, causing separation. Multiplication can also occur whereby a droplet breaks into more than two new droplets. Multiplication is unpredictable due to being a stochastic process - the exact collision point, angle, and shape of the colliding particles determine the outcome. 

The coagulation coefficient is the probability that a drop of radius $R$ collects a droplet of radius $r$ with unit concentration during unit time. It is a combination of the collision and coalescence efficiency - the probability that two droplets first collide, and then the probability that upon colliding, they coalesce. The coagulation equation describes how the number of droplets of a particular radius changes with time due to coagulation. The number of droplets with this certain radius is reduced when droplets of this size coagulate with other droplets. The number is increased when smaller droplets coagulate to form droplets of this radius. The equation can be derived by multiplying the coagulation coefficient with the number concentrations of the colliding droplets. The size distribution's change over time can be calculated by integrating the coagulation equation over all possible combinations of pairs of droplets.

Berry and Reinhardt (1974) calculate the change in size distribution numerically. An initial uni-modal distribution is able to evolve into a bi-modal distribution, where rain droplets are able to form within half an hour, representing the larger mode. The smaller mode does not change much over time, and represents the remaining cloud droplets which have not coagulated. The growth is very sensitive to the initial droplet size distribution. If there are too few initially large (>18/20 micrometer) rain droplets, then coagulation does not occur sufficiently fast for rain to occur, thereby suppressing precipitation. Thus, too many aerosols can result in lots of small cloud droplets, thereby preventing rainfall.

There is an upper limit to the coagulation of rain droplets. Too large droplets will break apart upon colliding due to their high kinetic energy which breaks up droplets into several smaller droplets. Aerodynamical break up breaks up large raindrops which become dynamically unstable when they fall in a turbulent flow. Thus there is an upper limit for raindrop sizes - in the 5-7 mm range.

# Ice microphysical processes

Ice-phase precipitation forms via nucleation, diffusional growth, secondary ice production, and collection processes. These stages govern ice crystal initiation, growth, and transformation into precipitation particles.

## Ice Nucleation

Ice formation in the atmosphere is a nucleation process, analogous to cloud droplet activation. Nucleation can be homogenous or heterogenous.

### Homogenous freezing

Homogenous freezing involves the freezing of pure water droplets or water vapour without the presence of an ice nuclei. Homogenous freezing requires the development of an embryonic ice nucleus formed by the random arrangement of water molecules into an ice lattice. Larger droplets contain more water molecules, increasing the statistical likelihood of a spontaneous embryo forming. Pure water droplets freeze spontaneously below -38 degrees C, which is important for cloud tops in convective columns where temperatures can reach below -38 degrees C ([[Ackerman and Knox, 2015]]).

Homogenous deposition does not occur because it requires an ice embryo to form by chance collision between vapour molecules which is impossible under atmospheric conditions. The homogenous nucleation of a liquid droplet would occur first, then followed by homogenous freezing. Though even homogenous nucleation is very unlikely under atmospheric conditions.

### Heterogeneous Freezing

Heterogeneous freezing is aided by the presence of foreign substances or suspended particles (ice nuclei) which enables freezing at warmer temperatures than homogenous freezing. Ice nuclei can facilitate freezing through the following processes.

**Heterogenous deposition:**

Water vapour molecules are directly deposited on the particle's surface as ice. The crystal structure between ice nuclei and ice is different, therefore heterogenous deposition requires temperatures much lower than the freezing point for ice to stabilise on the IN surface The exact temperature is dependent on the physical and chemical properties of the ice nucleus, but it is typically below -15 degrees Celsius.

**Condensation followed by freezing**

A liquid cloud droplet is formed first which then freezes. This cloud droplet is different to a typical cloud droplet because a solid, insoluble particle acts as the CCN. Therefore, the IN remains solid within the liquid cloud droplet and it can act as a nucleus for ice formation. Similarly, temperatures below the freezing point are required, but freezing is easier than heterogenous deposition for the same type of IN. Usually experiments cannot distinguish between a deposition event and an event followed by freezing.

**Contact Freezing**

An existing supercooled cloud droplet gets into contact with a solid particle, then freezing can happen by growing ice starting fro this nucleus at the surface. Again, if the ice nucleus is not an ice particle then temperatures well below the point of freezing are required.

**Immersion Freezing**

Like contact freezing, this process starts from an already existing liquid water droplet plus a separate solid ice nucleus. In contrast to contact freezing the ice nucleus does not stay at the surface but is submerged into the droplet where the freezing process can then start by growing ice from within the droplet.

___
 
 As for homogenous freezing, all heterogeneous freezing processes are stochastic processes governed by probability because freezing depends on the alignment of molecules. Therefore, the longer cloud droplets spend in a supercooled environment and the larger they are, the more likely the will freeze. Therefore, it can't be determined whether a droplet will be frozen under certain conditions and, unlike the Koehler Curve governing liquid droplet growth, no closed theoretical description of freezing exists. Therefore, laboratory experiments and empirical relationships are required to quantitatively describe freezing processes in the atmosphere. 

IN concentrations are very low - 1 per 10 million aerosols. They typically survive the cloud formation process due to being inefficient cloud condensation nuclei, and can therefore act as ice nuclei later.

Effective IN examples: silver iodide (–4 °C), kaolinite (–9 °C), organic materials like spores (as warm as –2 °C).
Silver iodide has a similar hexagonal crystal lattice structure to ice, therefore making it easier for water molecules to arrange themselves into an ice lattice, thereby permitting freezing at warmer temperatures.

## Secondary Ice Production

Observed ice crystal concentrations often exceed what nucleation alone can explain. Ice multiplication compensates for this discrepancy. 

**Hallett-Mossop Process (Rime splitering)**

The Hallett-Mossop process (Hallett and Mossop, 1974), or rime splintering, is an ice multiplication process which occurs when a graupel - a mixture of snow and hail that forms through the depositional growth of snow crystals or the partial melting and re-freezing of snow - collides with supercooled water droplets. Contact freezing occurs upon collision, however the freezing process takes a finite amount of time, forming different forms of ice depending on the size of the water droplet and the temperature.

| Condition                                 | Temperature Range | Droplet Size    | Outcome                                                         |
| ----------------------------------------- | ----------------- | --------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Rapid freezing                            | T < –8 °C         | Any             | Droplet freezes quickly and attaches to graupel; no splinters.  |
| Slow freezing with spreading              | T > –3 °C         | Any             | Droplet spreads and freezes on graupel; no splinters.           |
| Rime splintering (Hallett-Mossop process) | –8 °C < T < –3 °C | >25 µm diameter | Droplet partially freezes and splinters into new ice particles. |
| No rime splintering (too small)           | –8 °C < T < –3 °C | <25 µm diameter | Droplet remains intact due to surface tension.                  |
Rime splintering is stochastic, depends on kinetic energy of collision, and increases ice crystal concentrations under the right conditions.

The relatively warm conditions for rime splintering are not typically conducive to the formation of ice in the first place. Graupel particles that induce rime splintering typically originate from higher and colder parts of the cloud through mixing.

**Fragmentation**

Collisions between ice particles can lead to fragmentation, producing smaller splinters. Snowflakes break apart more easily when colder - wet snow is more stable due to liquid bridging ice crystals, increasing cohesion.

**Droplet shattering**

When droplets freeze upon contact freezing, large droplets can shatter and break into smaller splinters

**Sublimation Fragmentation**

Ice particles have very complex shapes, and different parts may be connected via narrow bridges. When these ice particles sublimate in an undersaturated environment with respect to ice, these bridges can sublimate, thereby leaving several individual splinters.

## Diffusional growth of ice

Ice particles, as with water droplets, grow through diffusional or depositional growth. Diffusional growth is limited by the diffusion of water vapour towards the ice particle and of heat away from the ice particle. The latent heat released during depositional growth is 10% larger than the latent heat released during condensational growth. 

Unlike water droplets, Ice crystals do not have a singular common shape. Therefore depositional growth cannot be described using a singular size parameter such as the radius for water droplets. Instead, the capacitance is used which is calculated for the known geometries of ice crystals.

| Particle Shape | Capacitance Formula                             | Notes                                     |
| -------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| Sphere         | $C = r$                                         | $r$: radius                               |
| Disk           | $C = \frac{2r}{\pi}$                            | $r$: radius                               |
| Needle         | $C = \frac{A}{\ln\left(\frac{a + A}{b}\right)}$ | $a, b$: semi-axes, $A = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ |
Further qualities of depositional growth are required into order accurately describe it:

**Ventilation**

For non-spherical particles the near field is not radially symmetric which can enhance the ventilation around the particle, increasing its growth rate. However, ventilation effects and their dependence on different particle shapes are quantitatively poorly constrained.

**Changing shape**

Due to the curvature along edges, corners, and tips, the local equilbrium saturation vapour pressure is higher - the Gibbs-Thomson effect. Therefore, air is locally less supersaturated around areas of high curvature relative to the air around the ice particle. Consequently, this creates a vapour gradient towards high-curvature regions. This causes more vapour to condense at tips and edges, causing preferential growth.

The shape depends on the environmental conditions - temperature and supersaturation with respect to ice. The crystal history also affects ice particle shapes. Ice particles can be formed under one set of environmental conditions and then be moved to an area of different environmental conditions. Therefore, knowing the actual environmental conditions of an ice particle is insufficient to determine its shape because its conditions will change over time and space.

### Wegener–Bergeron–Findeisen Process

The saturation vapour pressures over water and ice are different. The saturation vapour pressure of ice is always below that of water, with a maximum difference at -12 degrees C. In a cold environment prior to ice formation. there will be many supercooled liquid cloud droplets with very few ice crystals present. Thus, before significant ice formation, the the water vapour concentration in the environment is close to saturation for liquid water. However, upon the formation of ice, the ice crystals will find themselves in a strongly supersaturated environment due to the saturation vapour pressure being lower than that of water. Consequently, ice crystals grow rapidly by diffusional growth, thereby reducing the water vapour concentration in the environment below that for liquid water but remaining near or above that for ice. Consequently, supercooled liquid droplets are now in a sub-saturated environment, causing them to evaporate, providing increased water vapour concentration which is then drawn to ice crystals. Therefore, few ice crystals grow at the expense of many water droplets, permitting the fast development of precipitation. The cold rain process therefore dominates over the warm rain process.

The Wegener–Bergeron–Findeisen Process occurs most rapidly at -15 degrees C which balances the maximum vapour pressure differential at -12 degrees C with efficient latent heat dissipation,

**Cloud Seeding**

The Wegener–Bergeron–Findeisen Process explains natural cloud seeding. Cirrus clouds at high altitudes produce a few ice crystal which are large enough to slowly sediment downwards. However, due to their low water content, downward sedimentation will not produce precipitation at the ground. However, if ice crystals fall through cloud layers below containing supercooled cloud droplets, then the Wegener–Bergeron–Findeisen Process will quickly produce precipitation. 

Artificial cloud seeding can also be used to generate precipitation from pre-existing clouds with supercooled droplets. 
## Growth by accretion and aggregation

Collision and coalescence grows ice crystals. The most efficient accretion process is the collisions of larger ice particles with supercooled cloud droplets which freeze upon contact and add new layers of ice through riming. Aggregation occurs when two ice crystals coalesce. 

**Accretion:** ice crystal grows via droplet deposition
**Aggregation:** ice crystal grows via coalescing with other crystals

Fall velocities of ice particles are dependent on the shape and density, and are typically less than a meter per second, in contrast to rain in the millimetre size range which falls at velocities between seven and nine meters per second. 

The collection efficiency is near unity for collisions between particles of different phase (ice crystal and liquid droplet), independent of temperature. For collisions between ice crystals, the collection efficiency is strongly dependent on temperature. When temperatures are too low, the ice particles' surfaces are too hard to enable a bond. Ice crystal aggregation is only typically significant for temperatures warmer than -10 degrees C.

The size distributions of ice precipitation particles tend to follow power laws that have the same analytical form as the Marshall Palmer distribution for rain drops. However, the parameters in the power laws are much more variable than for rain, differing for different types of ice. Therefore, the power laws can be difficult to predict.

The size of ice crystals does not have an upper constraint because they are not broken apart by collisional break up or aerodynamic break up. Thus, the size of particles is only limited by the amount of time particles remain in the atmosphere before they fall out which is a function of the strength of updrafts with respect to the ice particle's mass.
