# Risk Management and Technology: Case Studies of Tsunami Evacuation Drills in Japan

Quick summary: encouraging individuals, especially the elderly, to actively take part in Tsunami drills to reduce their vulnerability

## Discussion

- In the context of risk management, vulnerabilities to various hazards (*and their interrelations*) must be identified and those who are vulnerable must be empowered to address the origin of their vulnerabilities.
- The magnitude and scale of the tsunami risk in Okitsu discouraged community members, especially the elderly, from undertaking tsunami management measures
	- “I’m already old. I would rather be swept away with my house in a tsunami,” “given that tsunami predictions are so serious, I’ve started to believe that it is meaningless to undertake further preparations for tsunami risk.”
- From a traditional psychological perspective, elderly people tend to lack motivation in terms of physical mobility [[Riad et al., 1999]]
- Involvement of technological tools provided individuals with one-to-one practice for tsunami evacuation
- Success in drills switched the mindset of evacuees - demonstrating to them that they *could* in fact successfully evacuate from a tsunami 
- [[Nayaka et al., 2018]] found that those who had taken part in previous evacuation drills before the Tohoku Earthquake and Tsunami were significantly more likely to evacuate than those who did not participate.
- Allowing participants to take part in 'single-person drills' gave participants agency over their own evacuation.
- This contrasts with traditional drills where the time, route, and destination are all preemptively decided by community leaders or government officials
- 'performed the role of initiative decision-makers other than passive followers' (p. 9)
- Additionally, self-drills allowed participants to perform trial-and-error behaviours, allowing them to experiment with various methods of evacuation rather than following a single government plan.
- Changes to participant's daily behaviours - 'I decide to visit the shelter every day from now on'

**Multi-Hazard Link**

- "we are walking on a road specifically paved for evacuation. But what should we do if this road collapses after an earthquake?"
- Trial-and-error style of drills allows for participants to try different scenarios.
- Since tsunami risk is very high following seismic shaking on the land, this is an essential component for effective multi-hazard mitigation 

**Communication**
- Communication must go both ways, between experts and the community.
- Communication in focus group sessions and community workshops has been useful for improving the relationships between science information users and producers in the context of risk management [[Crawford et al., 2018]]

**Limitations of study**
- study primarily focused on older adults - the appeal of the drill to younger persons requires revision.
- Younger people are more confident in their ability to quickly run to a high elevation location making them less likely to participate in drills.
