# Volcanic Ash in Kamchatka as a Source of Potential Hazard To Air Traffic

- 70 cyclones pass through Kamchatka each year, primarily in autumn and winter
- They migrate from Japan along the Kamchatkan east coast to the Aleutian Islands or into the Bering Sea, producing strong winds
- Highest altitudes of volcanic ash have been determined to be between 18-21 km
- Analysis of prevalent wind indicates the following over Kamchatka:
	- Wind directions change with the seasons, indicative of Kamchatka's monsoon-like character
	- Prevailing wind in P-K is to the northwest, southeast, and east
**Avachinsky**

- 27 km north of P-K
- January 13th 1001 eruptions resulted in ash falling  in P-K within an hour of the initial eruption.
- Area of ashfall was 400-500 km2 - Melekestev, 1991
- Eruption continued until January 24th with intense ashfalls on Jan 16 and 18th

- other volcanoes can affect air traffic
- primary impacts on air travel are when ash travels eastward toward the Alutiean islands
