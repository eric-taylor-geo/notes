# Where are we with multi-hazards, multi-risks assessment capacities?

**Multirisk assessment versus single-risk assessment for disaster risk management**

- One of the challenges for disaster risk management (DRM) is to prioritise the risks originating from different hazards to enable decisions on appropriate and cost-effective mitigation or preparedness measures.
- Heterogeneous methods of examining risks to individual hazards [[Marzocchi et al., 2012]] and the complex interactions between hazards hamper multirisk assessment 
- A building’s vulnerability to ground shaking may increase due to additional structural loads following volcanic ash fall or heavy snowfall (Lee and Rosowsky, 2006; Zuccaro et al., 2008; Selva, 2013). Vulnerability in these cases would be highly time variant.
- ![[Pasted image 20250323215846.png]]
- The Agenda 21 for Sustainable Development (UNEP, 1992), the Johannesburg Plan for Implementation (UN, 2002), the Hyogo Framework for Action (UNDRR, 2005) and the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (UNDRR, 2015) promote multihazard risks of natural hazards.
- Relative importance of different hazards and risks must be considered for a given region.
- However, different hazards differ in their nature, have different return periods, have different intensities, and have different effects. In referenced units, many are non-comparable.
- This does not only hamper the comparability between the threats, but it also makes it difficult to aggregate the single perils in a meaningful way in order to assess the total threat coming from all the hazards in a region.
- Thus, to overcome them, and as a first step towards a full multirisk assessment, one may treat them in the context of a multilayer single-hazard/ risk assessment approach, ignoring the interactions but harmonising and standardising the assessment procedures among the different perils
- Three major standardisation schemes can be distinguished in this context (Kappes et al., 2012; Papathoma-Köhle, 2016). They make use of:
	1. Hazard, vulnerability, and risk matrices
	2. Hazard, vulnerability, and risk indices
	3. Hazard, vulnerability, and risk curves
- The single-hazard-specific curves can be aggregated to obtain a total risk curve without considering the potential interactions between hazards [[Fleming et al., 2016]]

**Dynamic vulnerability: time- and state-dependent**

- The term ‘time-dependent vulnerability’ is used in the engineering community for distinguishing between the gradual deterioration of a building’s fragility due to corrosion and the abrupt changes when an earthquake strikes.
	- Highlights importance of assessing structural integrity following each ground shaking event - done in P-K
- State-dependent vulnerability changes occur when two hazards interact on the vulnerability level, and the first one alters the exposure or the state of exposed elements in a way that changes the response of the elements in the second.
- E.g., a building loaded by snow or ash and then exposed to an earthquake - likely for P-K!
- Zuccaro et al. (2008), Marzocchi et al. (2012), Garcia-Aristizabal et al. (2013) and Selva (2013) gave the example of the seismic vulnerability of buildings loaded by ash due to volcanic activity
- Selva (2013) presented an example for state-dependent exposure. In this case, strong local earthquakes changed the exposure to a tsunami by people escaping from their damaged buildings and concentrating in seaside areas, which is where tsunamis hit
- Garcia-Aristizabal et al. (2013) found that in Naples, the expected loss from earthquakes was remarkably sensitive to the thickness of an ash layer from volcanic activity assumed to load the roofs of the area’s buildings. Whereas for a 24-cm ash layer the expected loss from earthquakes increased by less than 20% compared to the case without load, it reached an amplification factor of six for a 41-cm thick layer.

## Barriers to implementation

- Barriers to effectively implementing multirisk assessment into DRM are found in both the scientific and practical domains and in between them.
- Individaul perceptual and cognitive barriers also may play a role in both domains [[Komendantova et al., 2016]]

**Science**

- Barriers in science domain stem from the lack of common stands for multirisk assessment across disciplines
- Even no consensus on the term 'multirisk'
- Lack of quantitative information is perhaps even more purvasive. Even with a 'perfect' coordination in how risk is assessed between spaces and different hazards, the lack of data or physical understanding of hazards greatly reduces the ability to appropriately judge and manage risk.

**Practical**

- Enforcement of building codes
- Perception of risk - low frequency events can be underestimated
	- Requires long term commitment 
- Link with pysch studies
## Conclusions

**Knowledge**

- Although theoretical frameworks exist, there is a requirement for a quantitative understanding of the environment
- Comprehensive databases needed for a multirisk assessment either do not exist, are not freely available, or are insufficient; such databases need to be established between the disciplines.
- Vulnerability information has mostly been conducted for specific cases, mostly related to the direct impact of the disaster rather than indirect consequences. More work needs to be done to understand indirect disaster impacts such as loss of work productivity, loss of systems and networks, cost of evacuation, medical assistance, and recovery.
