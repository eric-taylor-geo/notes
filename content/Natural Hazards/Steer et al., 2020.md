# Earthquake statistics changed by typhoon-driven erosion

Builds on [[Liu et al., 2009]]
- Significant increase in shallow (<15 km depth) earthquake frequency for at least 2.5 years following the typhoon Marakot, which hit Taiwan in 2009.
- Delivered 3 m of precipitation, causing exceptional landsliding and erosion.
- Scaling of earthquake magnitude and frequency underwent a sudden increase in the area where mass wasting was most intense.
- This suggests that the removal of landslide debris by rivers from southern Taiwan has acted to increase the crustal stress rate to the extent that earthquake activity was demonstrably affected.
	- Not universally observed - other examples of landslide activity following a typhoon (e.g., Chi-Chi earthquake, 1999) did not produce changes in seismicity, potentially because landslides were distant from rivers, resulting in little debris evacuation.
- Study is the first evidence of the impact of a single weather-driven erosion event on tectonics.
- B-value following typhoon Marokot increased for 2 years, thus indicating the increased frequency of smaller earthquakes with respect to larger earthquakes.
