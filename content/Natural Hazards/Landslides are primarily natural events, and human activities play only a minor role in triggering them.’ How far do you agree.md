A landslide can be defined as “rapid displacement of a mass of rock, residual soil, or sediments adjoining a slope, in which the centre of gravity of the moving mass advances in a downward and outward direction” ([[Terzaghi, 1950, p.1]]). Globally, landslides are heterogeneously distributed in space and time and are concentrated in areas of steep topography, such as High Mountain Asia, during the monsoon season ([[Froude and Petley, 2018]]). Their incidence is also increasing, and indeed, 2024 had the highest number of landslides in documented history ([[Petley, 2024]]). Their increased occurrence is attributed to several factors, namely, better detection of landslides using remote sensing techniques, climate change altering hydrometeorological triggers of landslides, and anthropogenic activity triggering or degrading slopes to be more vulnerable to landslides ([[Jaboyedoff et al., 2016]]; [[Muñoz-Torrero Manchado et al., 2021]]). Their impacts are also increasing due to the increased human occupation of mountainous areas ([[Fidan et al., 2024]]). There is no question that human activities play a role in triggering landslides, particularly those which are most fatal and difficult to mitigate – this essay from the outset unequivocally disagrees with the essay question. This disagreement will be explained by demonstrating human involvement in the direct triggers of landslides, and practices which may not necessarily trigger landslides, but may degrade slopes, increasing their susceptibility to failure. A trigger is hereafter defined as a single event with a start and end which results in a slope failure, whilst degrading factors cumulatively, over time, weaken slopes, enhancing their disposition to failure ([[Jaboyedoff et al., 2016]]).

It is further important to clarify how this essay distinguishes between the ‘natural’ and the ‘human’ for landslide triggers. The most significant global landslide trigger stems from a hydrometeorological hazard, rainfall ([[Froude and Petley, 2018]]). Seismic activity also plays a strong role in tectonically active regions ([[Keefer, 2002]]). Landslides triggered by such events are classified as having ‘natural’ triggers. ‘Human’ triggers, meanwhile, are defined as those caused by direct human activity ([[Fidan et al., 2024]]). For example, construction, slope-cutting, and mining. However, it must be acknowledged that this division is in fact blurred because anthropogenic activity affects natural triggers, especially under anthropogenically induced climate change ([[Fidan et al., 2024]]). This interaction is further explored in the final paragraph.

First, the causes of landslides. Key to understanding landslides from an engineering perspective is the factor of safety (FoS) which describes the ratio between resisting forces and driving forces on a slope ([[Terzaghi, 1950]]). An FoS greater than 1 indicates that a slope is stable whilst conversely, an FoS less than 1 indicates that a slope is unstable – though it may not have necessarily failed. Several factors affect the FoS, most importantly, the slope angle, load, material strength, and pore pressure ([[Sidle and Ochiai, 2006]]). Activities, whether natural or human, can alter these factors, lowering the FoS, triggering a landslide ([[Sidle and Ochiai, 2006]]). Having established the factors controlling the trigger of landslides, the role of natural and human factors in their triggering can be assessed.

From a natural perspective, increased loads from snowfall or rainfall, rainfall’s respective effect on increasing soil pore pressure, and seismic shaking can reduce the FoS. They can either reduce the FoS sufficiently to trigger a landslide within a single event, or degrade the FoS over successive events whose cumulative effect is that of one single larger event ([[Jaboyedoff et al., 2016]]). This is an example of a key multi-hazard interaction between other natural hazards and landslides – hazards can either directly cascade and trigger a landslide, or alternatively lower the FoS, thereby increasing the likelihood of failure during successive events ([[Jaboyedoff et al., 2016]]; [[Tilloy et al., 2019]]). Rainfall is the largest driver of landslides globally, particularly in tropical and temperate climates where monsoon seasonality brings heavy rainfall in short periods ([[Froude and Petley, 2018]]). Cycles between wet and dry seasons result in contraction cycles which produce cracks in slope regolith, lowering its FoS ([[Capitani et al., 2013]]). Given that rainfall is the largest driver, it could be plausible to attribute natural factors as the primary trigger of landslides. However, this perspective is too simplified and obscures the influence of human activities on slope vulnerability.

Human activity both directly triggers landslides and affects slope vulnerability. The direct triggering of unintended landslides occurs when the geotechnical properties of slopes and their evolution over time are poorly assessed ([[Jaboyedoff et al., 2016]]). The clearest activities triggering landslides are mining and the creation of artificial slopes, such as spoil heaps. For example, the 1966 Aberfan disaster in Wales saw the collapse of an overloaded spoil heap, causing 144 deaths ([[Siddle et al., 1996]]). However, there was also natural involvement – the spoil heap was constructed on a known natural spring, and the months leading up to the disaster had been especially rainy, saturating the spoil heap ([[Siddle et al., 1966]]). Thus, there remains an interaction between human and natural activity. However, more prevalent is the influence of human activities on slope vulnerability. Slope re-profiling lowers the FoS by modifying the slope gradient. It is highly prevalent in the building of roads in areas of high relief ([[Robson et al., 2025]]), where roads are cut into mountainsides, steepening mountain slopes on the inner side of roads ([[Hearn, 2011]]). Consequently, whilst the building of roads themselves does not induce landslides, so long as appropriate building measures are in place, the combination of a vulnerable slope with a later natural event, such as rainfall or an earthquake ([[Petley et al., 2007]]; [[Hearn, 2011]]), can cause landslides. Due to road building and other human activities, landslides occur at a much greater rate in the vicinity of human activity compared to further away, lending evidence that human activity plays a strong role in the direct trigger of slope failures or their degradation. These landslides are highly localised, and, due to their concentration in areas of human activity, have high impacts on people – for example Nepal’s recent rapid and haphazard expansion of its road network in rural, mountain areas, has been associated with a large increase in landslides and impacts, with Nepal accounting for 10% of global rainfall-triggered fatal landslides ([[Froude and Petley, 2018]]). Therefore, such landslides cannot be said to be primarily natural events, challenging this notion. Human activities, whilst not necessarily being the sole trigger of landslides, still play a large role in modifying the FoS, making slopes more conducive to failure. Furthermore, they are of particular importance because, inherently, landslides in and around areas of human activity will have greater human impacts.

Human activities further play a role in natural landslide triggers. However, directly assessing their role in landslides is difficult because human impacts may occur over long time periods, making it more difficult to link them to landslides directly ([[Depicker et al., 2021]]). The climate is changing due to anthropogenic activity ([[IPCC, 2023]]). It has already been established that meteorological events are a key trigger of landslides. Thus, the modification of meteorological events which affect landslides due to anthropogenic climate change can be said to have human involvement. Monsoons are forecasted to increase in intensity due to increased evaporation ([[Kitoh, 2017]]). Thus, the landslide risk increases as more intense rainfall results in more significant events, which reduce the FoS. In areas of human activity, human involvement is thus twofold – first is their modification to slopes due to direct activity, which alters the vulnerability of slopes to natural events, and second is their modification to the natural events themselves. In areas that are not directly modified by human activity, climate modification is still relevant and presents risks to human populations through multi-hazard cascades ([[Van Wyk de Vries et al., 2022]]). Climate change has been associated with increased rockfalls and landslides in snow and ice-covered mountains due to permafrost thaw, which weakens mountainsides ([[Deline, 2009]]). Coupled with the growth of supra- and pro-glacial lakes, the probability of the spatial extent of landslides overlapping with the spatial extent of lakes increases. Thus, a potential multi-hazard cascade chain exists, where human-induced climate change modifies high-elevation slopes through thawing, causing landslides which can collapse into lakes, causing glacial lake outburst floods (GLOFs) which cause infrastructure damage and casualties to downstream populations ([[Gurung et al., 2017]]). For example, a GLOF in Sikkim, India, in 2023 was triggered by a landslide into a proglacial lake which had formed due to glacial melt under climate warming ([[Sattar et al., 2025]]). Thus, human activity plays a less clear but still evident role in exacerbating the risk of landslides and their subsequent multi-hazard impacts on human populations in regions further from direct human activity.

To conclude, human-natural-multi-hazard interactions complicate the understanding of the drivers of landslide occurrence. Human activity does directly trigger landslides. But it also has the capacity to lower the FoS sufficiently so that a natural event, which may not otherwise have had the capability to cause a landslide by itself, triggers a landslide. Furthermore, human activity is altering our climate under anthropogenic climate change ([[IPCC, 2023]]), altering the frequency and intensity of natural events ([[Kitoh, 2017]]). Therefore, from a multi-hazard perspective, where landslides are a singular component within a more complex multi-hazard framework, human activities’ involvement in triggering landslides, especially those which have the greatest human impacts, is not only inevitable, but significant ([[Van Wyk de Vries, 2025]]). Thus, to answer the question – I disagree with the statement. Natural events such as rainfall may still be the largest individual trigger of landslides, but human activities play a significant role in modifying the predisposition of slopes, enabling the triggering of landslides which may not have otherwise occurred.
