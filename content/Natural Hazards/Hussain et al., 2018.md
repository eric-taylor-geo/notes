# Constant strain accumulation rate between major earthquakes on the North Anatolian Fault

- Earthquakes are caused by the release of tectonic strain accumulated between events
- Advances in satellite geodesy permit the measurement of interseismic strain accumulation with a high degree of accuracy.
- Strain accumulation rates calculated from geodetic measurements along the North Anatolian Fault are constant in space. 
- Given that different points along the fault have had earthquakes at different times and are thus at different points in their earthquake cycles, it is evidence that the strain accumulation rate is not only constant over space but also constant over time over the 250-year interseismic period along the NAF - except in the 10 years following an earthquake.
- Thus, short-term geodetic observations can directly contribute to long-term seismic hazard assessment.

**Multi-hazard context and climate change**

- The earthquake was coupled with heavy snow and extreme cold temperatures. Storms and flooding exacerbated impacts on people living in tents.
- Heatwave (42 degrees) when people had moved from tents into metal containers - offer little protection from extreme heat.
- Further human impacts: homelessness, missed school, political instability, geopolitical instability between Turkey and Syria.
- Under climate change which hazards get worse? It is not so much the geological hazards (albeit, there is some -> [[Liu et al., 2009]]; [[Steer et al., 2020]]), but rather the hydrometeorological hazards which cascade from geological hazards. These will intensify with climate change.
