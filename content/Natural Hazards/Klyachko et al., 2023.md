## 2 The Software Block Approach to Ensuring the Sea Coast Sustainable Safety

- The Kamchatka Peninsula, the eastern region of the Russian Federation, located in the northwestern part of the Pacific Rim, has extremely diverse ground conditions and is subject to the country’s most dangerous volcanic eruptions, earthquakes, hurricanes, and tsunamis. Thus, in connection with it, since the late 1960s this territory has become an experimental site for engineering seismometric observations on residential buildings of various design solutions and for testing the seismic resistance of buildings with various types of seismic isolation.
- In the late 1980s and early 1990s, the Russian Federation's Center on Earthquake Engineering and Natural Disaster Reduction developed the "PREventitive Sustainable Safety" (PRESS) to protect the diverse socioeconomic urbanisation systems subject to unmanaged natural impacts [26] (Klyachko, 1989)
- PRESS consists of two programs
	- Program Risk Analysis (PRANA)
	- Program Risk Management (PRIMA)
- PRESS is complex for it requires the consideration of additional probable tsunami triggers (underwater / coastal landslides), secondary natural and anthropogenic impacts, and simulataneous/associated disasters -> *multi hazard*
- In the 1990s, tsunamis were seen as a cascading hazard, with their genesis, earthquakes, being the primary hazard.
- 80% of tsunamis are seismogenic -> therefore that is where the focus lies
- Greatest disasters on sea coasts occur from near-shore earthquakes and subsequent seismogenic tsunamis

- Informatics-Analytics DataBASE (DIABSE) of tsunami hazardous area is developd using:

- Database is constantly updated:

## 3 Methods and Mechanisms/the Toolbox for the Tsunami Risk Analysis and Management

- Disaster mitigation tools should be subdivided into research, engineering, regulatory, educational, and special regulation methods. The fact that these areas of activity are carried out by specialists of various profiles and officials shows that coordination of this complex work is required in order to gain the effective result.
- Considers the worst possible scenariors:
	- Local tsunami
	- Closely located seismic trigger
	- Additional tsunami amplifying factors (e.g. underwater and coastal landslides similar to 2018 Indonesia Tsunami ([65 and 66]))

### 3.1 Tsunami Warning System

- TSW is integrated with Pacific warnings (not specifically mentioned in this paper, find different citation)
- Automatic forecast immediately after M>7 earthquake
- It should be taken into account that the Tsunami Warning System (TWS) may fail to work and maybe late with an alarm. Moreover, repeated false alarms of the Tsunami Warning System prevent people from responding to real danger in a timely and correct manner. Even the most advanced TWS in Japan could not prevent the death of more than 14,000 people from the tsunami on March 11, 2011
- More technological work required -> increase number of tsunami buoys, submarine cables used, satellite data, methods to calculate characeristics

- Education and training of the population for tsunami disaster preparedness play an important role in tsunami mitigation. In this direction of disaster management, we will note:
	- When developing leaflets for the population, cultural, mental, age, psychophysical, housing, and other characteristics of the population should be taken into account, avoiding simple duplication of global standard recommendations.
	- Advance preparation of a family action plan (in emergency situations) and an emergency kit.
	- Advance knowledge of the procedure, routes, and final evacuation place, including the vertical evacuation.
	- During the training, it is especially important to consolidate the methods and techniques of response by numerous repetitions to bring the reaction to automatism.

- Important to tsunami risk mitigation:
	- technically impossible and economically inexpedient to get fully protected against a tsunami
	- The main objective of tsunami protection is to minimise the risk associated with people's life and health, while the acceptable risk of material damage is determined by the owner
- Rules govern structures - Set of Rules 292
	- Hydrodynamic loads
	- Weighing forces
	- Suffusion
	- Soil base liquefaction
- Special attention is paid to critical infrastructure and key facilities - facilities and life support networks, difficult to evacuate buildings, dangerous obkects
- The availability and timely TWS operation is considered - including whether it fails
- Rules 292 dictages:
	- Facility location must be outside tsunami hazard area or must be in tsunami protected areas
	- Space planning solutions to achieve maximum streamlining and adjustable permeability of the lower floors of coastal buildings
	- Strength of load bearing structures
	- Building of dams, reinforced barriers which are ¼ of a meter above the local calculated vertical splash
	- Measures to prevent large objects and debris from entering the tsunami stream
- Bridges near the coast are most vulnerable to tsunami effects
	- Mitigated by building above maximum flow level, or by engeering solutions which reduce the run-up intensity - e.g., enclosing structures, planting forests
	- Some coastal railways are so narrow that it is impossible to ensure tsunami safety on them - therefore it is recommended to provide dead-end railway branches leading to safe zones where trains can move to after receiving alarms
	- Thus, track destruction is seen as inevitable and will require immediate repair

## Conclusions:

- Because tsunamis are a very rare natural event, experience gained directly from the results of engineering surveys of tsunami disasters is slowly accumulating. That is why the results of natural and laboratory physical modeling of the tsunami interaction with various structures are becoming more and more important and fruitful. Therefore, this scientific and experimental direction should be developed everywhere and as a priority.
