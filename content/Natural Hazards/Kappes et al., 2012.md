# Challenges of analyzing multi-hazard risk: a review

## Multi-Hazard Analysis


- A multi-hazard could be defined as "the totality of relevant hazards in a defined area" [[Kappes, 2011]] (p. 6,7)
- Definition of what is a relevant threshold based on thresholding
	- Spatial, probability, cost, number affected, etc.
- Alternatively, we can define hazards thematically through a 'more-than-one-hazard' approach whereby hazards with strong interrelations (e.g., volcanic eruptions resulting in lava flows, ash fallout etc.) are analysed together.
- Yet, whilst individual processes for individual (or thematically grouped) hazard analyses exist, much fewer studies analyse multiple hazards.
- Experience is therefore lacking.
- Multi-hazard risk analyses are not just the sum of single-hazard risk examinations because:
	- Hazard charcteristics differ - and therefore, also the methods to analyse them
	- Hazards are related and influence eachother -> hazard chains / cascades
	- Natural processes exert diverging impacts on risk and vulnerability
	- A variety of risk description and quantification measures exist and have to be adapted to allow the comparison of multiple risks

- In summary, the comparison of hazards is difficult due to different process characteristics. Classification and index schemes help to overcome this problem; however, they are specifically elaborated for one purpose and/or type of stakeholder and can thus only be applied in the respective specific context. Menoni (2006, p. 10) concludes that “[i]t is hard to find common units of measures” that are useful for emergency managers as well as urban and regional planners since they “face specific problems provoked by hazards in a given context.” A possible solution to this problem is to analyze multi-hazard risk instead of multi-hazard. Risks, although emerging from different hazards, are directly comparable since they describe and/or quantify the possible consequences in numbers or probabilities of loss of life, injury, damages, etc. For the calculation of risks, the possible impact of hazards has to be related to the vulnerability of, for example, people, buildings, and infrastructure. Risk is, as highlighted in the definition by Varnes (1984), a function of hazard and vulnerability of the elements at risk. Therefore, vulnerability is a one key element in risk analysis, and in the following, physical vulnerability approaches proposed in a multi-hazard context will be reviewed.

## Analyses of the physical vulnerability for multiple hazards

- Vulnerability assessments are further challenging owing to the spatiotemporal relations of hazards, for they alter vulnerability 
- Relations between hazards complicate vulnerability assessment
- Hazards overlap spatially and temporally, and arising hazard relations may not only influence the overall hazard level, but also the vulnerability of elements at risk
	- E.g., decreasing building vulnerability to one hazard may increase its vulnerability to others
