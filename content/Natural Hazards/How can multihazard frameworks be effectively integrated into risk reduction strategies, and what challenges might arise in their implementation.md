Multihazard frameworks refer to approaches which consider both the selection of major hazards that an area may face, but also accounts for the specific contexts in which hazardous events may occur simultaneously, cascadingly, or cumulatively over time ([[UNDRR, 2023]]). Taking a multihazard approach in thinking about natural hazards and their management is useful as it is holistic, accounting for interactions between hazards and this better reflects observed reality, as hazard events do not occur in isolation ([[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]). Due to the complexities and uncertainties associated with natural hazards, they are often managed individually, as isolated events, however multihazard approaches can and should be increasingly integrated into risk reduction strategies. Use of multihazard approaches in risk management is important to help us prevent any asynergies that can be caused by overlapping, incompatible single hazard risk reduction strategies, and reduce overall social and economic impacts of hazard events ([[de Ruiter, 2020]]; [[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]). This essay aims to outline how multihazard frameworks and the webs of connections between hazards they demonstrate can be created and used within risk reduction strategies, for example through hazard matrices and cascades. It also seeks to outline the challenges associated with using multihazard frameworks in risk reduction and management, including issues of imperfect knowledge surrounding hazard interactions and incompatible management priorities for different hazards.

A method through which multihazard frameworks can be effectively integrated into risk reduction strategies is through the use of hazard matrices to identify relationships and interactions between different natural hazards, which can then be communicated to relevant institutions to create holistic management approaches ([[Gill and Malamud, 2014]]). Hazard interactions can cause direct and indirect risk to populations, and it is important that both sources of risk are identified to allow multihazard risk reduction strategies to be as efficient as possible ([[Hochrainer-Stigler et al., 2023]]). One example of a hazard interaction which needs accounting for is the potential for a primary hazard to trigger a secondary hazard, for example a heavy rainfall event directly causing a landslide, as saturation of land can cause a slope to become unstable and force downhill movement of material ([[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]). Less concrete hazard interactions can also drive risk to populations, through positive feedback cycles and during the occurrence of compound hazard events, where the spatial and temporal overlap of two seemingly unconnected hazards can together create impacts worse than the sum of their parts ([[Tilloy et al., 2019]]; [[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]). These hazard interactions, alongside others such as catalysis and impedance relationships, can be identified using a hazard matrix, such as the one used in [[Gill and Malamud’s, 2016]] paper. By placing all potential hazards which could occur in an area in a matrix, it allows us to identify all of the circumstances in which certain hazards could overlap, and the impact of these interactions on the population in the area concerned ([[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]). Once these interactions are identified, they can be demonstrated to key stakeholders, such as national and local governments, environment agencies, urban planners and local communities, to aid decision making regarding risk reduction and management at a variety of scales. This can ensure that hazard interactions are accounted for, and therefore that risk to vulnerable populations is minimised ([[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]). A multihazard approach to risk reductions needs to remain dynamic, as multihazard interactions are not fixed and are likely to change in the future, for example due to impacts of climate change on creating new interactions and influencing the likelihood and pace of existing interactions ([[Hochrainer-Stigler, 2023]]). Constantly evolving, holistic multihazard frameworks should be integrated into hazard management procedures to ensure risk reduction strategies better reflect the reality of experiencing natural hazards for people in hazardous locations, and this can be done through use of hazard matrices to identify and address these interactions, and communication of these relationships to relevant stakeholders.

However, despite the importance of including holistic multihazard frameworks into risk reduction strategies, there are a number of challenges in their implementationA. One of these is the level of communication required between scientists, decision makers, institutions and communities in understanding multihazard interactions and creating holistic frameworks which truly represent the experience of multihazard events. Natural hazards are commonly managed in an independent and isolated form due to the complexities and uncertainties of different types of hazard- including the scales of impact, frequency, return period and predictability ([[Gill and Malamud, 2014]]). As a result of this, it requires substantial work to understand and manage these hazards coherently together. For example, it is important for affected communities to be able to communicate their experiences of different hazards and their interactions to risk reduction management bodies, in order to construct an accurate multihazard framework for an area. However, there is potential that these local scale perspectives are not listened to as scientific understanding is privileged, which can harm the accuracy of multihazard frameworks being created and implemented. Similarly, poor communication between management bodies for different hazards can occur due to fragmented and contentious vocabulary used in existing risk reduction strategies ([[Tilloy et al., 2019]]). These issues of communication and terminology can make the inclusion of multihazard interactions and frameworks in risk reduction strategies difficult.

Another challenge arising from the inclusion of multihazard frameworks in risk reduction strategies is that it is impossible to account for every relationship between hazards, as interactions are dynamic and unpredictable. Some hazard interactions can be impossible to identify, as multihazard events produce a range of ‘known unknowns’ ([[Sakic-Trogrlic et al., 2024]]). This means that whilst it is known that multihazard interactions produce a range of risks, they are poorly understood due to the complexity of multihazard events, and this lack of understanding can complicate the implementation of accurate and efficient multihazard frameworks ([[Albulescu and Armas, 2024]]). Even in circumstances where the interactions between hazards are known, each hazard event is likely to be different to the previous one, with hazards being of varying severity, or events falling in different socio-economic or political circumstances. Therefore, multihazard risk management strategies can be difficult to plan and implement due to the unpredictable nature of natural hazards and interactions between them ([[Tilloy et al., 2019]]). Additionally, it is important to acknowledge that interactions have potential to change in the future, and new interactions have the potential to emerge ([[Hochrainer-Stigler, 2023]]). Therefore, it is vital that multihazard frameworks remain dynamic in order to be effective, and this constant change can make implementing multihazard approaches into effective risk reduction strategies challenging.

A final challenge of implementing multihazard frameworks into risk reduction protocols is the incompatibility of risk management strategies to individual hazards which can make taking multihazard approaches difficult. Some hazards which interact, such as during compound multihazard events, may require responses which are completely incompatible, making complete multihazard risk management very difficult to achieve. One example of this is the compound hazard event of flooding and the Covid-19 pandemic, where the hazards overlap spatially and temporally. The hands-on nature of sandbagging and evacuation required to tackle flooding hazards is incompatible with the social distancing required to manage the spread of Covid-19, and this led to insufficient responses to both hazards, and resulted in severe impacts larger than the sum of parts ([[Simonovic et al., 2021]]; [[Ishiwatari et al., 2020]]). Therefore, even when the responses required to tackle individual hazards are well-understood, it can be hard to create a multihazard framework of effective responses which suit all hazards in an area that can be properly implemented.

In conclusion, multihazard events are the result of a range of interactions and relationships between individual natural hazards, and whilst complex to identify and plan for, should be included where possible in risk reduction strategies to better represent the reality of multihazard events and reduce social and economic impacts they can cause ([[Gill and Malamud, 2014]]; [[de Ruiter, 2020]]). This can be achieved through the use of hazard matrices to identify the range of hazard interactions in one area, before these findings are reported to appropriate decision makers to plan and implement multihazard frameworks to reduce risk ([[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]). There are challenges associated with this- including issues of required communication, difficulty in identifying hazard interactions, and incompatibilities between hazard management protocols required, meaning creating and implementing an effective multihazard framework can be difficult ([[Sakic-Trogrlic, 2024]]; [[Tilloy et al., 2019]]; [[Simonovic et al., 2021]]). However, it is evident that use of a holistic risk reduction approach is useful in reducing the risk to people and communities, and therefore it should be used wherever possible, particularly as ongoing anthropogenic climate change is likely to create more risks and worsen the frequency and severity of multihazard events in the future.



# How can multihazard frameworks be effectively integrated into risk reduction strategies, and what challenges might arise in their implementation

 Risk is the intersection of a hazard (hazard), an exposed population (exposure), and the likelihood that the hazard impacts them (vulnerability). Thus, risk reduction strategies seek to mitigate one or more of the individual components comprising risk. In the presence of a population facing multiple hazards, mitigating risk becomes more complex because it involves mitigating the risk from multiple hazards, which may not occur in isolation and can occur independently but simultaneously, trigger other hazards via a cascading relationship, or increase the likelihood of another hazard occuring ([[Zschau, 2017]]; [[Tilloy et al., 2019]]). Thus, a _multihazard_ framework is required to be implemented into risk reduction strategies to mitigate risk from the various hazards a population faces appropriately. A holistic overview of multihazards is essential for risk reduction to prevent asynergies, which may be caused by overlapping, incompatible single-hazard risk reduction strategies, in order to reduce the overall socioeconomic impacts of hazard events (Gill and Malamud, 2016). This essay will outline the methods in which multihazard frameworks can be effectively integrated into risk reduction strategies, as well as the challenges which may arise in their implementation, namely, the difficult in finding effective reduction strategies, contrasting language and scientific knowledge and understanding, and the practical implementation of strategies in the real world.

The standardisation of different hazards is one way of initiating the effective integration of multiple hazards into risk reduction strategies. Different hazards differ in nature, impact, and return period, which makes quantitatively comparing and aggregating the hazards in a meaningful way in order to assess the total threat originating from hazards in a region (Zschau, 2017). Thus, to overcome them, and as a first step in creating a multihazard framework, a multilayer single-hazard framework can be used, which standardises the assessment procedures among different hazards. For instance, hazard/risk curves present the likelihood of damage as a function of hazard magnitude ([[Kappes et al., 2012]]). Thus, whilst different hazards will have difficult ways of assessing their magnitude - e.g., moment-magnitude for earthquakes (Hanks and Kanamori (1979) or the volcanic explosivity index (VEI, Newhall and Self, 1982) - having a continuous function over a range of plausible hazard magnitudes enables the aggregation of different hazard curves into a total hazard curve (Flemming et al., 2016). Indeed, Gardia-Arestizabel et al. (2013) present cumulative hazard curves for volcanic and earthquake hazards in Nepal and Italy to demonstrate the cumulative effects of seismic activity associated with volcanic activity. However, a multi-layer single-hazard approach still does not consider the interactions between hazards, and indeed, the impacts of multiple hazards can be more than the sum of the impacts of each occurring in isolation ([[Terzi et al., 2019]]). Thus, considering the interactions between hazards is essential within a multihazard framework and its effective implementation in risk reduction.

A hazard matrix can be used to identify the interactions between natural hazards. This information can then be communicated to relevant authorities and stakeholders to implement effective risk reduction strategies (Gill and Malamud, 2016). Interactions refer to the unidirectional or bidirectional effects between one hazard/process and another hazard/process (Gill and Malamud). Various terminology in the multihazard literature has been used to describe different interactions between hazards, which Tilloy et al. (2019) divide into 5 main categories: 1) hazards can be independent, but occur simultaneously, e.g., a storm occuring at the same time as an earthquake 2) one hazard can directly trigger another, a cascading relationship, e.g., an earthquake triggering a tsunami 3) a hazard can increase the likelihood of another hazard at another point in time, a change-making relationship, e.g., a forest fire denuding vegetation and hardening soil making flooding more likely 4) hazards can be part of a compound event, e.g., lavaflows and ash originating from a volcanic eruption, and 5) hazards can be mutually exclusive, whereby two hazards cannot occur simultaneously in time and space, e.g., heavy rainfall and a forest fire. In using a matrix, all n hazards facing a population are compared with eachother, providing at least n^2 interactions (Gill and Malamud, 2016). Of these n^2 interactions, a smaller proportion will be viable and relevant for disaster risk reduction strategies. Hazard matrices are also visually clear, which is useful for effectively communicating hazard interactions with stakeholders. Upon the identification of relevant hazards, the multihazard framework can be communicated to relevant stakeholders, policymakers, and environmental agencies to develop mitigation strategies which consider hazard interactions to minimise damages. Therefore, hazard standardisation combined with hazard matrices permits the understanding of the threat from multihazards through aggregating multiple hazards into hazard curves (Kappes et al., 2012; Flemming et al., 2016) and through the use of hazard matrices, which permit the identification of hazard interactions (Gill and Malamud, 2016). Upon understanding the threat from multihazards, they can be more effectively mitigated, thereby aiding in reducing risk.

The second part of this essay will discuss the challenges arising in implementing multihazard frameworks in risk reduction strategies. First, a multihazard framework requires a detailed scientific understanding of the hazard processes and interactions at play (Ward et al., 2022). Thus, there is a scientific limitation on how hazards are understood, and gaps in knowledge or databases can hinder the development of multihazard frameworks. Some hazard interactions can be impossible to identify, as multihazard events produce a range of ‘known unknowns’ (Sakic-Trogrlic et al., 2024). Thus, unidentified interactions result in an incomplete multihazard framework which limits its effectiveness at mitigating risk from multihazards. Even in circumstances where interactions are known, actually quantifying the level of interaction is difficult because future hazard events may not have the same magnitudes as previous events, thereby adding a degree of unpredictability in multihazard frameworks (Tilloy et al., 2019). The change of hazards through time demonstrates how multihazard interactions are dynamic, and therefore, multihazard frameworks must be continuously updated to account for the changing nature of hazards. Climate change will affect the likelihood of hazards occurring and thus must be considered; however, the degree of future climate change is itself uncertain, and its impact on future hazards even more so, thereby adding more layers of complexity to dynamic multihazard assessment (Hochrainer-Stigler, 2023). Thus, limits in the scientific understanding of hazards provide a challenge for the effective implementation of multihazard frameworks due to an incomplete understanding of multihazard interactions.

Even with a fully holistic understanding of multihazards, the practical implementation of risk reduction strategies remains challenging. The practical implementation of risk reduction will be broken down into three components: 1) finding suitable risk reduction strategies for multihazards, 2) community adherence to strategies, and 3) the language of multihazards. Certain risk reduction strategies to mitigate one hazard can increase vulnerability to other hazards ([[Ward et al., 2022]]), or even act completely against another hazard. For instance, floods, which are the most prevalent natural hazard, occurred during the Covid-19 pandemic, which required people to work together in sandbagging to prevent property damage, thereby directly counteracting quarantine regulations, further increasing the virus' spread (Simonivic et al., 2021). Natural hazards occurring simultaneously with pandemics can therefore exacerbate the pandemic's impacts through mitigation measures against other natural hazards ([[Van Wyk de Vries and Rambabu, 2021]]). Thus, there exist risk reduction strategies that may be effective against isolated events but increase the impacts of simultaneously occurring hazards, exacerbating their societal impacts, thus demonstrating a challenge in the effective implementation of multihazard frameworks.

Second is adherence to risk reduction strategies. Even if a perfect scientific understanding of multihazards existed, coupled with a perfect risk mitigation strategy which would completely negate risk, a failure of the community to adhere to the strategies would render the strategy futile. Thus, community involvement is central to risk mitigation, during or predating a hazard event (Donovan and Oppenheimer, 2014; Lechner and Rouleau, 2019) During or before an event, evacuation is one of the most effective methods of mitigating risk - people's exposure is minimised by changing their physical location, thereby mitigating risk. However, individuals perceive their own level of risk, which is based on their own understanding and experiences of hazards, resulting in varied responses to evacuation orders ([[Lechner and Rouleau, 2019]]). Thus, there is a different understanding of risk between non-elite and elite actors (Nicholas et al., 2025), complicating the implementation and adherence to multihazard risk reduction strategies. On a longer temporal scale than evacuation, in perhaps the week or days in advance of a predicted hazard event, adherence to risk reduction strategies remains crucial. The adherence to earthquake building codes, or rather the lack thereof, is a clear example of the challenges of implementing risk reduction strategies.

The language surrounding multihazards in the multihazard literature is itself a challenge in the implementation of multihazard frameworks. No single definition for multihazards exists (Ward et al., 2022), which can hamper a) the understanding of what multihazards are and their contexts and b) their transferability into different contexts (Ward et al., 2022). The Sendai Framework emphasises multihazard approaches (UNDRR, 2015), yet it is each signatory's responsibility to implement its own frameworks for multihazard risk reduction strategies. Thus, there is an inconsistency in multihazard frameworks across borders. Multihazards transcend borders ([[Cassidy and Mani, 2022]]), and thus, a challenge is that different frameworks operate spatially and there is no singular *global* authority for multihazard risk mitigation. Certain global authorities do exist, for instance, the IPCC (Hulme, 2024) and planetary defence. Yet, for other major hazards with the potential to have global impacts, such as climate change or famine , for instance VEI7+ eruptions (Harrington, 1992; Newhall et al., 2018; Raible et a., 2022), there remains no global authority or mitigation strategy to handle them (Cassidy and Mani, 2022). Thus, the different languages used to describe, understand, and communicate multihazards across space present a large challenge to their implementation to combat risks with the potential to have global impacts.

In conclusion, multihazard events are the product of two or more hazards which interact in several possible ways (Gill and Malamund, 2016). Hazard curves and hazard matrices (Flemming et al., 2016; Gill and Malamund, 2016) can be used to standardise hazards in order to account for their differeing impacts and return periods (Zschau, 2017) and matrices can be used to understand the interactions between hazards (Gill and Malamund, 2016). A holistic understanding of the multihazard environment can then be communicated to policymakers in order to produce effective risk mitigation strategies. However, several challenges hamper their implementation. This essay has discussed the difficulty in understanding multihazards themselves and gaps in knowledge and datasets, the practical implementation of risk reduction strategies, whereby the simultaneous occurrence of otherwise unrelated hazards presents a huge difficulty, adherence to strategies, and the differing language surrounding multihazard mitigation, which complicates broader-scale risk reduction strategies. Future risk reduction strategies should seek to further scientific understanding, involve communities in hazard identification and mitigation stratgiies to build trust between authorities and people, and seek clarity over multihazard language in order to permit the broader application and collaboration of risk reduction strategies (Ward et al., 2022)
