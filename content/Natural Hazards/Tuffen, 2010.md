# How will melting of ice affect volcanic hazards in the twenty-first century?

## Hazards at ice- and snow-covered volcanoes

- Historical eruptions at more than 40 volcanoes worldwide have involved disruption of ice and show ([[Major and Newhall, 1989]]
### Perturbation of ice and snow 

- Ice perturbs volcanic activity through: ([[Major and Newhall, 1989]])
	- Mechaniscal erosion and melting by flowing pyroclastic debris or blasts of hot gases
	- Melting of ice or snow surface by lava flows
	- Basal melting by subglacial eruptions or geothermal activity
	- Ejection of water by eruptions through a crater lake
	- Deposition of tephra onto ice and snow
- The hazards associated with meltwater production are exacerbated when transient accumulation occurs with craters or calderas, as this can lead to even higher release rates of meltwater when catastrophic drainage is triggered by dam collapse or floating of an ice barrier that allows rapid subglacial drainage
- The total volume of meltwater floods may be restricted either by the amount of pyroclastic material or lava available to cause melting or by the volume of ice and snow that can be melted.

### Explosive eruptions

- Hazards posed by explosive eruptions at ice- and snow-covered volcanies are typical of those at other volcanoes - tephra fallout, pyroclastic flows, lava flows etc.
- Though they are modified by interactions with water.
- Interactions between magma and meltwater may trigger phreatomagmatic activity, even during basaltic eruptions, which would otherwise be effusive, not explosive.
	- **[[Ciano et al., 2023]]: March 2017 Mount Etna**
- When ice is thick, the explosive phase of the eruption can be attenuated or entirely take place beneath the ice surface, reducing hazards asociated with ashfall and pyroclastic debris.

### Edifice Instability and Collapse

- Collapse of edifices is favoured by:
	- Constraints by ice cause the development of structurally unstable, oversteepened edifices
	- Melting oc ice can create weak zones at the ice-bedrock interface
	- Hydrothermal alteration driven by snow and ice melt can weaken edifices

## Changing ice thickness on volcanoes

- Ice thinning on volcanoes is in mostly in response to climate warming, but eruptions and geothermal activity can also influence the mass balance of ice summits.
- Stratovolcanoes hosting thin glaciers, such as Kilimanjaro,may become completely ice-free in the coming century ([[Thompson et al 2009]])
- E.g., Popocatépetl, Mexico, lost all of its summit ice in 2004 due to a mixture of climate warming and eruptive activity ([[Julio-Miranda et al., 2008]])
- 1985 Nevado del Ruiz eruption was VEI 3 and removed 10% of the volcano's ice volume
- Large influence on mass balance due to low accumulation rates on tropical glaciers.
- In contrast, volcanoes beneath ice caps don't contribute as much to ice cap mass balance due to already high annual ablation and accumulation rates. However, continuous geothermal heat fluxes will have significant effects on ice dynamics and mass balance over longer time scales by enhancing basal melting and creep at the bed.

## Influence of ice recession on past volcanic activity

### Evidence

- Unloading of ice during deglaciation in Iceland causes decompression, which, according to models, leads to a greater degree and depth range of mantle melting. 
- This is reflected in a 30-50 fold increase in the rate of kagma eruption in the 1.5 ka after deglaciation in Iceland, reflected in volcanic deposits ([[Maclennan et al., 2002]])
- Short time delay between inferred ice unloading and enhanced volcanism shows that the increased generation of magma after deglaciation is rapidly transported from source to surface without a prolonged storage time in magma chambers

- Statistical analyses of eruption datasets show that changes can also influence volcanic activity elsewhere in ice thickness ([[Nowell et al., 2006]])
- Though, studies are limited in their usage of global climate proxies of d18O records as an indication of ice thickness changes rather than local ice thickness changes in individual volcanoes which are poorly constrained
- Whilst the frequency is known to increase, there is uncertainty on how the magnitude of eruptions is affected.
