# Human-induced landslides: toward the analysis of anthropogenic changes of the slope environment

## Human-induced drivers

- Classification of triggering factors (short term) and aggravating/degrading factors (long term) (Leroueil et al., 1996; Vaunat and Leroueil, 2002)
- Triggering factors are typically well covered in project assessments of slope stability, however degrading factors are typically not due to uncertainty in the evolution of slope stability over time.
- The evolution of slope stability can be described by two sets of variables (Jaboyedoff and Derron, 2005):
	1. Internal parameters
	2. External factors which modify (1)
- Both evolve over time and both can be the individual sources of events which result in slope failure.
- Need to define event - has a clear start and end.
- Triggering factors can be linked to a single event, whilst degrading factors  over time and cumulate the effect of several events -> temporal scales are thus important -> *link to [[Gill et al., 2021]]*
- Concept of Factor of Safety is useful for illustrating [[Terzaghi, 1950]]
	- Triggering events tend to change the factor of safety during a single event, leading to failure.
	- If repeated without failure, processes evolve into a degrading factor.
- Human induced landslides are not restricted to the poor - affect countries across all ranges of development indicators -> case studies

## Attempt to classify the changes made by human activities

- Authors propose 7 examples of human induced changes or actions which affect slope stability
1. Slope re-reprofiling
2. Groundwater flow perturbation
3. Surface water overland flow modifications
4. Land-use changes and land degradation
5. Inappropriate artificial structures
6. Vibration and explosives
7. Ageing and degradation of infrastructure
- Each is not mutually exclusive and many times of human induced changes can apply to multiple classes

### Slope re-profiling

- modify the factor of safety by modifying the slope
- common examples include roads, infrastructure, mines / quarries, buildings
- Most common human-induced landslides are from cut and fill for road construction which is particularly prevalent in Southeast Asia -> [[Robson et al., 2025]]
- Loading a slope can trigger a slide, and the most famous example is the Rissa landslide (Norway) 1978 which occurred in quick clays. It was triggered by a 700 m3 earth fill placed on the shore inducing a first slide 80 m wide, and during the next minutes, the slide reached 5-6 Mm3 by retrogression (Gregersen, 1981). One person among the five families living on the slide was killed.
- Re-profiling slopes along rivers can also influence landslides if no appropriate structural measures against erosion are considered (Jaboyedoff et al., 2019)
- Creation of new slopes is also a well-known triger -> e.g., Aberfan disaster in Wales (Siddle et al., 1996)
	- 1966, 144 deaths
	- After that event, the National Coal Board revised the legislation on colliery and mine tips (Mine and Quarries Act, 1969)
- submarine landslides can be triggered or degraded by shore filling such as the case during the construction of the Nice airport in 1979. A 15 Mm3 submarine landslide propagating along the Var river delta and created a tsunami (Dan et al., 2007), killing 11 people

### Groundwater flow pertubation and fast pore water pressure changes

- most important factor impacting slope stability is the pore water pressure [[Terzaghi, 1950]]
- Changes induced by human activities have several origins:
	1. water leakages from distribution and collecting networks or swimming pools
	2. variations in lake or river levels
	3. Decrease of permeability
	4. Increase of pore pressure by loading (Hutchingson, 1984)

### Surface flow modifications

- Caused by modifications due to land use changes e.g., concreting over of terrain
- Diversions to normal surface flow routes can place surface water on slopes previously unexperienced to surface flow during heavy rainfall events.
- Repeated surface water flow changes can degrade the factor of safety
- the landslide that occurred during heavy rainfall in April 2010 in favelas in Niteroi (Rio state, Brazil) and killed 166 people (Dourado et al., 2012), was certainly caused by unmanaged surface water. The location of favelas in steep slopes increased the risk since pre-existing landslides underneath the buildings could be reactivated (Anderson and Holcombe, 2013).

### Land-use changes and land degradation

- One of the most significant cases related to land use changes is Sarno (Italy): in 1998, a flow slide involving volcanic ash killed 137 people. The threat was wellknown, since the Bourbon king in 1830- 1860 had built channels and basins to control these recurrent mud-flows (Cascini and Ferlisi, 2003; Caporale, 2000; Brondi and Slavatori, 2003). Nevertheless, some houses were constructed directly on these protective works that were additionally no longer maintained. This cause was aggravated by road cutting deposits, disturbing slopes and indirectly concentrating water flows (Cascini et al., 2008).
- Terraced slopes can induce more landslides as compared to the neighbouring natural slopes. The flat areas created and heterogeneous material of the terraces may cause unstable situation due to high infiltration and particular groundwater paths (Crosta et al., 2003)
- Forest fires are mainly induced by humans - 90% of wildland fires in the US have anthropogenic origins
- Landslides and debris flows occur more frequently on burnt areas - thus showing how fires are an indirect cause of landslides and debris-flows (Cannnon and DeGraff, 2009)
- Thus, wildfires indicate a multi-hazard interaction which alter the disposition of a slope to landsliding -> [[Tilloy et al., 2019]]

### Inappropriate artificial structures

- Poorly designed reinforced structures due to wrong estimations of geotechnical parameters or geometry of slopes
- Can be sources of degradation or trigger the failure
- As an example in a very steep Swiss valley, a very heavy protective wall was built against rockfalls in 1988 in the village of Gondo (Switzerland). On October 14th 2000, during heavy period precipitation (BWG, 2002) a debris slide was triggered and accumulated behind this protective wall for rockfall. A few minutes later the wall broke and a debris flow destroyed part of the village, killing 14 people (Rouiller and Joris, 2001). The causes were numerous: (1) building heavy wall on scree slopes influences the groundwater circulations (it seems that after its building some springs disappeared below) (see section 4.2), (2) this structure was not designed to retain a debris-slide

### Discussion

- As already mentioned, many issues linked to HIL are linked to poor or rapidly growing countries where best practices are not respected for several reasons.
	- Though this isn't necessarily restricted to less developed countries
- Considering the above arguments, it is clear that three main issues are linked with time issues, are not well assessed in practice, when dealing with potential HIL:
	1. The evolution of geotechnical properties of slopes (degradation) - time dependent slope stability evolution
	2. Ageing of infrastructures
	3. Changes related to slope modifications
