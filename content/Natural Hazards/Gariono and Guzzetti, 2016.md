# Landslides in a changing climate

- Different phenomena influence the stability of slopes and cause landslides, including e.g., precipitation, snow melting, temperature changes, earthquake shaking, volcanic activity, and various human actions. Climate and its variations control or influence some of these phenomena, and chiefly precipitation and temperature [[Sidle and Ochiai, 2006]]
- It is therefore expected that climate (and its changes) influences slope stability at different temporal and geographical scales [[Seneviratne et al., 2012]] [^1] 
- [[Crozier, 2010]][^2] was the first to review, list and examine systematically the mechanisms by which climate change can affect landslides and the stability conditions of slopes, thereby assessing their impact of the predicted climate changes on landslides and engineered slopes.
	- They concluded that the incompleteness of landslide inventories and climate records limits the possibility of evaluating the impact of expected environmental and climatic changes on frequency
	- Impact of climate change on landslides is likely minor, compared to impacts from human disturbance -> anthropocene ≠ climate change
- Modelling approaches:
	- Investigate the stability conditions of slopes driven by changes in long-term rainfall/pore pressure variations obtained from future rainfall projections [[Fowler et al., 2007]][^3]
	- Many models are single landslide studies
- Empirical approaches:
	- Determine geographical and temporal variations in the occurrence, frequency, or rate of (re-)activation of landslides.
	- Use historical landslide occurrences with climatic records, mainly rainfall and temperature

### 2.1. Evaluation of slope stability conditions using downscaled climate projections

- [[Ciabatta et al., 2016]] [^4] use global climate model projections alongside landslide early warning systems to assess the performance of the system using a catalogue of landslides in Umbria, central Italy.
- They found an increase of > 40% in landslide occurrence in Umbria, mainly in winter due to increased rainfall
- However, summer months saw a strong decrease in soil moisture and an increase in rainfall did not produce a change in landslide occurence.
- Different studies have found different results when looking at different locations, highlighting the complexities of predicting future landslides.

### 3. Influence of climate on slope stability and landslide hazard

- The influence of climate and its variations on landslides can be classified broadly as: (i) local or regional (or global), (ii) of short- or long-term impact, and (iii) direct or indirect.
- Finds that climate and landslides operate on different and only partially overlapping scales
- Local impacts influence a single slope, a portion of a slope, an individual landslide, or a small catchment
- Regional influences affect landslide occurrence in areas ranging from a few hundreds to several thousands of square kilometers i.e., a province, a state or country, or a broad geographical or physiographical region
- The regional impact of climate change was also ascertained for engineered slopes. [[Loveridge and Spink, 2010]][^5], working in England, highlighted that clay slopes will be at greater risk in the future from increased-magnitude seasonal cycles of moisture changes, leading potentially to loss of roads and railways serviceability e.g., due to track settlement, and greater rates of strain softening
- Different temporal periods:
	- Short term climatic effects range from a few years to several centuries 
		- Influences the amount of rainfall inducing landslides [[Stoffel et al., 2014]][^6]
	- Longer term:
		  Rainfall altering landcover types and land use, affecting slope stability conditions, and the type, abundnace, and frequency of landslides [[Sidle and Ochiai, 2006]]
- Climate change also affects the human impact on natural landscapes which in turn has direct and indirect consequences on slope stability

### Changes in the stability of individual slopes

- Using factor of safety [[Terzaghi, 1950]]
-  [[Crozier, 2010]] considered six climate-related factors and discussed their effects and interactions in controlling the local stability conditions, including: (i) precipitation totals, (ii) rainfall intensity, (iii) air temperature, (iv) wind speed and duration, (v) changes in the weather systems and the related (vi) meteorological variability
	- See [[Crozier, 2010]]
- Rainfall intensity increases -> more infiltration -> more subsurface drainage and through flow -> build-up of perched water tables, reducing effective normals stresses and shear strength -> less slope stability
- Temperature increases -> more evapotranspiration on vegetated slopes -> positive effects on slope stability
	- Thus, so long as evapotranspiration increase outweighs rainfall increase -> more slope stability [[Comegna et al., 2013]][^7]
- Temperature increase -> snow melt -> increased pore pressure -> reduced slope stability
- Temperature increase (mountain regions) -> permafrost thawing -> increase in rock slope failures [[Huggel et al., 2013]] [[Stoffel et al., 2014]] *low key just use [[Deline, 2009]]*
- Freeze thaw cycles -> can either increase or decrease fracturing depending on where the cycle oscillates between [[Sidle and Ochiai, 2006]]
	- If previous cycle never went above freezing but now oscillates around freezing -> more weakening
	- If cycle originally oscillated around freezing but now is entirely above freezing -> less weakening
- Change in wind speed -> change in evapotranspiration -> change in soil moisture -> change in slope stability [[Crozier, 2010]]
