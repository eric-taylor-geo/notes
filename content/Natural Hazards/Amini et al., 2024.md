# Methodology to incorporate seismic damage and debris to evaluate strategies to reduce life safety risk for multi-hazard earthquake and tsunami

- Work incorporates two aspects of tsunami evacuation:
	- Effect of earthquake-induced damage to buildings on egress time
	- The effect of earthquake-induced debris on horizontal evacuation time
- Uses Seaside, Oregon as a testbed community.
- Uses fragility analysis to estimate seismic damage to buildings and resulting debris that covers portions of the road network. 
- Horizontal evacuation time is determined based on the shortest path to shelters, taking into account increased travel time due to debris
- Effects of different mitigation strategies are quantified.
- Results indicate the fatality and life safety risk of a near-field tsunami increases by 4.2–8.3 times when the effects of building egress and earthquake-induced debris are considered


# Background

- Near-field tsunamis can reach the onshore within 20-40 minutes after the initial earthquake - higher risk and threat to life in coastal communities (Henry et al., 2017; Katada et al., 2006)
- Rapid evacuation is therefore likely to be on foot
- Previous lack of integration of the damage to infrastructure and buildings on evacuation modelling (Castro et al., 2019)

# Literature review

- [[Mackinoshima et al., 2020]]: Evacuee's behaviour during tsunami evacuation can be categorised into four stages: receiving notification, risk recognition, response activity, and evacuation movement.
- [[Friere et al., 2013]]: tsunami risk assessment based on cost-weighted distance model to evaluate the effect of daytime and nighttime populations on tsunami evacuation modeling in the Lisbon Metropolitan Area
- Population risk significantly changes between nighttime and daytime populations
- [[Frucht et al., 2021]] developed a tsunami risk assessment on Haifi, Isreal - concluded that increasing community preparedness level and the seismic retrofitting of existing structures are two main effective risk mitigation strategies
- [[[Bernardini and Ferreria, 2022]] -> targeting seismic retrofitting to vulnerable, historical buildings to reduce building damage and ease the evacuation process

# Methodology

- Five components
1. Identify hazards and severity that threaten the community
2. Select infrastructure systems that are important to the community and have considerable impacts on selected resilience metrics
3. Identify the population that is at risk due to earthquake and tsunami hazards
4. Identify the spatial and temporal distribution / patterns in the population
5. Probabilistic simulation of various casualty models - estimation of ground shaking-induced damage to buildings. Uses HAZUS methodology
