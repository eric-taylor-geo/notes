# Community resilience, latent resources and resource scarcity after an earthquake: Is society really three meals away from anarchy?

- The coordination and mobilisation of official aid after a disaster takes time - people are typically recommended to have enough supplies to last for at least 3 days
- Thus, the majority of research measuring preparedness for a disaster is based on having these recommended measures in place
- Mobilisation of official aid may take longer than 3 days - economic and geographical barriers may interfere in mobilisation efforts.

- Research simulates earthquake event to examine the reported stock of resources available at the household level
- Coastal and hilly geography of Wellington, NZ provides limited access in a post-earthquake scenario making a resource-scarce scenario plausible -> links with Russia

## Discussion

- half the population lack the resources to survive for 3 days
- Upon resource shortfall - a key concern for emergency management is avoiding panic or disorder.
	- Panic buying can become contagious [[Shou et al., 2013]]
	- Key solution is promoting community leadership around collective resilience and improving early communication around resource needs
- Socially unacceptable acts (e.g., looting) were atypical
- After 7 days participants indicated that they would take necessary resources without paying but remained unlikely to engage with stereotypical looting behaviour - behaviour which is often shown in the media
- People act morally, gathering resources they need to survive but will not engage in unnecessary unlawful acts.
	- This result fits with a post-conventional schema for decision-making (e.g. Rest et al. [1999](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11069-014-1187-3#ref-CR23 "Rest J, Narvaez D, Bebeau M, Thoma S (1999) A neo-Kohlbergian approach: the DIT and schema theory. Educ Psychol Rev 11(4):291–324")), where acts committed may be illegal or atypical in day-to-day life, but are seen as acceptable based on the extreme context of the situation.
- In a post-disaster resource-scarce situation, participants determined that taking necessary resources was no longer stealing; rather this was survival, and therefore right. Arguably, there was a belief that this choice would be reciprocated by others, so that the overall social norm structure was consistent across the community (i.e. a norm shift).

- Perhaps counter-intuitively, the people that prepare the most for a disaster are the most likely to seek early assistance.
- Study finds no relationship between the actual number of days of supplies people had available and the number of days they would wait for aid.
	- People's motivation was anxiety over limited official aid
- If the key driver for seeking aid is anxiety over the limited supply of official aid, then this issue is going to be exacerbated the larger the scale of the disaster.
