# A review of quantification methodologies for multi-hazard interrelationships

- Reviews 146 articles
- [[Gill and Malamud, 2016]] four step for a multi-hazard framework:
1. Interrelations are not really considered and hazards are simply superimposed on a region
2. -4 include hazard interactions, coincident hazards and hazard vulnerabilities.

- Authors identify three primary challenges associated with quantifying multi-hazard interelationships:
1. Fragmentation of literature in the field 
2. Gaps in the multi-hazard approaches taken by different institutions
3. Complexity of multi-hazard events
	- How to address a deterministic equation based understanding which might apply to 'all' events that are similar vs a case study based empirical understanding which might be applicable to a given scenario of a specific event

- Authors create a mult-hazard interrelationship literature database to:
1. To encompass the broadest possible number of terms and approaches for multi-hazard assessments.
2. To understand different possible interrelations between natural hazards
3. To focus on quantitative methods for hazard interrelations.

## Interrelationships between hazards

- Authors describe five interrelation types between hazards based on literature review

**Independence**

- Coincidence between hazards can occur. It implies a spatial and temporal overlapping of the impact of two hazards without any dependence or triggering relationship.
- Also include in this category cases where two hazards impact the same area, independently, at different times (e.g., cyclone occurring few weeks after an earthquake).
- E.g., 2010 Pacaya volcanic eruption and tropical storm Agatha which hit the Pacific coastline of Guatemala almost simultaneously, leading to exacerbated damages due to ash blocking drainage system of rainfall triggering lahars [[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]

**Triggering (cascading)**

- Implies a primary and a secondary hazard. A natural hazard might trigger zero, one, or more secondary natural hazards [[Gill and Malamud, 2016]]
- Second natural hazard may be identical or different from the primary hazard.
- E.g., Earthquake triggering landslides which can trigger a flood [[Catane et al.. 2012]]

**Change Conditions**

- One hazard alters the disposition of a second hazard by changing environmental conditions.
- E.g., a wildfire may denude an area of vegetation, hardening the soil and thus amplifying the strength of floods through increasing over ground flow and reducing infiltration [[Cannon et al., 2008]]

**Compound Hazard**

- Different hazards are the result of the same 'primary event' which is not necessarily a hazard.
- In this case there is thus not a primary and a secondary hazard, rather the different hazards occur simultaneously
- E.g., the co-occurance of river flooding and sea surge could be as a result of the same large-scale process (e.g., tropical cyclone)
- Hazards are dependent and form a multi-hazard event.

**Mutual Exclusion**

- Two natural hazards which exhibit negative dependence or are mutually exclusive.
- For example, heavy rainfall and forest fire.
- Negative dependence is usually on a particular spatial and/or temporal scale
