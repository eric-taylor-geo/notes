# Hazard interactions and interaction networks (cascades) within multi-hazard methodologies



From a probabilistic viewpoint, generalising across multiple individual events for each triggering relationship, we can also infer that some triggering relationships are more likely to occur than others. For example, Gill and Malamud (2014) use a nine-point scale to classify the spatial overlap and temporal likelihood of each of the 78 primary-secondary natural hazard triggering relationships that they identified. An example of a triggering relationship with low spatial overlap and low temporal likelihood is a landslide triggering a volcanic eruption. An example of a triggering relationship with high spatial overlap and high temporal likelihood is a storm triggering a landslide

Case studies:

- **Independent Hazards**: Tropical Storm Agatha in Guatemala occurred 2 days after the eruption of Pacaya volcano in May 2010. Ashfall blocked an already inadequate drainage system in Guatemala City, increasing the intensity of flooding during the storm (UN, 2010).
- **Cascading**: The Guatemala Magnitude 7.5 earthquake in 1976 triggered multiple aftershocks and more than 10,000 landslides, rock falls, and debris flows (Harp et al., 1981). Many mass movements occurred along poorly built roads and railways, blocking vital transport routes (Plafker et al., 1976). Mass movements further blocked rives and triggered upstream flooding, and the breaching of landslide dams resulted in further flooding ([[Harp et al., 1981]]).
- **Change making:** A Magnitude 7.8 earthquake in Nepal, April 2015, triggered thousands of avalanches and landslides. Also increased the probability of further landslides by weakening the regolith. Landslides were activated in subsequent monsoon rains ([[Bilham, 2015]]).

The authors propose the use of two visualisation frameworks to represent networks of hazard interactions:
1. Hazard interaction matrices
2. Hazard process/flow diagrams

Interaction refers to the unidirection and bidirectional effect(s) between one hazard/process and another hazard/process.
1. Triggering relationships
2. Increased-probability relationships
3. Catalysis/Impedance
