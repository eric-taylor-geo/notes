# Properties of natural supraglacial debris in relation to modelling sub-debris ice ablation

## Abstract

- Increasing need to successfully model the surface energy and mass balance of debris-covered glaciers
- Paper measures vertical temperature profiles within debris on Ngozumpa glacier, Nepal.
- Demonstrates:
1. Conductive processes dominant during the ablation season in matrix-supported diamict
2. Ventilation may be possible in coarse surface layers
3. Phase changes associated with seasonal change have a marked effect on the effective thermal diffusivity
	- Thermal conductivity is 30% higher in summer than in winter - values dependent upon the volume and phase of water in the debris
- Surface albedo and debris thickness vary widely over small scales
- Probability distribution is positively skewed in the upper glacier and is more normal nearer the terminus 

## Introduction

- Modelling required in sufficient detail to improvement hydrological resource management
- Variations in surface temperature, primarily driven by diurnal and seasonal cycles, propagate through the debris profile with the same period, but decreasing amplitude and increasing lag, dependent on depth and the efficiency with which the debris can transfer heat [[Farouki, 1981]] [[Williams and Smith, 1989]]
- The dampening depth of surface temperature is proportional to the period of oscillation, so longer period oscillations will penetrate deeper into the profile.
- Energy absorbed when heating debris during the daytime is returned to the atmosphere at night rather than being transferred to the ice below.
- Hence, beyond a critical thickness, which is dependent on the optical and thermal properties of the debris as well as the meteorological conditions, melt is less than that of bare ice surfaces ([[Østrem, 1959]]; Loomis, 1970; Fujii, 1977; Nakawo and Young, 1982; Mattson et al., 1993; Nicholson and Benn, 2006)
- debris-covered glaciers have a different extent to clean-ice glaciers under the same climate, and will respond differently to the same climatic forcing (Jansson and Fredin, 2002).

## Modelling

- Owing to difficulties in obtaining and extrapolating ablation measurements over debris-covered glaciers, mathmatical modelling of melt rate from meteorological variables is preferable
	- [[Nakawo and Young, 1982]] [[Nicholson and Benn, 2006]] [[Reid and Brock, 2010]]
- Within debris, energy transfer can occur through conduction, radiation, convection, advection (via a fluid) or phase changes [[Farouki, 1981]]
- For simplicity, most sub-debris ablation models treat all energy transfer as if it were a conductive energy flux governed by the conductivity of the debris layer and the temperature gradient within the debris.
- Under the assumption that the temperature gradient is predominantly in the vertical plane, the 1D energy flux in the vertical direction is:
$$
Q_c = -k_e \frac{∂T_d}{∂z}
$$
- When the ice temperature below the debris is below freezing, any positive conductive heat flux through the debris to the ice interface will be conducted into the ice body, warming it to the freezing point.
- Upon reaching the freezing point, the melt rate can be calculated using the energy flux at the debris-ice interface, the latent heat of fusion, and the density of the ice:
$$
M = \frac{Q_c}{L_fρ_i}
$$

## Discussion

- Thermal diffusivity varies through time and space [[Hinkel, 1997]]
- Albedo varies and is controlled by lithology 
	- Link to [[Benn et al., 2012]]
- However, even areas with uniform lithology can display varying albedo dependent upon the maturity of debris-cover
- Debris thickness increases down-glacier within the debris-covered zone however this is a poor predictor at a small spatial resolution due to debris thickness varying widely over small scales
- For distributed glacier modelling, the debris thickness distribution is better represented as a probability density function
- In the absence of knowledge of the stratigraphy of the debris layer, the bulk thermal and compositional properties are applied uniformly over the whole depth of the debris.
- "Application of simplified energy balance models using diurnal linear representations of vertical temperature gradients through the debris is appropriate only in unstratified debris layers during periods with stable meteorological conditions within established winter or summer seasons. However, averaged over longer time intervals, errors in computed ablation rate may tend to cancel out."
