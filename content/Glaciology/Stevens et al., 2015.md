# Greenland supraglacial lake drainages triggered by hydrologically induced basal slip

- 16 GPSs
- Water in North Lake initially drains into moulin slowly, perturbing the bed, causing uplift which then initiates fracture allowing the lake to drain
- New way of considering methods of lake drainage – feeding of moulin is sufficient to perturb basal friction, causing uplift and the combination of these two allows for the fracturing of ice to allow hydrofracture for rapid drainage
- For 3 consecutive years, GPSs show distinctive behaviour. Analysis of uplift, and horizontal motion (both along flowline and at right angles to the orientation of a fracture which forms through the lake) used to identify:
1. Start of precursor – event before main fracture (draining into moulin)
2. Horizontal frature initiation
3. Maximum fracture opening
- Three years of data - recurring process
![[Pasted image 20241108143659.png]]
- Network Inversion Filter (NIF) algorithm used to model all GPS time series as sum of three deformation sources: (1) hydro-fracture opening, (2) basal cavity opening (due to rapid injection of meltwater), and (3) extra basal slip above background rate (due to enhanced basal lubrication)
- Invert data to produce a field of basal slip and cavity opening/closure at the bed. Allows for best combination of model variables which best match the actual surface displacement data gathered using GPS.
- 2011 Event:
	- Precursor - basal slip mostly southwest of moulin, cavity opening to southeast. Indicates injection of meltwater to bed before local hydrofracture initiation
	- After precursor - hydro-fracture opens first at moulin, then propagates east.
	- Time of maximum hydro-fracture opening - basal cavity volume close to North Lake pre-drainage volume (0.007 ± 0.001 km3)
- Shows that initial drainage via a moulin is sufficient to start hydrofracturing processes
