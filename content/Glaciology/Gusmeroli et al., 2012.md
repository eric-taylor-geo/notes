# Twenty years of cold surface layer thinning at Storglaciären, sub-Arctic Sweden, 1989-2009

Storglaciären in Sweden is an example of a well-studied studied polythermal glacier (Gusmeroli _et al._, 2012). Storglaciären is a predominantly temperate glacier, apart from a cold, permanently frozen surface layer overlying the temperate core in the ablation area (Pettersson _et al._, 2007).

Much of ice at Storglaciären is kept temperate by the refreezing of spring melt as it percolates into firn in the accumulation zone, releasing latent heat. In the ablation zone however, spring and summer melt run off the glacier quickly, rather than refreezing, allowing the layer of cold ice to persist (Hooke, Gould and Brzozowski, 1983).

The cold surface layer has been thinning at a constant loss rate between 1989-2009. The climate is the dominant control on cold-layer thickness (Pettersson _et al._, 2007). The presence of the permanently cold ice is caused by particularly cold winter temperature and its thickness is determined by the temperature gradient throughout the cold layer.

Study demonstrates that repeated ice-penetrating radar surveys can provide an additional parameter to extract a climatological signal in glaciated areas. Gusmeroli _et al_. (2012) provide the first time series of thermal changes in a polythermal glacier, using surveys conducted in 1989, 2001, and 2009.
