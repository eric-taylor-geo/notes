# Evaluating the Retreat, Arrest, and Regrowth of Crane Glacier Against Marine Ice Cliff Process Models

Cited in [[Bassis et al., 2024]]:

the portion of the calving cliff of Crane Glacier in the Antarctic Peninsula that extends above the waterline exceeded 100 m following the collapse of the Larsen B Ice Shelf; it retreated rapidly, thinned, and then readvanced, suggesting that, at least in this case, cliff failure did not lead to catastrophically unstable retreat

MICI is built on the premise that cliff-calving rates scale with ice thickness—if climate forcing drives ice margin retreat into the thicker ice found in the interior basins of West Antarctica, calving rates will increase (a positive feedback inherent to the geometry of the system), reinforcing retreat. In theory, this process could be arrested by exogenous changes in the terminal stress state that inhibit cliff failure (like the development of mélange or sea ice, or retreat into a narrowing fjord) or by compensating negative feedbacks in the system, such as acceleration and thinning, which could re-establish marginal ice shelves with thinner terminal ice cliffs

Ice sheet models struggle to reproduce the rates of historic SLR without the inclusion of MICI ([[DeConto and Pollard, 2016]]).

Satellite remote sensing cannot provide direct evidence of ice cliff failure, however it can be used to record retreat and determine if behaviour is consistent with the critical requirements of the MICI hypothesis:
- Terminus failed by cliff calving
- Unforced acceleration of the calving rate associated with a taller terminal ice cliff (not compensated for by simultaneous terminal acceleration and thinning)

## Instability or forced retreat?

- the acceleration of calving and retreat was (a) driven by further forcing, or (b) was the result of positive feedbacks in the system, potentially associated with cliff-calving from an increasingly tall terminal ice cliff.
- Lack of direct ocean observations inhibits the discrimination of these two scenarios
- Surface temperature record and behaviour elsewhere provides some context.
- Temperatures have been cooling since 1990 over the AP ([[Turner et al., 2016]]), seeing a reduction in surface melt production.
- The intensification of cyclonic wind speeds in the Weddell Sea region following cooling promoted widespread glacier terminus advance around the peninsula.
- However, Crane Glacier retreated despite this greater regional trend, retreating by 10.3 km in the 2 years following the Larsen B collapse.
- The mass dis-equilibrium induced by terminus changes drove widespread thinning of the glacier's interior, reducing driving stress and slowing flow speeds.
- The retreat stabilised, and the terminus remained stable for 6 years with no further retreat, despite having a taller terminal cliff in 2004 than in 2002.
- Suggestions for this: 
	- Narrowing of the fjord, increasing lateral drag
	- Sea-ice or mélange providing backstress
	- Retreat into thinner ice along a prograde slope
	- Retreat into ice which is not preconditioned for failure by crevassing or other processes which induce damage
- Indeed, Crane's retreat stops at what appears to be the narrowest part of the fjord, lending evidence that the sidewalls could have provided additional buttressing stress, reducing the calving rate sufficiently to balance with the ice-flow rate.
- Regrowth during 2010-2021 promoted by the formation of land-fast sea ice which provided buttressing to the glacier, reducing the calving rate. Formed an ice shelf
- Widespread melt ponds appeared on the sea ice in 2021, causing a break up of the ice shelf, and the terminus retreated back to its previous stable position.

- Following LBIS collapse, calving rates accelerated when the rest of the Peninsula was experiencing glacier terminus advance, consistent with a positive feedback in the Crane Glacier system and a potential geometric instability.
- The maximum terminal cliff height was 111 m - under current models, terminal stresses at this height are below the failure threshold of ice.
- Thus, this implies that the ice was damaged, highlighting the importance of assessing ice damage in projecting future ice sheet behaviour.

- Assessing ice shelf break-up and ice cliff failure is difficult due to their rapid nature, which requires high temporal sampling, and thus, ideally, in situ observations.
- Thus, this study provides evidence for MICI, but cannot attribute the initial unstable retreat of Crane Glacier to MICI - though it contributes to evidence.
