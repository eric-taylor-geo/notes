- Sea ice (may) provide some level of buttressing

- Whilst ice shelves surround 75% of Antarctica’s coastline -> the question is one of what proportion of them are actively buttressing the ice sheet
	- Not spatially homogenous across ice shelves
	- E.g., Ross Ice Shelf edge is passively buttressing -> therefore a major calving event here would have little impact.

## Atmosphere/Weather

- Weather system is influenced by the topographic effect of the continent - weather systems progress clockwise around the continent - less longitudinal penetration toward the pole than the arctic
- Extreme heatwaves occur through the formation of atmospheric rivers which deliver warm, moist air from the subtropics
- Work required to determine whether atmospheric rivers could deliver temperature extremes over vulnerable areas (e.g., ice shelves) 
- Cyclones have resulted in major calving events (e.g., Brunt Ice Shelf in 2020 [[Francis et al., 2022]]) and rapid sea ice decline in the Weddell Sea in 2016/17 [[Turner et al., 2020]]
- Still a significant proportion of events which are not attributed to either rivers or cyclones -> e.g., persistent high pressure systems and mid-latitude weather fronts
- Loss of ozone layer resulted in stratospheric cooling (Ozone is a GHG which warms the upper atmosphere) which altered thermal gradients, cuasing a poleward shift of the belt of westerly winds that encircle Antarctica [[Thompson and Solomon, 2002]]
- Change in circulation patterns resulted in warming over the Antarctic Peninsula and cooling in parts of EA [[Thompson et al., 2011]]

## Sea Ice

- Sea ice limits the transfer of heat and gases between the ocean and atmosphere
- Sea ice growth and melting affects salinity which therefore affects stratification and the thermocline -> affects global ocean circulation.
- Sea ice is recognised by the WMO as a Global Climate Indicator, which is key to describing and presenting the changing climate (WMO, 2023)
- Sea ice extent varies typically between a minimum of 2-4 million km2 in late February and a high of 18-20 million km2 in September (NSICS, 2023)
- Since 1978, there has been a gradual (but uneven) increase in Antarctic sea ice extent despite a warming climate.
- Record high in winter 2014 but also a record low in summer 2017 [[Turner et al., 2017]] [[Parkinson and DiGirolamo, 2021]]
- Recently, many more record lows NSIDC, 2023
- Antarctic loss varies spatially - record lows in Ross and Amundsun Seas, whilst Weddell Sea and Indian sector remain more consistent (but still lower than long term mean)
- Truly capturing sea ice changes requires understanding its volume which requires thickness as well as extent - this is more difficult to estimate from satellite data.
	- Scientific Committee on Antarctic Research" "Sea ice thickness remains arguably the largest single gap in our knowledge of the climate system"
- Coupled climate models have universally simulated a decreased in A sea ice cover since 1979 however this mismatches with pre-2017 satellite obsercations [[Roach et al., 2020]]
- Therefore, low confidence in the ability of climate models to simulate sea ice change under various future forcing scenarios

## Ocean

- Extreme oceanic temperature events are typically characterised as "Marine Heatwaves" (MHWs) - extended periods of anomalously high temperatures
- Predicted to increase in frequency and intensity in the next few decades [[Burger et al., 2022]]
- Data scarcity limits future trends
- Circumpolar Deep Water is warmer than the water beneath ice shelves and wataer proximal to marine-terminating glaciers
- Intrusion of CDW has been identified as a cause for the rapid retreat of ice shelves and glaciers in WA - e.g., Pine Island, Thwaites [[Rignot et al., 2014]] -> essay citations

## Ice Shelves

- Ice shelves fringe 75% of Antarctica's coastline - provide buttressing support which stabilises the rate of ice flow from the grounded ice sheet and its overall contribution to global sea level [[Siegert et al., 2020]]
- Larsen-A, B, and Wilkins ice shelves collapsed catastrophically in 1995, 2002, and 2008, respectively.
- Larsen B collapse was triggered by widespread meltwater ponding and subsequent hydrofracturing of the ice [[Scambos and Hulbe, 2000]] (*why is this a citaiton for this -> it was published before the collapse*)
- West Antarctic Ice Sheet ice shelf imbalances caused by intrusion of warm modified CDW
- Interannual and long-term variability of ocean temperatures is linked to atmospheric teleconnections and forcings associated with ENSO [[Jenkins et al., 2018]]
- Complex link between atmospheric and ocean processes plays a role in ice shelf stability and calving [[Francis et al., 2021]]
- A change in calving frequency is indicative of longer-term change 

## Land ice

- New satellite remote sensing has revealed the presence of meltwater far inland of the grounding line [[Stokes et al., 2019]]
- As well as supraglacial ponds, meltwater has been observed to pond within firn aquifers also upstream of the grounding line [[Bell et al., 2018]]
- Supraglacial meltwater has the potential to impact ice flow through hydrological connections with the bed - such as in greenland where connections to the bed are well established, resulting in seasonal changes in ice velocities in response to meltwater input [[Joughin et al., 2008]]
- Seasonal accelerations in ice flow have been observed [[Boxall et al., 2022]], however it is unclear whether this is as a result of hydrological connections or other forcing events e.g., oceanic
- Firn aquifers can also dampen the signal and delay the response [[Wallis et al., 2023]]
	- *Their release of water may also result in a more complex response than simply seasonal*
- Refreezing releases latent heat, changing ice rheology [[Bell et al., 2018]]
- The removal of ice shelves impacts ice flow - ice shelves provide backstress, buttressing grounding glaciers. Glaciers feeding ice shelves accelerate upon ice shelf disintegration [[Scambos et al., 2004]]
- Changes in basal conditions alter ice flow - e.g., Kamb Ice stream was fast flowing until ~1865 AD upon which it became stagnant, resulting in thickening [[Pritchard et al., 2009]] -> link with 1B essay reading on changing basal conditions
- Potential suggestion that water supplied to the bed was rerouted to the neighbouring Whillans ice stream - considered to be natural variability, beyond anthropogenic influence
- However, because ice sheet upstream of the grounding line is still influenced by dynamics at the grounding line, which subsequently has the ability to affect upstream dynamics, glaciological change in the ice sheet interior may too be affected by future (and present and past) anthropogenic activity
- Only 0.2-0.4% of Antarctica is exposed above ice [[Burton-Johnston et al., 2016]] - this will likely increase with warming and may result in debris sources, will which reduce surface reflectivity and enhance melt

## Cascading extreme events

- Often, extreme events do not occur in isolation and their impacts can be linked in cascades.
- For example, the atmospheric river in March 2022 that led to record temperatures in central East Antarctica also led to surface warming of land ice and ice shelves on the coast, break-up of sea-ice, and collapse of the Conger Ice Shelf ([[Lhermitte et al., 2023]])
