#  Seasonal changes in the morphology of the subglacial drainage system, Haut Glacier d'Arolla, Switzerland

-	Headward channel growth reached 65 md-1 
-	Chanel growth occurred down-glacier through moulins and up-glacial channel formation was from the retreating snowline exposing new moulins and crevasses to inputs of meltwater.
Introduction:
-	Temporal changes to the morphology of subglacial drainage systems has been linked to glacier flow instabilities (Kamb et al., 1985; [[Kamb, 1987]])
-	Most bulk runoff on temperate glaciers derives from meltwater at the glacier surface. Therefore, changes within englacial/subglacial drainage systems are likely to be linked to surface processes which control the flux of meltwater to the glacier base.
-	415 dye-tracer experiments conducted at Haut Glacier d’Arolla during melt season in 1990 and 1991.
Processes and mechanisms of drainage system evolution
-	Correlation between the location of the channel head and the location of the snowline at the glacier surface.
-	Suggests that the retreat of the summer snowline determines the rate at which the channelised system expands up the glacier.
-	Suggests that the greater diurnal amplitude of meltwater discharge is associated with runoff from impermeable, low albedo ice surfaces (revealed by snowmelt)
-	Consistent with Kamb’s (1987) suggestion that cavity-like systems may collapse into channelised systems if water pressures increase significantly beyond a steady-state value. 
-	Kamb (1987): cavities on the lee of obstacles, linked by narrow orifices.
-	[[Walder and Fowler, 1994]]: suggest that increasing discharge promotes the growth of conduits into either R- or N- channels depending upon the underlying sediment and glacier surface slope. R-channels are favoured due to the high slopes characteristic of mountain glaciers.
-	Upon the exposure of low albedo ice – strongly peaked diurnal discharge cycles would drain into moulins.
-	Snow cover would smooth and delay the diurnal signal.
-	Channels with be able to discharge water at a lower pressure than any pre-existing or surrounding distributed systems. This creates a pressure gradient which draws water to the channel, resulting in an increase in local flow velocity within the distributed system -> enhances ice melting in around the channel head, facilitating headward growth.
-	Disconnected stores of water may disrupt or create a lag in the delivery of surface runoff.
-	In the case of Haut Glacier d’Arolla, a party firn aquifer on the glacier surface may have slowed the rate of channel extension in the upper glacier.
-	Larger aquifers may be sufficient to half channel extension however, at Haut Glacier d’Arolla, the aquifer is patched and poorly developed following successive negative mass balance years. Thus, in this location, the aquifer can slow the process but not completely halt the growth of channels.
-	As channel growth is linked to supraglacial meltwater input, their growth will be affected by the distribution of crevasses and moulins.
Conclusions:
-	Calculations between the two years suggest that most channelised systems which formed in the 1990 melt season did not survive to the 1991 melt season.
-	At the onset of spring melt, the inefficient distributed system will have been able to cope with slowly varying inputs of meltwater via the enlargement of cavities by a combination of melt, substrate erosion, and enlargement by enhanced basal sliding – able to maintain a steady-state.
-	However, following the retreat of the snowline and the exposure of low albedo ice, diurnal magnitudes and amplitudes of meltwater increased, generating high water pressure perturbations within the systems which are significantly large enough to cause the distributed network to evolve into major channels.
-	Each individual moulin directs channel growth down-glacier. However, the net system grows up-glacier due to continuously exposing new moulins and crevasses up-glacier.
-	The rate of channel growth was inhibited by the presence of a discontinuous firn aquifer.
-	Highlights four factors which control the evolution of subglacial drainage systems over melt seasons:
-	Weather conditions
-	Pattern of snowline retreat – depends upon the initial snow cover distribution which is dependent upon a myriad of factors – topography, wind, avalanching etc.
-	Snow may not retreat regularly -> storm events may cause channel formation far above the retreating snowline.
-	Spatial distribution of moulins and crevasses.
-	Too many entry points may deliver many low discharge inputs into the system rather than the high pressure perturbations required to destabilise a distributed system.
-	Too little entry points may result in surface melt not even reaching the glacial bed.
-	The degree of similarity between the patterns of water flow on the glacier surface and glacier bed.
-	Subglacial flow is often in the direction of the slope of the glacier surface (because of overburdening pressure or..?) except for when the slope of the bedrock surface is 10x the ice surface, or in the opposite direction ([[Shreve, 1972]]) -> tf?
-	Haut Glacier d’Arolla: has similar patterns of water flow (Sharp et al., 1993): therefore supraglacial water inputs into the glacial ed are likely to add to any water flowing from further ap the glacier -> adding to discharges.
-	In other cases where there is no correspondence, meltwater may not add to the preferred axes of subglacial drainage, resulting in insufficient amplitude and magnitude of pressure perturbations required to destabilise the distributed system.
