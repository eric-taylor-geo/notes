# Increased channelization of subglacial drainage during deglaciation of the Laurentide Ice Sheet

-	This paper uses an unprecedented data set of over 20,000 eskers to reconstruct the evolution of channelized meltwater systems during the final deglaciation of the Laurentide Ice Sheet (13–7 kyr B.P.)
-	demonstrate that eskers become more frequent during deglaciation and that this coincides with periods of increased rates of ice margin recession and climatic warming
-	Such behavior is reminiscent of the seasonal evolution of drainage systems observed in smaller glaciers and implies that channelized drainage became increasingly important during deglaciation

**Discussion**:

-	Esker sediments are derived predominantly from local substrates (Bulduc, 1992)
-	No obvious variation in the lithology of the region to suggest that the observed increase in eskers during glaciation was due to changes in sediment.
-	Authors suggest that instead, the increase in esker density during deglaciation was as a result of increased meltwater fluxes in the subglacial hydrological system due to an increased volume of surface meltwater being routed into the subglacial system via moulins and englacial channels
-	Link: highly mirrors seasonal changes but on millennial level time-scales.
-	Consistent with numerical modelling experiments which predict increases in esker density with increased meltwater supply (Boulton et al., 2009); [[Hewitt, 2011]]).
