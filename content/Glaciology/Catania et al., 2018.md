# Geometric Controls on Tidewater Glacier Retreat in Central Western Greenland

![[Pasted image 20250520164940.png]]
- Monitor 15 tidewater glaciers in Central Western Greenland between 1999 and 2016
- Use Landsat-7 data to monitor glacier termini

## Timing of progressive retreat onset

- Climate modes operating in the North Atlantic (e.g., Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation, coupled with recent, long-term warming) have resulted in the northward intrusion of deep water along the western coast of Greenland ([[Straneo and Heimbach, 2013]])
- The retreat of tidewater glaciers in Western Greenland has been coincident with the intrusion of warm water since the late 1990s ([[Motyka et al., 2011]])
- A delay in the northward transport of Atlantic Water could explain the observed several year delay in progressive retreat onset for ING and UMI, the northernmost two glaciers in the ROI.

## Changes in Velocity and Terminus Position

- Question of whether flow acceleration causes glacier retreat, or whether glacier retreat causes flow acceleration ([[Benn et al., 2007]]).
- Some observations / models suggest that enhanced calving triggers a dynamic change in flow speed and ice thickness, which rapidly propagates up glacier ([[Nick et al., 2010]]).
- Conversely, others have suggested that acceleration may be initiated by changes in ice rheology and basal sliding, and increased calving is simply a consequence of up-flow acceleration ([[Bevan et al., 2015]]).

- Found no clear relationship between velocity and change in terminus position, thereby suggesting that the force-balance adjustment to retreat is complex.
- Examples:
	- ING has slowed since 2011 despite experiencing ongoing retreat through the end of the record in 2016
	- SIL experiences virtually no change in velocity even though it has undergone a 4-km retreat, with an acceleration in retreat in 2010

## Topographic control on retreat

![[Pasted image 20250520173836.png]]

- Despite a few years' difference in the onset of retreat, given the size of the area covered, retreat is essentially near-synchronous across the region.
- Bed conditions cannot result in a regionally coincident glacier retreat; rather, an external force must have pushed the glacier termini to locations where retreat is favourable.
- The range of retreat onset times could indicate that some glaciers may have required a greater push than others, possibly due to variations in bed conditions.

- While regional ocean and air temperature changes in the late 1990s appear responsible for pushing the glaciers in this region into an initial state of retreat, the pace and duration of retreat since then are more closely tied to fjord geometry.
- Retrograde beds result in increased ice discharge, leading to feedback which accelerates grounding line retreat ([[Schoof, 2007b]])
- Study finds that the amount of retreat is related to the length of the overdeepening behind the terminus at retreat onset.
- Two stable glaciers (STR and KAS) lie on prograde bed slopes that extend up the glacier by 2 and 1.5 km, respectively - only retreated 10 and 500 m, respectively, between 1999 and 2016.
- Some glaciers retreated over prograde bed sections who's lengths are shorter than the seasonal variation in the terminus - thus, not only do beds need to be prograde for stability, their prograde sections must be long enough so that typical seasonal changes in the terminus do not result in retreat beyond the length of the prograde bed section.

- There are cases where the fjord geometry cannot explain all the observed variability in terminus position.
- KAN stabilised on a retrograde bed - attributed to an enhanced ice flux during retreat which began to exceed the terminus melt rate ([[Rignot et al., 2016]]).
- Study observes sustained high velocities despote a stable terminus
- KAN is also pinned in a region of the fjord with a small narrowing, which may contribute to its stability by enhancing lateral drag.

## Conclusions

- While changes in runoff and ocean temperatures pushed the glaciers into a new state, the fjord topography is largely responsible for modulating the response to climate
- The control of topography provides explanations for previous studies which found greater variability in the retreat rate and durations of retreat between neighboring glaciers
