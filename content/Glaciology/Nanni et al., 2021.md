#  Observing the subglacial hydrology network and its dynamics with a dense seismic array


-	Importance of understanding when subglacial conditions impede or accelerate glacial flow for increased understanding of the vulnerability of glaciers to increased meltwater input as a result of changing climates -> contribution to sea level rise
-	Study uses a seismic-based approach to create a 2D map of the subglacial hydrology network – observes the day-to-day shift from a distributed system, favouring glacial flow, to a channel system, impeding glacial flow.
-	Method can be adaptable and applied to other glacial settings – ranging from alpine to polar glaciers.
Introduction:

-	Surface meltwater is routed to the glacial bed via crevasses and moulins. Within the glacier meltwater flows towards the terminus though the subglacial drainage system.
-	Water pressure regulates ice-bed mechanical coupling which is controls glacial sliding – significance on glacial movement speed and their stability and SLR.
-	Cavity-dominated (distributed):
-	Inefficient, high water pressure, reduces ice-bed mechanical coupling and promotes high sliding speeds. (Lliboutry, 1968; [[Kamb, 1987]])
-	Channel dominated system:
-	Efficient, lower water pressure, lower sliding speeds. ([[Röthlisberger, 1972]]; Hubbard et al., 1995)
-	The spatial persistence of high water pressure regions at the glacial bed depends on local rock permeability, the configuration of the subglacial drainage system and the connectivity across cavities and from cavities into channels.
-	Many contemporary observations of glaciers are point based through the drilling of bore holes to measure water pressure at various points or spatially integrated via dye-tracing or hydro chemical analysis. These methods are limited for they only provide small representations of a large, heterogeneous drainage system.
-	Bartholomaus (Hamish Montgomery but you can call me Barty) et al. (2015) show that turbulent water flow with velocities of the order of 1 ms-1 generate detectable seismic noise which can be used to predict the physical properties of subglacial channels – water pressure, size, space.
-	Multiple high resolution seismometers are required to create well-resolved subglacial drainage system maps – matched-field processing (MFP) techniques
-	MFP techniques have previously been used to locate spatially well-separated noises – oil and gas injection, hydrothermal injection, englacial moulins, icequakes.
-	Study adapts MFP techniques to locate spatially distributed noise sources.
-	Study uses an MFP output between 0 and 1 – 1 indicates a global phase coherence which is expected for a single, dominant and punctual source. Lower outputs indicate several local phase coherences – multiple sources acting over an area. 
-	Applying this to a glacier, higher outputs indicate dominant channels, whilst lower outputs indicate activity coming from a wide area – as characterised by a distributed drainage system.
Observing the switch from a cavity-like to a channel-like drainage system

-	Sources more spatially distributed at the beginning of the observation period – Late April. 
-	Inferred physical properties show low drainage efficiency and an increased surface acceleration – 50% higher due to enhanced basal sliding due to high water pressure in cavities.
-	Finds that seismically detectable water flow occurs not only in channels – as seen in previous studies (e.g., Gimbert et al., 2016) – but also in orifices between cavities (not previously recognised)
-	These findings are consistent with borehole field observations (Rada et al., 2018; Kabanaugh and Clarke, 2001) as well as theoretical studies (Kamp et al., 1987)
-	From May 10th subglacial flow localised into a narrower zone – MFP increases and the hydraulic pressure gradient decreases – follows the idea of low pressure dominant channel flow.
-	Hydraulic radius doubles
-	Consistent with the notion that increased water discharge increases drainage efficiency.
-	Interesting note:
-	Upon an initial increase in discharge, the glacier accelerates significantly before switching to a channel-like system and slowing down. Indicates a time lag between switching, during which increased pressures from increased discharge result in an increased flow before the rate of ice melt exceeds that of creep which leads to lower pressure and a ‘switch’ to a channelised system (Benn and Evans, 2010)
-	 
-	Study’s methods can determine:
-	when and where a cavity-dominated system transitions to a channel-dominated system
-	Localised areas of high or low hydraulic connectivity – whether that be within channels, within cavities, or in orifices -> therefore, can discriminate between high pressure locations with high connectivity which drain when develop, and areas where water becomes stored which are thought to have a large impact on basal traction over multiyear timescales (Hoffman et al., 2016; Williams et al., 2020) -> vulnerability of ice sheets (Greenland) and instability.
-	Limitations / perspectives:
-	Scaling constraints – requires a large number of sensors over large areas (10,000 sensors for a 10km x 10km area). However, new technologies are make large-scale seismic investigations more practical at limited costs -> distributed acoustic sensing
-	Quantitative interpretations of physical properties at local scales will benefit from combination with in situ observations. 
-	Seismic methodologies can guide where boreholes are drilled. 
-	Combination with other measurement techniques will enhance the quantitative interpretation of seismic observations increasing the accuracy of inferred physical characteristics
