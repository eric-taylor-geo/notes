#  Seasonal reorganisation of subglacial drainage inferred from borehole measurements

Abstract:

-	Boreholes dug into the subglacial drainage system of Haut Glacier d’Arolla, Switzerland.
-	Prior to drainage reorganisation into channels (31st July, 1993), boreholes displayed uncoordinated behaviour – impermeable patches with storage spaces.
-	Upon drainage reorganisation, borehole water levels were ford by discharge related changes in channel water pressure. 
-	Responses:
-	Synchronous – occurred in boreholes directly connected to the channel
-	Damped and lagged – occurred in boreholes connected to the channels via a more resistive drainage system
-	Inverse
-	Alternating inverse / lagged responses

Introduction:

-	Borehole water level measurements are used to characterise the behaviour of the subglacial drainage system via calculations of the head difference which are used to infer the likely flow of water between adjacent pairs of boreholes.
-	(however, hydraulic gradients between boreholes was not calculated as the true position of the base of each borehole was not known and the array did not allow the determination of the direction of maximum slope of the subglacial hydraulic potential surface)
-	Basally derived water tends to be more mineralized and turbid than supra/englacially derived water -> however, caution must be made with interpretation.

Distribution and reorganisation:
-	Boreholes were classified as either ‘connected’ or ‘unconnected’ to the glacier drainage system.
-	Connected holes: saw water level fall during or after drilling and subsequent fluctuation
-	Unconnected holes: saw no fall in water level
-	Period 2 (28th – 3rd August 1993) saw a major subglacial drainage reorganization – post-event water level fluctuations were very different.

Subglacial Channel Formation:

-	Dye-tracing studies at Haut Glacier d’Arolla provide some of the best evidence for the evolution of drainage systems.
-	Nienow (1993): the dye travel time from injected moulins to the glacier snout decreased over the melt season until it reached a stable minimum which provides evidence for the development of more efficient (channelised systems) drainage networks over the melt season.
-	Decreasing travel time began before the snowline passed each moulin. However, the minimum travel time was only reached up to five days after the snowline had passed inject moulins.
-	Nienow (1993) concludes that by the time the minimum time was reached, the moulin had become connected to a system of major channels – prior to this date, inefficient distributed drainage systems lay between the moulin and channel system – channel system grew headwards towards the moulin, reducing the size of the distributed system until the moulin was surpassed.
-	Gordon et al. uses water input modelling to determine whether switch in drainage systems was linked to changes in water inputs.
-	Suggest that channel formation was initiated by the rise of maximum and mean diurnal water inputs into upstream moulins.
-	Also heavy rainfall resulted in the instant connection of H35 borehole.

Conclusions

-	Major changes in borehole behaviour developed as a result of the development of a subglacial channel.
-	Channel formation was as a result of rising melt and rainfall-induced discharges, resulting in high water pressures at the glacier base.
-	Holes directly connected to channels showed identical fluctuations in water levels
-	Those connected via resistive drainage systems showed dampened and lagged fluctuations.
