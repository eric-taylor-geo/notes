# Rising atmospheric moisture escalates the future impact of atmospheric rivers in the Antarctic climate system


- Atmospheric rivers only occur 1% of the time at a given location along the Antarctic coastline, yet they are associated with 13% of the annual total precipitation and 35% of the interannual variability in precipitation over Antarctica ([[Wille et al., 2021]]).
- Snowfall is the dominant impact of ARs on the Antarctic Ice Sheet in the present day
- ARs are also associated with the majority of extreme surface melt events in West Antarctica and on the Antarctic Peninsula, contributing to the destabilisation of buttressing ice shelves ([[Wille et al., 2025]]).
- Studies suggest that atmospheric warming will drive strong AR frequency increases in future climates due to the exponential relationship between temperature and atmospheric moisture capacity through the Clausius-Clapeyron effect ([[Zhang et al., 2024b]]).
- Uses reanalysis and climate models to determine the impact of warming on the frequency of ARs and their impacts on the Antarctic Ice Sheet.

## Results

### Change in frequency

- When applying present-day thresholds for AR detection (98th percentile of the poleward component of integrated water vapour transport), using CESM2, the frequency of ARs over Antarctic Ice Sheet double.
- When raising the threshold by the relative increase in integrated water vapour transport at the end of the 21st century to account for moisture increases, future AR frequencies are similar to the present-day.
	- *I feel like this is a no shit scenario? Things that happen 2% of the time relative to a threshold will also occur 2% of the time if the threshold is suitably scaled*
- With a scaling in the threshold, there are three distinct regions of alternating AR frequency increase and decrease.
- Increases are around West Antarctica, Dronning Maud Land, and the Amery Ice Shelf, and decreases are over the Antarctic Peninsula, Enderby Land, and Wilkes Land
	- Magnitude of frequency change is less than 0.6 days yr-1
- Moisture changes dominate increases in AR frequency relative to present-day thresholds, while changes in atmospheric circulation play a secondary role in producing a small, zonal wave three-like change in AR frequencies through eastward shift of the polar jet and southward shift of the storm track ([[Raphael, 2004]]). _ATMOSPHERICS MENTIONED IN GLACIOLOGY 🦅 !!!_

### Impacts on precipitation

- Present day (1980-2014), ARs contribute 418 ± 29 Gt yr-1 of precipitation (1.15 mm SLR equiv, 13 ± 0.6% of annual total precipitation) according to CESM2.
- AR preciptation is highest in coastal Antaractica (100 mm w.e. yr-1) and decreases rapidly from the coast towards the interior (10 mm w.e. yr-1), which is rarely reached by ARs.
- Different models produce a bias in precipitation - CESM2 exhibits a positive bias relative to MERRA-2, up to 450 mm w.e. yr-1 for total annual precipitation.

- Using present-day vIVT thresholds for future AR detection, AR precipitation increases 2.5 times over Antarctica over 2066-2100, rising to 1012 ± 138 Gt yr-1 (2.8 mm SLR equiv).
- Increases in AR precipitation outpace increases in total precipitation, causing the relative contribute of ARs to total precipitation to rise to 24 ± 2% (from 13 ± 0.6%).
- Though, when using adjusted thresholds, the relative contribution of ARs remains the same.
- Despite no change in the relative contribution of ARs when using adjusted thresholds, they do contribute to more rainfall (up to 40 mm w.e. yr-1).
- Given the compounding impacts of rainfall in Antarctica, including the raising of firn temperatures through refreezing of liquid water, reduction of surface albedo, firn air depletion, and ice erosion – all of which precondition the surface for widespread melting ([[Amory et al., 2024]])

## Discussion

- The future role of ARs in the Antarctic climate system is extremely vulnerable to the detection method chosen.
- Ultimately, how we detect Antarctic ARs and attribute precipitation in the present-day, and how we adapt these methodologies to future climate states, will determine how we describe the importance of ARs in the Antarctic climate system.
- Need for a finer spatial resolution (currently is a 1 degree model grid spacing) to provide higher fidelity representations of AR structure, interactions with topography, and surface impacts, to allow for regional climate modelling for future AR studies ([[Gilbert et al., 2025]]).
