# Response of debris-covered glaciers in the Mount Everest region to recent warming, and implications for outburst flood hazards

## Abstract

- Wastage of debris-covered glaciers can result in the formation of large moraine-dammed lakes which poses a risk of glacier lake outburst floods (GLOFs)
- Paper uses observations of glaciers in the Everest region to provide insight into debris-covered glacier responses to climate change.

## Introduction

- Debris cover affects glacier response to climate change by altering surface ablation rates and spatial patterns of mass loss ( Nakawo et al., 1999 , Benn and Lehmkuhl, 2000 , Kirkbride, 2000 , Benn et al., 2003 )
- Mass loss and lake formation on debris-covered glaciers are the outcomes of various processes that translate climatic signals into changes in glacier ablation zones.
	- Debris presence complicates the chain of cause and effect than on clean-glaciers - results in a non-linear response to climate forcing - difficult to predict
- Varying initial conditions can determine whether lakes will form under the same climatic forcing - thus it is important to understand the glaciological context (controls on mass balance, ice dynamics, and meltwater evacuation and storage) in order to determine suitable risk mitigation and management

- Paper uses field and remote sensing observations of the Mount Everest Region
- Defines Everest Region as:
	- "we define the Everest region broadly, to include the entire horseshoe-shaped complex of peaks and ridges straddling the border between Nepal and Tibet (China), between the Tama Kosi basin in the west, the Dudh Kosi basin in the south, and the Arun river basin in the north and east"
- Includes 4 of the 6 highest mountains:
	- Mount Everest (8848m)
	- Lhotse (8516m)
	- Makalu (8642m)
	- Cho Oyu (8201m)

- There is a complete lack of *in situ* mass balance data for debris-covered glaciers in the Everest Region

## Climatic Background

- Everest Region is near the northern limit of the South Asian monsoon
- Experiences summer precipitation maximum and cold, dry winters
- Data from Pyramid Meteorological Station on Khumbu Glacier (5050 m) shows that 85% of precipitation falls between June and September [[Bertolani et al., 2000]]
- Tropical cyclones and westerly disturbances bring heavy autumn and winter snowfall
- Accumulation can occur at high elevations throughout the year.
- Data from [[Asahi, 2010]] shows a strong negative precipitation gradient from South to North up the Dudh Kosi valley to the South of Mount Everest.
	- Precipitation exceeds $2 myr^{-1}$ at 2000 m a.s. lear the mountain front and declines to 465 $mm yr^{-1}$ at Khumbu Glacier (5000 m asl)
- Orographic effects superimpose significant local variation upon this broad regional gradient [[Barros et al., 2004]]
- Direct measurements of precipitation above 5000 m are lacking - this data gap has been addressed using satellite data, however inconsistencies remain between different methods of estimating preciptation totals [[Andermann et al. 2011]] - *note - is this still the case?*
- Climatic records in the Himalaya are of short duration but many present evidence of a warming trend:
	- 0.6ºC per decade between 1971 and 1994 [[Shrestha et al., 1999]]
	- Continued warming since [[Shrestha and Aryal, 2011]] (2770 m)
	- 0.3ºC per decade at 4300 m [[Yang et al., 2006]]
	- Climate records show little or no trend in precipitation - however there are large internal variations associated with large-scale phenomena such as ENSO [[Yang et al., 2006]]

## Glaciers of the Everest Region

- As defined by the authors, the Everest Region contains an area of 1930 km2 of glacier ice and permanent snow - 445 km2 of which is debris-covered 
- 664 glaciers in the region from glacier inventory [[Higuchi et al., 2010]]
	- 47 are debris-covered but these 47 occupy 80% of the total glacierised area [[Sakai et al., 2000b]]
- Three major debris-covered glaciers flow from the flanks of Mount Everest:
	- Khumbu Glacier (south)
	- Rongbuk Glacier (slay) (North)
	- Kangshung Glacier (east)

- Mountainsides lining debris-covered glacier margins deliver snow, ice, and rock onto the glacier through avalanching [[Benn and Owen, 2002]] [[Hambrey et al., 2008]]
- Where accumulation occurs through direct snowfall, snowfields can develop on the upper accumulation areas of glaciers
- However, many glaciers lack snowfields and accumulation occurs at the base of steep backwalls (*it's giving Cwm Idwal*) [[Benn and Lehmkuhl, 2000]]
- Debris cover tends to develop immediately below the equilibrium line altitude of avalanche-fed glaciers.
- Glaciers with snowfields in addition to being avalanche-fed can have clean-ice areas between their ELAs and areas of debris melt-out
	- This is important to consider when estimating the ELA from the maximum elevation of debris-cover [[Owen et al., 2009]]

**Debris Composition**
- The lithological composition of debris reflects its source.
- Glaciers in catchments with complex geology can exhibit what is referred to as 'compositional striping' 
- Values of debris albedo measured on Ngozumpa Glacier were between 0.15 and 0.25 [[Nicholson, 2005]]
- *Link with [[Nicholson and Benn, 2013]]*

- Debris-covered glaciers in the Everest Region have undergone significant downwasting in recent decades [[Bolch et al., 2011a]]
- Accompanied by reduced glacier surface gradients and velocities and increasing proportions of lower ablation zones becoming stagnant [[Quincey et al., 2009]]
- Tongues of large debris-covered glaciers are highly irregular and studded with supraglacial ponds and lakes
- [[Benn et al., 2001]] characterise two types of supraglacial lakes, based on their elevation relative to the base level of the hydrological system.
	- Perched lakes are located above the level at which water leaves the glacier. They can only exist if they are underlain by impermeable glacier ice and rarely exceed 100 to 200 m in diameter before drainage occurs
	- Base-level lakes are located at the elevation at which water leaves the glacier. These lakes can persist much longer as long as moraine dams remain intact.
**GLOFs**

- Glacier lake outburst flood
- Several GLOFs have occurred in recent decades across the Everest Region.
- 1977 saw the drainage of a series of lakes on and below Nare Glacier which caused significant erosion and loss of agricultural land downstream [[Buchroithner et al., 1982]]
- 1985 saw a moraine-dammed lake in front of Langmoche Glacier almost completely drain following an ice avalanche [[Vuichard and Zimmermann, 1987]]
	- Destroyed a newly completed hydro-electric plant and nermous homes and agricultural land - 4 fatalities
- 1998 - Sabai Tsho burst [[Otsi et al., 2011]]
- These events brought the risk of GLOFs in the region to the attention of locals and the need for hazard assessment and mitigation [[Reynolds, 1998]] [[Yamada, 1998]]
- Erosional scars indicate that there have been additional GLOF events than recorded in the literature
## Recent changes in glacier volume

- [[Bolch et al., 2011a]] measure volume changes on glaciers south of Mount Everest (no mention of Rongbuk asides from agreeing with climate data)
- Differing from clean-ice glaciers, on most debris-covered glaciers in the study, the maximum mass loss was not at the terminus (which is debris-covered) (as would be expected on clean-ice glaciers) but further up the ablation area.

## Ice Velocities

[[Quincey et al., 2009]] distinguish three types of velocity structure on debris-covered glaciers in the Everest Region (n = 20)

**Type 1:**
- Displacement across the whole surface
- Only example is Kangshung Glacier on the lee side of Mt Everest - frequently exposed to high westerly winds which results in lots of hanging glaciers feeding ice onto the main glacier.
- Lee side accumulation may be buffering Kangshung Glacier's response to regional climate warming
**Type 2:**
- Displacement in their upper areas but extensive stagnation or slowly moving ice in their lower ablation zones
- Most glaciers tier 2
**Type 3:**
- No clear evidence for displacement anywhere on their surface

- The distribution of Type 3 and 2 glaciers raises the possibility that stagnant zones will extend farther up many glaciers if warming trends continue.
- Khumbu Glacier:
	- Evidence for progressive decreases in surface velocities [[Seko et al., 1998]] [[Nuimura et al., 2011]]
	- Area of stagnant ice increasing [[Luckman et al., 2007]]
- Most likely cause of stagnation is the reduction in ice thickness and surface gradient.

## Mass Balance

- There are no direct measurements of ablation or accumulation rates on debris-covered glaciers in the Everest Region
- Snow accumulation rates have been reconstructed from an ice core on East Rongbuk [[Kaspari et al., 2008]] - ~0.45m per year
	- Long-term changes in accumulation rates have been linked to variations in northward incursions of the South Asian monsoon [[Kaspari et al., 2007]]

- Ice ablation occurs through four main processes on debris-covered glaciers:
1. Melting of clean ice close to glacier ELAs
2. Melting beneath surface debris
3. Melting of ice cliffs and calving around the margins of supraglacial ponds in the debris-covered area
4. Calving into deep proglacial lakes
