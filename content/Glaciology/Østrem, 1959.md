# Ice Melting under a Thin Layer of Moraine, and the Existence of Ice Cores in Moraine Ridges

**From Proposal**:

[[Ben et al 2007]]

Understanding the thickness of supraglacial debris is crucial due to its significant impact on glacier thermodynamics by attenuating the energy transfer from the atmosphere to the ice surface (Østrem, 1959; [[Brock et al., 2010]]). The melt rate at the ice surface increases as debris thickness increases until a critical thickness is reached, which represents the maximum ablation rate, owing to the lower albedo of the debris surface compared to clean ice and snow (Østrem, 1959). Beyond this threshold, the attenuation of thermal energy from the atmosphere through the debris negates the increased energy absorption at the surface by the darker debris, resulting in debris insulating the ice surface, thereby reducing a glacier’s sensitivity to external fluctuations in temperature (Østrem, 1959). Therefore, the ability to monitor the debris thickness of mountain glaciers is crucial for understanding mountain glaciers’ response to a changing climate.
