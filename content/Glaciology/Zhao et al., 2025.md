# Active ice sheet conservation cannot stop the retreat of Sermeq Kujalleq glacier, Greenland (Jakobshavn Isbræ)

- Targeting geoengineering on glaciers has been proposed to mitigate their melt, whereby artificial barriers are added to basin/fjord inlets in order to prevent the intrusion of deeper, warm water ([[Wolovick and Moore, 2018]]).
- Warm Atlantic Water ([[Wood et al., 2021]]) and mCDW ([[Stokes et al., 2022]]) are driving retreat in Greenland and Antarctica, respectively. Both of which are intruding beneath the cool, fresh surface water.
- Raising the elevation of inlets inhibits deeper water intrusion, allowing the glacier to thicken, which can increase buttressing.

- The study models the retreat of Jakobshavn Isbræ using an ice sheet model combined with a fjord model, and forces it under varying climate scenarios to 2100.
- Artificial barriers to limit the intrusion of warm water, cooling the fjord and reducing melt.
- However, even in doing so, the glacier is unable to stabilise.
- Authors thus suggest that the glacier is in an unstoppable phase of marine ice sheet instability on a rapidly deepening, retrograde sloping bed, which continues 40 km inland.
	- Under low atmospheric forcing, the glacier stabilises on this prograde bed.
	- Under higher atmospheric forcing, the glacier retreats nonetheless.
- The lack of reaching a stable state under any future climate forcing, even when using historical data close to the 1960 forcing, indicates that the glacier has surpassed the tipping point of MISI.  

## Indigenous concerns [[Glaciology - Human]]

- Retreat of grounding lines has resulted in increased productivity within fjords through the release of nutrients from sediment entrapped in ice, promoting the local fishing economy ([[Mettiäinen et al., 2024]]).
- Erecting a barrier would harm traditional ways of life.
- Based on the retreat irrespective of barrier scenario, it would neither be of a local or global benefit to make an intervention at Jakobshavn Isbræ
- There are other fjords, however, where no local fishing communities rely on their productivity, which could be possible sites of local geoengineering.
- Zacharia Glacier, northeast Greenland, has been in disequilibrium since 2003 ([[Mouginot et al., 2015]]).
- A barrier over a ridge may have better potential to stabilise the glacier. Important because it is one of two glaciers which drain the northeast Greenland ice stream, which drains 12% of Greenland's ice.
