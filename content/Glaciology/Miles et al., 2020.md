# Hydrology of debris-covered glaciers in High Mountain Asia

- Short term increase in discharge, followed by long term decrease. The passing of this peak water discharge threatens water security in many regions, including the Himalaya
	- (Bolch et al., 2019; Eriksson et al., 2009; Hannah et al., 2005; Huss and Hock,  2018; Immerzeel et al., 2010; Winiger et al., 2005).
- Around 25% of Earth’s population is dependent on melted glacier ice and/or seasonal snow 56 for drinking water, irrigation or hydroelectric power (Immerzeel et al., 2010);
- It is therefore crucial that the production, transportation, and storage of meltwater is understood
- Research is limited by the remoteness and inaccessibility of Himalayan glaciers.
- The configuration and efficiency of water transport across and through debris-covered glaciers is different to that of clean-ice glaciers ([[Fyffe et al., 2019b]])

## Supraglacial Hydrology

- The spatial pattern of melt is complicated by the presence of debris
- The ablation rate peaks at a debris thickness of 2-5mm - this is refGerred to as the **effective thickness** (Adhikary et al., 2000; Evatt et al., 2015; Inoue and Yoshida, 1980; Juen et al., 2014; Lejeune et al., 2013;  [[Nicholson and Benn, 2013]], 2006; [[Østrem, 1959]]; Singh et al., 2000; Takeuchi et al., 2000).
- The values of the effective thickness depend on the thermal conductivity of debris, and are thus controlled by the geology where debris is sourced from - varying in space.
- Their values can also vary in time based on whether the debris is wet or dry (Casey et al., 2012; Collier et al., 2015, 2014; Gibson et al., 2017b;  [[Nicholson and Benn, 2013]]; Pelto, 2000)
- Beyond the effective thickness, the attenuation of thermal radiation outweighs the effect of the decreased albedo of the glacier surface, thereby inhibiting the receipt of surface energy at the ice-debris interface.
- Thus, the ice is insulated from daily flucations in incoming solar radiation and only longer-term changes in the surface energy balance are reflected in the debris-ice interface (Bocchiola et al., 2015; [[Brock et al., 2010]]; Conway and Rasmussen, 2000; [[Nicholson and Benn, 2013]]; [[Østrem, 1959]]; [[Reid and Brock, 2010]]).

**Meltwater Generation:**

- Supraglacial ponds, often formed as a result of a thick surface debris layer, enhance the ablation rate.
- Ice cliffs also form from the slumping of debris - calving at supraglacial pond margins, or into englacial voids - which exposes steep, bare ice or thinner debris-covered surfaces ([[Benn et al., 2012]])
- Ice cliffs are responsible for a huge proportion of ablation on debris-covered glaciers 
	- (Brun et al., 2016; Buri et al., 2016b; 139 Han et al., 2010; Juen et al., 2014; Reid and Brock, 2014; Sakai et al., 2002, 2000; Thompson et al., 2016)
- Despite only covering 2% of total glacial area, they account for almost 70% of total ablation on debris-covered glaciers *(damn)* (Immerzeel et al., 2014; Sakai et al., 1998)
- Undercutting and calving processes at the cliffs around supraglacial ponds can also increase ablation 
	- (Brun et al., 2016; Buri et al., 2016a; Miles et al., 2016; Röhl, 2008; Thompson et al., 2016).

**Meltwater Storage**:

- Supraglacial ponds are largely absent from clean-ice valley glaciers - but are prevalent on low-gradient areas of ice sheet margins (Chu, 2014; Sundal et al.,  2009).
- The gradient has the greatest control over the location of supraglacial ponds for both clean and debris-covered glaciers ([[Miles et al., 2017b]]; [[Quincey et al., 2007]];  Reynolds, 2000; Sakai, 2012; Sakai et al., 2000; Sakai and Fujita, 2010; Salerno et al., 2012)
- Velocity and motion also control supraglacial pond formation [[Miles et al., 2017b]]

**Pond formation:**

- Pond growth is initiated through subaqueous melting at the base of any initial slight depression. 
- Water that accumulates in the depression is heated by solar radiation resulting in lateral and vertical ablation where ice is in contact with water, resulting in the growth of the pond,
- This further steepens margin slopes, allowing the exposure of bare ice.
- References:
	- (Chikita et al., 1998; Mertes et al., 2016; Miles et al., 2016; Stokes et al., 2007;  Thompson et al., 2012)
- Ponds can turn into moraine-dammed lakes when multiple coalesce [[Benn et al., 2012]]
- Supraglacial ponds, at various stages of their evolution are present simultaneously on debris-covered glaciers and their area has been increasing in recent decades (Benn et al., 2000; Watson et al., 2016)
- Deepened ponds become connected to the englacial hydrological system, resulting in drainage which inhibits further pond expansion ([[Benn et al., 2001]]; [[Mertes et al., 2016]])
- Ponds can exploit inherited structural weaknesses in ice such as former crevasses and englacial channels that have previously been forced close through longitudinal compression (Benn et al., 2009, 2012, 2017; [[Miles et al., 2017b]]) - *Thus, the location on the glacier is important. Also other factors such as slope gradient and velocity to determine the presence of cracks*
- The relationship between pond growth and drainage into the englacial hydrological system can result in a periodic cycle of expansion and drainage.
- Upon a more permanent connection to the englacial hydrological system, supraglacial ponds become more stable ([[Benn et al., 2001]]; [[Miles et al., 2017a]])
- The increasing presence of ponds has been described as the clearest indicator of the influence of climate change on debris-covered glaciers (Salerno et al., 2012).

**Meltwater Transport**:

- Conditions required for the formation of supraglacial streams:
	- A supply catchment is required ([[Benn et al., 2017]]; [[Gulley et al., 2009a]])
	- Rate of stream incision (driven by thermal erosion) must outpace the rate of surface lowering (Marston, 1983)
- The presence of debris will affect these conditions.
- Hummocky topography will limit the size of supraglacial catchments ([[Fyffe et al., 2019b]]), and will prevent streams from persisting over long distances ([[Benn et al., 2017]])
- Supraglacial streams may drain into debris-covered glaciers through crevasses or moulins ([[Gulley et al., 2009a]]; Iwata et al., 1980), or through ‘cut-and-closure’  (Gulley et al., 2009a; Jarosch and Gudmundsson, 2012).
- Supraglaicl streams are, therefore, often observed down glacier of crevasses but still up glacier of hummocky topography and thick debris presence.
- Example:
	- Perennial supraglacial sstream on the upper ablation area of Khumbu Glacier [[Gulley et al., 2009a]] [[Miles et al., 2019]]
- Streams within debris-covered areas can undergo rapid changes to their course

**Supraglacial knowledge gaps**

- Surface lowering is resulting in increased debris thickness and the emergence of thin supraglacial debris layers upglacier [[Gibson et al., 2017a]]
	- This is likely to result in a cycle of further decreasing albedo and increased surface meltwater production (further exacerbating surface lowering) until newly exposed debris thickens sufficiently to insulate the surface and break this cycle [[Stokes et al., 2007]]
- Meltwater measurement is crucial but difficult underneath debris layers
- The evolution of future surface geometry is uncertain - meaning there are large uncertainties in whether meltwater will be transported off the glacier through channels or whether it will be stored in supraglacial ponds, affecting diurnal proglacial discharge.
- More understanding is needed on the storage and transportation through and between supraglacial ponds
- Discerning supraglacial streams in debris-covered regions remains difficult - especially for smaller streams
- Water storage within and below supraglacial debris layers - this would result in a lag time to the transport of meltwater throughout the englacial hydrological system

## Englacial Hydrology

**Englacial Zone**

- Exploration of englacial conduits in debris-covered glaciers has been primarily carried out in the Nepal Himalaya
	- E.g., Khumbu Glacier [[Gulley et al., 2009a]]
- Gulley et al. (2009a) propose three formation mechanisms for englacial conduits within debris-covered glaciers

>**Cut-and-closure**
- Cut-and-closure type conduits are more prevalent on debris-covered glaciers than clean-ice glaciers
- These processes require more rapid supraglacial channel incision which could result from the presence of a cold ice surface or from supraglacial debris impeding surface lowering
- Conduits may be repeatedly abandoned and reopened depending upon the variation in water discharge throughout the year.
- Conduits rarely close completely owing to their relatively shallow depth (low overburden ice pressure means that deformation and creep rates are slow)
- Sediment deposited within englacial streams provide routes of secondary permeability through which the conduit could be reactivated [[Gulley et al., 2009a]] [[Benn et al., 2009a]]

>**Exploiting lines of secondary permeability**
- Melt water can exploit lines / planes of secondary permeability
- These include relict cut-and-close conduits, former surface crevasses which have since been closed
- Discharge through low-permeability zones enlargens lines of preferential flow, resulting in englacial conduits forming through heat dissipation [[Benn et al., 2012]]

>**Hydrofracturing**
- Hydrofracturing is mostly restricted to the upper, debris-free areas where surface runoff can freely enter open crevasses [[Benn et al., 2012]]
- In the lower ablation area, the low surface gradient reduced the capacity for crevassing (low strain from $tanα$)
- [[Benn et al., 2009a]] observed multiple stages of hydrofracture followed by conduit closure on Khumbu Glacier

**Higher englacial**

- If a stream exploits a crevasse for sufficient time, it forms a moulin
- Shallow-gradient conduits in the lower ablation area of Khumbu Glacier suggest their formation through cut-and-closure rather than crevasse exploitation [[Miles et al., 2019]]
- The surface gradient of the ablation area of debris-covered glaciers is typically low which results in a low hydraulic gradient ([[Shreve, 1972]]), therefore encouraging meandering and the formation of various sinuous englacial conduits [[Miles et al., 2019]] - this has been observed on Khumbu Glacier [[Benn et al., 2017]]
- More perennial conduits are located along the edges of debris-covered glaciers ([[Benn et al., 2017]]) - the margins provide more hydraulically efficient pathways than the center of the glacier due to the presence of crevasse traces
- Engalcial conduits may become re-exposed after surface lowering [[Miles et al., 2019]]
- Many englacial systems have been observed at present depths, linking supraglacial ponds.
- Linked supraglacial-engalcial systems may be created / maintained by supraglacial pond drainage [[Gulley and Benn, 2007]] [[Narama et al., 2017]]
- Englacial conduits therefore are likely to play a role in the life cycle of supra glacial ponds
- The rapid expansion of englacial conduits in response to increase meltwater influx can result in ceiling collapse, thereby exposing subglacial channels - this in turn exposes new bare ice faces, including ice cliffs, which can contribute to further lowering of the glacier surface [[Benn et al., 2017]]

**Lower englacial**

- The efficiency of deeper englacial networks varies in efficiency in response to:
	- Supraglacial pond drainage
- Systems can adapt rapidly to greater meltwater inputs [[Miles et al., 2019]], as observed on Khumbu Glacier.
- The efficiency of englacial networks changes throughout the melt season - monsoon precipitation in HMA can result in the reopening of englacial and subglacial conduits
- Meltwater utility results in conduit erosion and further ablation [[Miles et al., 2017b]] [[Sakai et al., 2000]]

**Storage**

- Englacial reservoirs can store meltwater
- These have been inferred on various glaciers [[Hewitt et al., 1989]] [[Rounce et al., 2017]]

**Miage Glacier** [[Fyffe et al., 2019b]]

- Upglacier ice is covered by a thin debris layer - during the melt season it produced an efficient drainage system to the terminus
- The heavily debris-covered lower ablation area restricted the development of an efficient subsurface system. The drainage system of the lower ablation area instead flowed into the efficient system driven from upglacier.

**Further research**

- Links between englacial system and other hydrological domains
- Distinction between supraglacial streams and shallow englacial streams

## Subglacial Hydrology

**Subglacial Zone**

- Knowledge of subglacial drainage systems in HMADCG is limited
- Glaciospeleological investigations concluded that the proglacial stream of a retreating tributary of Khumbu Glacier reached the bed ([[Benn et al., 2018]])
- Also evidence along the edge of the neighbouring Ngozumpa Glacier [[Benn et al., 2017]] [[Miles et al., 2019]]
- Water did not persist subglacially and exited the glacier supraglacially - the up-routing of water likely occurs due to a high hydrological base-level (common in the region) - this potentially follows the glacier's cold-temperature transition surface [[Miles et al., 2018]] [[Miles et al., 2019]]
- Beyond these studies - all other information regarding subglacial drainage is inferred (as of time of writing ofc)
**Inferred Studies:**
- *E.g.,  [[Zhang et al., 2023]] inference of subglacial drainage networks from surface velocity studies*
- Surge phases are indicative of basal lubrication from subglacial drainage [[Benn et al., 2017]] [[Quincey et al., 2009]]
- Channelised subglacial drainage systems have been inferred from outlet streams at the terminus of DCG. During the melt season, these streams discharge heavily debris-laden water implying sediment entrainment during transport along the bed [[Quincey et al., 2009]] - this is indicative of subglacial streams having a high competence and capacity, conducive to channelised flow.
- Comparisons between supraglacial and proglacial solute concentrations on Lirung Glacier show that proglacial water is high in $Ca^{2+}$ and $SO_4^{2-}$ concentrations which indicate contact with reactive debris which occurs during subglacial drainage (?) [[Bhatt et al., 2007]]
- A perennially active subglacial stream on Dokriani Glacier was inferred to be connected with the englacial system based on proglacial electrical conductivity measurements [[Hasnain and Thayyen, 1994]]

**Variations in efficiency and network**

- Increased in flux and size of subglacially eroded suspended particles through the melt season was indicative of the increasing efficiency and interconnection of the subglacial system of Gangotri Glacier [[Haritashya et al., 2010]]
- Dye tracing experiences have shown the transition from a distributed to a channelised network over the melt season [[Liu et al., 2018]]

**Knowledge gaps**

- Little is known owing to the difficulty in accessing subglacial systems
- Lots of DCG in HMA terminate in lakes, which increases the likelihood of a form of subglacial drainage system but reduces its likelihood of being channelised - *why?*
- Lakes also prevent access to outflow streams
- Unknown whether subglacial networks flow directly into proglacial ponds at the bed or are routed upwards to join englacial / supraglacial streams (whilst it has been inferred that englacial streams do sometimes connect back with the supraglacial network [[Miles et al., 2019]])
	- Or whether subglacial channels are lost to groundwater
- It is important to track lost meltwater compenent
	- Groundwater
	- Short-Long-term storage englacially
	- Evaporation

## Proglacial Hydrology

**Proglacial Lakes**

- The presence of proglacial lakes is one of the most distinctive characteristics of HMA debris-covered glaciers.
	- far more common than their clean-ice counterparts
- Lakes form from the continuation of thinning and supraglacial pond growth
- The deposition of debris creates high terminal moraines.
- Proglacial lakes determine the hydrological base-level of the glacier, and are often dammed by terminal moraine [[Thompson et al., 2012]]
- Moraine-dammed proglacial lakes represent the final stage a tripartate evolution of debris-covered glaciers, as defined by [[Benn et al., 2012]]
1. All parts of the glacier are dynamically active
2. Surface lowering begins and ice velocities decrease
3. Glaciers are stagnant and rapid recession may occur
- The formation of a proglacial lake is indicative of stagnation and rapid recession which can occur through the expansion of the proglaical lake [[Benn et al., 2012]]
- An increasing number of proglacial lakes growing in size has been observed across the Hindu Kush Himalaya [[Thompson et al., 2012]]
- Proglacial lakes expand through similar mechanisms to supraglacial ponds - through subaqueous melting and subaerial ice face melting - until they are limited by the substrate.
- Once the proglacial lake triggers calving, it becomes the dominant process of lake grwoth [[Röhl, 2008]] [[Thompson et al., 2012]]
- The water depth can become sufficient to trigger extending flow in the now-unsupported ice cliff, increasing flow velocities and weakening ice through crevasse formation - crevasse formation will also further expose bare ice, increasing ablation rates and surface lowering
	- This can result in rapid and unstable calving - was ibserved at Tasman Glacier, NZ [[Kirkbride and Warren, 1999]]
	- Modelled in Bhutan Himalaya [[Tsutaki et al., 2019]]
- Larger proglacial lakes can alter glacial microclimates owing to thei rlower albedo and higher thermal heat capacity relative to the surrounding ice and soil which can produce locally cooler summer air temperatures and warmer autumn temperatures [[Carrivick and Tweed, 2013]]
	- Implications on summer ablation and meltwater production
- If a DCG terminates in a moraine-dammed proglacial lake, then the majority of water transported through a DCG will pass through the lake [[Benn et al., 2017]]

**Proglacial Streams**

- Proglacial runoff forms a significant fraction of discharge to large rivers downstream.
- The Indus, Dudh Koshi, Ganges, and Brahamputra rivers all stem from glacial meltwater [[Pritchard, 2019]] [[Ragettli et al., 2015]] [[Wilson et al., 2016]]
- Surface debris presence affects the proglacial hydrograph 
- Peak melt can occur up to several hours after the maximum radiation receipt at the debris surface [[Carenzo et al., 2016]] [[Conway and Rasmussen, 2000]] [[Evatt et al., 2015]]
	- [[Fyffe et al., 2014]] found peak melt to be 24 hours after peak radiation receipt for debris layers >0.85 m thick.
	- Produces a later and less pronounced peak in diurnal pattern of debris-covered glacier's proglacial streams [[Fyffe et al., 2019a]] [[Fyffe et al., 2014]]
- Englacial storage can also affect basal water pressure and minimise lag in proglacial discharge [[Brock et al., 2010]]
