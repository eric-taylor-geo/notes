# Inferring Debris Properties on Debris-Covered Glaciers: Implications for Glacier Modelling

- Debris, ranging from thin surface dust to medial moraines and thick, continuous layers in ablation zones, partially covers glaciers all around the world
- Debris modifies the energy transfer from the atmosphere to the ice and fundamentally controls sub-debris melt rates.
- Physical properties (surface roughness and thermal conductivity) have only been derived from local measurements – modelling studies have relied upon these measurements – adding uncertainty in models ([[Reid and Brock, 2010]])
- Relatively few studies undertaken to evaluate the resulting property values -> possibility?
- Study takes properties of debris at Piramide Glacier, Chili.
- Find that deriving local debris properties from measurements is challenging and that measured values can differ greatly from literature values.
- Values derived can differ greatly depending on the method used.
- Furthermore, these values can differ from values required by an energy-balance model to accurately represent sub-debris ice melt.
- Energy-balance models typically assume a representation of heat transfer within the supraglacial debris layer based solely on conduction and requires a bulk thermal conductivity value – highlights the need to reevaluate measurements in the field and to reconsider debris properties in melt modelling.
