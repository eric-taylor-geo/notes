# Remotely sensed debris thickness mapping of Bara Shigri Glacier, Indian Himalaya

Limits of [[Mihalcea et al., 2008b]] and [[Mihalcea et al., 2008a]]:
- rely on a known relationship between debris thickness and the surface temperature -> extensive field measurements required for the date of satellite thermal imagery aquisition
- Limits the transferability both temporally to other scenes of the same glacier and spatially to other glaciers
[[Foster et al, 2012]] overcome these limitations by using an energy balance model approach for Miage Gliacer.
Physical models which can just use satellite imagery alongside meteorological or reanalysis climate data allow for the analysis or remote glaciers without the need for in situ data.
[[Rounce and McKinney, 2014]] develop further by applying energy balance modelling in the Everset Region 

This paper furthers Foster et al, applying it to Bara Shigri Glacier, India

## Model:

- Similar surface energy balance
- Differs slightly:
- Air temperature is typically extrapolated from station data by applying a spatially constant lapse rate - however this may not hold for debris covered glaciers.
- Foster et al propose an empirical relationship in the form:
- $T_a = a +bT_s$
- Derived from measurements on debris-covered glaciers
- Air temperature is strongly related to surface temperautre in the morning with strong insolation and low wind speed - this is especially relavent for the time of landsat aquisition

Modifications to Foster:
- don't use a linear temperature gradient
	- Citations relevant for dis: [[Conway and Rasmussen, 2000]] [[Han et al., 2006]] [[Nicholson and Benn, 2006]] [[Reid and Brock, 2010]] [[Lejeune et al. 2013]]
	- [[Rounce and McKinney, 2014]] demonstrate this

## Conclusions

- The energy-balance approach probably underestimates debris thickness, which is in line with results from other studies. We suggest that this underestimation is mainly due to the unknown vertical temperature profile and the rate of heat stored, and the uncertain turbulent sensible heat flux. Within the upper debris layers, both the vertical gradient of debris temperature and the rate of change of heat stored in the debris are likely much higher than assumed, thus leading to an underestimation of debris. Only detailed debris temperature measurements at different depths would help to improve knowledge of the thermal processes within the supraglacial debris. As mentioned by [[Rounce and McKinney, 2014]], mixed-pixel effects may also lead to an underestimation of debris thickness; however, they would probably not explain the overall underestimation across the tongue.
