# Drivers of Seasonal Land-Ice-Flow Variability in the Antarctic Peninsula

- Seasonal ice-flow variability has been detected on the Antarctic Peninsula ([[Boxall et al., 2022]]).
- Ice flow variability over intra-annual timescales has not been considered within discharge-based calculations of ice sheet mass balance ([[Rignot et al., 2011]]), thus depending on the acquisition timing of the observations used for velocity, Antarctica's mass balance may be over- or under-estimated, given the oversight of seasonality in discharge ([[Boxall et al., 2022]])
- Study correlates seasonal velocity records with a range of local-to large-scale climatic and oceanographic variables, each of which would be expected to presage seasonal speed-up.


- Find statistically significant correlations between surface meltwater  and ice velocity with a 0-1 month lag across all 16 studied glaciers
- Response is analagous to mechanisms observed in Greenland and valley glaciers where supraglacial malt enhances summertime velocities by lubricating the bed.
- A lack of surface ponding implies that hydrofracture is limited - unlike as observed in Greenland
- Authors suggest that the presence of aquifers within the snowpack, similar to those detected in Greenland ([[Poinar et al., 2017]]), near the northern ice front of GVIIS ([[Miller et al., 2022]]) could provide an alternative means of hydrofracture and meltwater delivery to the bed.
- If these features are close to the hydrofracture threshold, seasonal surface melt accumulation could rapidly result in hydrofracture, allowing rapid meltwater delivery to the bed and an almost instantaneous acceleration in glacier velocities
