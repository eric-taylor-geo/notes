# Ongoing grounding line retreat and fracturing initiated at the Petermann Glacier ice shelf, Greenland, after 2016

- Petermann buttressing 4%
- Reconstruct grounding line migration between 1992 and 2021 - the grounding line retreated 4 km in the western and eastern sectors and 7 km in the central part.
- The majority of retreat in the central sector took place between 2017 and 2021 where is receded more than 5 km along a retrograde bed, grounded 500 m below sea level.
- Glacier thinning due to flow acceleration results in grounding line retreat because a thinner glacier results in flotation sooner
- Calculating the grounding line retreat rate as simply a function of ice thickness ([[Rignot et al., 1998]]) change provides insights into the drivers of retreat.
- With dynamic thinning occurring at a rate of 1 m yr-1 on the ice sheet  ([[Smith et al., 2020]]), coupled with a -0.11% bed slope and 0.8% surface slope, the grounding line would be expected to retreat at 130 m yr-1, which is below satellite observations.

- Warm ocean water would erode the ice shelf faster by enhancing the submarine melt rate at the grounding line, promoting its retreat.
- Applying increased ocean melt from a thermal forcing of 0.3 degrees C (due to warm Atlantic water), increases the rate of thinning by 13 m y-1, which results in a grounding line retreat of 1.6 km yr-1, within 15% of the observed retreat.
- Thus, despite these simple assumptions, they indicate that the recent retreat of the grounding line is mainly caused by rising ocean temperatures rather than dynamic thinning.
