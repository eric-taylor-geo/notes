# Grounding line stability in a regime of low driving and basal stresses

- [[Weertman, 1974]] was the first to provide theoretical descriptions of the stability of marine-based ice sheets - 'stable ice sheet can occur if the bed slopes away from the center of the ice sheet'
- [[Schoof, 2007b]] developed a boundary layer theory for an ice stream undergoing rapid sliding at its grounding line - ice flux at the grounding line is a monotonically increasing function of ice thickness at the grounding line.
- It, therefore, follows through that ice sheets sitting on a retrograde bed is inherently unstable [[Schoof, 2012]] - refered to as the MISI hypothesis

- Studies have considered two distinct types of flow on grounded ice:
	- Those dominated by vertical shear e.g., [[Weertman, 1974]]
	- Those dominated by basal sliding e.g., [[Schoof, 2007b]]
- Assumptions that basal stress is roughly equal to driving stress and at the grounding line, the longitudinal stress must be balanced with hydrostatic pressure [[Schoof, 2007b]]
- Based on these assumptions and balances, [[Schoof, 2007b]] concluded that the ice thickness at the grounding line must be small relative to the ice thickness in the interior - thus the ice thickness gradient must be large near the grounding line
- However, observations display low surface gradients [[MacAyeal et al., 1995]] on present-day West Antarcrtic ice streams - eg., those flowing to the Ross Ice Shelf
- This is perhaps due to the weak beds beneath ice streams [[MacAyeal, 1989]]

## Discussion and conclusions

- Study focuses on the Siple Coast which has a low surface gradient which implies a low driving stress - tanα is low
- Model finds that low surface slopes are a direct result of low or absent basal shear and that the surface slow increases in response to the magnitude of basal shear. 
- Thus, observed low surface slopes on the Siple Coast are indicative of a low basal shear based on this model's findings.
- The study highlights the importance of accurate bed elevation measurements - low amplitude bed undulations can significantly increase form drag, but they cannot currently be resolved due to being much smaller than a single pixel.

- Finds that in low driving and basal stress regime, unconfined marine ice sheets do not conform exactly to the marine ice sheet instability hypothesis 
- Rather, (in)stability of the grounding line is determined by a combination of form drag, basal shear, the accumulation rate, and their gradients.
- Thus, bed shape alone is not a sufficient indicator of marine ice sheet instability.
