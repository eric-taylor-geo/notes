# The stability of grounding lines on retrograde slopes

- Understanding of the WAIS is crucial for quantifying estimates of future SLR
- Struggles with modelling - calculated grounding line positions are strongly dependent on grid resolution [[Vieli and Payne, 2005]]
- Shallow-ice approximation models do not allow for the correct description of grounding-line motion

## Model

- models in one horizontal dimension show that rapidly sliding marine-based ice sheets are subject to MISI
- However, this is less clear for two horizontal dimensions - paper seeks to demonstrate that MISI dos not hold unconditionally in 2HD
- Uses two models: three dimensional full stokes model and a vertically integrated model - (Shallow shelf approximation) SSA

## Discussion

- MISI idea rests on ice flux at the grounding line being a monotonically increasing function of ice thickness at the grounding line
- If this holds, then steady-state grounding-line positions of marine-type ice sheets on retrograde slopes are unstable [[Weertman, 1974]]
- [[Schoof, 2007b]] demonstrates that ice flux can be expressed as a function of the ice thickness and the longitudinal stress - ice flux is an increasing function of both of these variables.
- in 1HD, longitudinal stress is also an increasing function of thickness and thus ice flux is always an increasing function of the grounding line thickness.
- Whilst this holds in 1HD, modelling by the authors shows that in 2HD it is possible to have ice configurations where longitudinal stresses decrease with thickness which causes flux to be a decreasing function of cie thickness. In these scenarios, MISI is not expected to operate and it is possible to have a stable grounding-line on a retrograde slope.
- However, whilst possible theoretically, no such stable grounding line has been identified.
- Model also only looks at the stability of ice sheets with respect to the retrograde slope, and does not consider external forcing.

## Conclusions

- study provides specific numerical examples for model geometries where stable grounding lines are located on retrograde slopes.
- Marine ice sheets on such slopes are not inherently unstable.
- Confidence in these findings is robust owing to results agreeing from two different numerical models - full stokes and shallow shelf approximation using vertical integration
