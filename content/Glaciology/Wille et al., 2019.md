# West Antarctic surface melt triggered by atmospheric rivers

- Use an atmospheric river detection algorithm alongside surface melt datasets to produce a climatology of atmospheric river-related surface melting
- Demonstrates that atmospheric rivers are associated with a large proportion of high surface melt eventsx
- Event frequency: 12 per year
- Associated with % melt:
	- Ross Ice Shelf: 40%
	- Marie Byrd Land: 100%
	- Wilkins, Bach, George IV, Larsen B/C: 40-80%
- All events were related to high-pressure blocking ridges what directed anomalous poleward moisture transport toward the continent.
- Major melt events only occur a couple times per decade however future warming may result in an increase int heir frequency, with implications on ice shelf stability

## Main

- ARs represent narrow bands that contain enhanced water vapour fluxes typically associated with the low-level jet ahead of an extratropical cyclone's cold front and within the cyclone's warm conveyor belt.
- They cover only 10% of the globe's surface but account for 90% of the meridional moisture transport in the mid-to-high latitudes [[Nash et al., 2018]] https://doi.org/10.1029%2F2017JD028130
- Context of ARs in polar regions:
	- Delivery of warm, moist air, contributing to accumulation in EA [[Gorodetskaya et al., 2014]] https://doi.org/10.1002%2F2014GL060881
	- Sea ice decline in Arctic regions [[Komatsu et al., 2018]] https://doi.org/10.1038%2Fs41598-018-21159-6
	- Decreasing ice mass on the Greenland Ice Sheet [[Mattingly et al., 2018]] [[Neff, 2018]] https://doi.org/10.1029%2F2018JD028714 https://doi.org/10.1038%2Fs41558-018-0297-4
- Resultantly, ARs both increase accumulation and ablation, contributing either positively or negatively to surface mass balance

## Summer surface melting

- ARs can result in melt in regions which typically do not experience melting conditions - e.g., interior portions of the Ross Ice Shelf and the base of the Transantarctic Mountains
- 30-50% of melt days occurred within a day of an AR landfall
- Further inland, melt days decrease but the percentage apportioned to ARs increases (60-90% for higher up into Marie Byrd Land)
- AR landfall over the West Antarctic Ice Sheet triggers melting through various physical mechanisms
**Clouds and rainfall**
- The intrusion of high integrated water vapour from the South Pacific Ocean leads to the development of mixed-phase clouds with high liquid and ice water paths
	- These clouds reduce radiative cooling - decreases meltwater refreezing [[Van Tricht et al., 2016]] https://doi.org/10.1038%2Fncomms10266
- Rainfall brought by ARs (e.g., towards the Amundsen Sea coast) intensifies surface melting through decreasing surface albedo 
- If AR is perpendincular to coastal mountain ranges near the Amundsen Sea coast, föhn winds (RAHHHH föhn winds MENTIONED) can enhance surace melting through isentropic drawdown in the leeward regions of Marie Byrd Land and Edward VII Peninsula [[Zou et al., 2019]] https://doi.org/10.1002%2Fqj.3460
- After AR dissipation, clouds and residual precipitation water can continue to cause surface melt for around 5 days
- E.g., observed during Jan 2016 AR over Ross ice Shelf and Marie Byrd Land [[Nicholas et al., 2017]] https://doi.org/10.1038%2Fncomms15799
- No relation between integrated vapour transport (which is used to determine the magnitude of the AR) and the magnitude of 2 m temperature anomalies
	- Thus, beyond the 2m temperature anomaly crossing the threshold for AR detection, their is no further affect on magnitude -> *interesting?*

## Winter surface melting

- Summer melting dominates
- However, winter melting events on the AP account for 25% of the annual surface melt due to local mesoscale climatic features [[Kuipers Muneke et al., 2018]] https://doi.org/10.1029%2F2018GL077899
- Wilkins Coast: 90-100% of winter melt days and magnitude are linked with AR activity
- Other regions e.g., (Larsen B, C, Bach, George VI) in the range of 40-80%
- Similar mechanisms to summer surface melting

## Implications of AR behaviour changes

- ARs are a critical factor in surface melting on major ice shelves of WA
- Currently, impact of ARs is limited as major melt events only occur a couple times per decade on the Ross Ice Shelf and Marie Byrd Land.
- Continued warming and an increase in activity could lead to an increased melt frequency
- Vulnerable ice shelves on the AP already have highly saturated firn layers and thus an increased surface melt could dramatically enhance ponding, resulting in hydrofracturing [[Alley et al., 2018]] https://doi.org/10.1016%2Fj.rse.2018.03.025
- Already two ice shelves, Larsen B and Wilkins have collapsed followed by AR-related melt.
