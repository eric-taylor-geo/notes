#  [[Benn and Evans, 2010]] Chapter 12

Introduction
-	When viewed at the scale of entire glaciated basins, the characteristics and distribution of glacial sediments and landforms can reveal shifting patterns and processes of glaciation over complete glacial cycles – these can complement modern, short-term studies of present-day glacial environments.
-	Reconstructions of past glacial environments are only as good as the observations and assumptions that go into them.
-	Inappropriate interpretation can result in misleading conclusions.
-	As our understanding of glacial dynamics improves, this problem lessens – but still room for improvement.
-	Ice sheet records are being linked with regional and global climatic and oceanographic changes to provide clearer impressions of long and short term oscillations in ice masses and their sediment-landform signatures.
12.2.2 Ingredients for palaeoglaciological reconstruction
-	Reconstruction requires a wide range of complimentary information:
-	Extent, thickness, flow patterns of ice, changes over time.
-	It is therefore both a temporal and spatial problem that integrates glacial geomorphology, glaciology, and geochronology.
-	Klemen et al. (2006) (and other papers) identify several key ingredients for effective palaeoglaciological reconstruction:
-	Ice sheet/glacier footprint
-	Margins
-	Thickness
-	Chronology of events
-	Task of reconstruction is further complicated by the time-transgressive nature of ice sheet growth and decay 
-	Superimposition and partial-to-total reworking of the evidence of previous events.
-	Creates a palimpsestuous landscape which must be interpreted (Kleman, 1992, 1994)
-	“Each new generation of the landscape inherits the topography, substrate type, drainage characteristics and other factors established by the previous generation, so that the older landscape components act as important constraints on subsequent landscape evolution” (p. 583)
-	Consistent protocols set out by palaeoglaciologists – protocols stem from the ‘inversion’ concept – whereby genetic models are applied to glacial landform-sediment assemblages to determine the form and dynamics of the ice sheet responsible for their production.
-	Need to develop holistic conceptual models for the genesis of landform-sediment assemblages.
12.2.3 Landsystems and process-form models

-	Landsystems are “strictly defined as areas of common terrain attributes, different from those of adjacent areas, in which recurring patterns of topography, soils and vegetation reflect the underlying geology, past erosional and depositional processes, and climate” (p. 584)
-	Hierarchy of scale:
-	Individual landforms
-	Land facets 
-	Landsystems
12.4.5 Ice Sheet Drainage
-	Former ice sheet beds retain imprints of subglacial hydrologic processes, although this record is fragmentary, time-transgressive, and sometimes difficult to interpret.
