# Processes at the margins of supraglacial debris cover: Quantifying dirty ice ablation and debris redistribution

- "Ablation of partially debris-covered ice (dirty ice) is thought to be higher than ablation of both clean and completely debris-covered ice (Reid and Brock, 2010; Evatt et al., 2015). On debris-covered glaciers, dirty ice areas exist up-glacier from the margin of the continuous debris cover, where the ablation rates are some of the highest found across the glacier ([[Fyffe et al., 2014]]). "
- "The increase in the spatial extent of supraglacial debris covers, which typically operates over decadal timescales (Kirkbride and Warren, 1999; Stokes et al., 2007; Bolch et al., 2008; Bhambri et al., 2011; Thakuri et al., 2014) occurs by the up-glacier migration of the margin of the continuous cover, so understanding melting processes here is crucial to be able to predict future changes in debris cover and glacier mass balance"
- "Robust predictions of glacier mass balance are imperative for accurate forecasting of future glacier runoff that considers the combined influence of climate change and changing supraglacial debris cover."
- Challenge is to determine future changes to cover extent and thickness - governed by complex relationships between debris supply, debris thickness, sub-debris melt, surface topography and glacier dynamics 
- "Existing studies of debris cover formation are theoretical or restricted to the glacier centre line ([[Anderson, 2000]];[[ Kirkbride and Deline, 2013]]; Anderson and Anderson, 2016; Wirbel et al., 2018)"
- Study uses repeat high-resolution UAV imagry of a dirty ice region of Miage Glacier 

1. What is the influence of percentage debris cover, albedo, clast size and clast thickness on dirty ice ablation rates, and how do these variables interact?
2. How can clast thickness and albedo be determined from UAS outputs to facilitate modelling of dirty ice ablation?
3. What are the processes that control debris supply and remobilization in dirty ice areas

- Calculate ablation by differencing repositioned and vertically corrected DEMs using 2.5D differencing
	- Correct for down-valley glacier flow and ice emergence velocity

### Debris supply and remobilisation

- observed patterns of debris being redistributed into hollows and stream channels, increasing localised debris cover
- Clast velocity in the dirty ice area is twice that of the debris covered area (with a larger standard deivaiton too) 
	- Clasts move by a 'topple-walk mechanism'
	- Clasts retard ablation beneath them, resulting in them building an ice pedestal as surface lowering occurs around them. 
	- They then roll down and the process repeats
	- Larger clasts form larger pedestals, resulting in longer travel distances

### Ice surface and debris cover evolution 

- "Primary debris supply to the dirty ice area is from the melt-out of crevasse fills and dispersed englacial debris"
- "Debris released from crevasse traces is rapidly remobilized by melt and rainwater and concentrated into hollows and stream"
- "Overall, there was a net gain of debris along the moraine/dirty ice margins, but locally, removal of debris also occurs."

- In models of debris cover evolution (e.g., [[Anderson, 2000]] [[Rowan et al., 2015]] [[Anderson and Anderson, 2016]]), the debris accumulation rate at the surface is determined from the ablation rate and englacial debris concentration
	- However, these don't consider debris dedistribution
