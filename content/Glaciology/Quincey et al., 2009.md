#  Quantification of Everest region glacier velocities between 1992 and 2002, using satellite radar interferometry and feature tracking

- Some mention of Rongbuk in here - but as per, not much  

## Abstract

- Many glacier snouts in HMA are stagnant and have low surface gradients which are conducive to the formation of glacial lakes, dammed by debris or mraine
- Study uses interferometry and feature-tracking to quantify the extent of stagnation in 20 glaciers across the Everest region.
	- Allows for the relationship between topography and dynamics to be examined
- n = 20
- 12 show evidence for flow but with long, stagnant tongues, indicative of recession and decay
- Stagnant snouts are characterised by low <2º surface angles 
- Technique:
	- Satellite Radar Interferometry (SRI)
	- Satellite Radar Feature-Tracking (SRFT)

- Authors classify glaciers into three groups:
1. Type 1 - displacement across the whole glacier surface
2. Type 2 - displacement but only in the upper areas of the debris-covered tongue or in the clean-ice areas
3. Type 3 - no detectable activity across the surface

	- Evidence for progressive decreases in surface velocities [[Seko et al., 1998]] [[Nuimura et al., 2011]]

- Most likely cause of stagnation is the reduction in ice thickness and surface gradient.

## Discussion
- During periods of negative mass balance, therefore, mass loss is less near the terminus than higher up the ablation zone, resulting in reduction of the glacier surface gradient, ice stagnation and surface down-wasting (Reference ReynoldsReynolds, 2000; Reference Bolch, Buchroithner, Pieczonka and KunertBolch and others, 2008b; Reference Scherler, Leprince and StreckerScherler and others, 2008).

**Topographic profile shape as a proxy for glacier health**

- Data collected from the glaciers within this study suggest that the centre-line profile can indicate the health of a glacier.
- Dynamic glaciers (e.g., Kangshung Glacier) are characterised by a convex profile indicative of a large throughput of mass to the debris-covered tongue from higher elevation areas
- During periods of negative mass balance, the mass loss is greatest a a point several km up-glacier of the terminus [[Kadota et al., 2000]], thus reducing the overall surface gradient, causing it to adopt a concave form
	- In extreme cases, a reverse gradient can be observed
## Conclusions

- Little spatial variability in glacier flow (with the exception of Kangshung and Ngozumpa tributary glaciers)
- 19/20 glaciers were either stagnant or approaching stagnation across large proportions of their debris-covered areas.
- Large topographic control on glacier flow - glaciers fed by the highest and widest accumulation areas reach the furthest down-valley
- Glaciers in the Everest region are in poor health - "low flow at high elevations, and long, stagnant, debris-covered tongues at lower elevations"
