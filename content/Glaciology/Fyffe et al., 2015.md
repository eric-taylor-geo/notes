# An investigation of the influence of supraglacial debris on glacier-hydrology

**Introduction**:

-	Ice ablation has a non-linear relationship to the thickness of overlaying debris – relationship is determined by the thermal and radiative properties of debris.
-	Dominant effect of debris cover is reduced ice melt – hourly energy balance modelling suggests that debris causes the diurnal melt signal to be muted (Fyffe et al., 2014)
-	Dye-tracing studies on debris-free temperate glaciers have shown an evolution of subglacial drainage systems, displaying increased efficiency over time – due to increased in volume of melt water as a result f the retreat of the seasonal snow line (Nienow 1993)
-	Study aims to:
-	Understand the influence of debris cover on the diurnal amplitude and magnitude of surface meltwater input into the glacial drainage system.
-	Determine the seasonal evolution of the englacial and subglacial hydrological system and how this relates to the spatial distribution of supraglacial debris.
-	Field data was collected over two ablation seasons at Miage Glacier
-	5th June – 13th September 2010
-	4th June – 16th September 2011

**Findings:**

-	The formation of a channelised network does not follow the retreat of the snowline during the melt line – differs to clean glaciers.
-	The upper ablation zone which only consists of party debris cover displays high surface melt rates and is connected to an efficient channelised drainage network.
-	The lower, continuously debris-covered area is drained via an inefficient system which may gradually feed into the main channelised network.
-	Occasionally, streams drain into the main channelised system.
