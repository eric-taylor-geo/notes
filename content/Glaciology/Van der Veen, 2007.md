# Fracture propagation as a means of rapidly transferring surface meltwater to the base of glaciers

- The location of measurements reported by [[Zwally et al., 2002]] have an ice thickness of 1 km and sub-freezing temperatures over most of the thermal profile of the ice sheet.
- Thus, any drainage connection between the surface and glacier bed to cause increases in ice motion must establish itself sufficiently quickly so that meltwater does not refreeze.
- Usual mechanisms through which drainage conduits develop [[Röthlisberger, 1972]] [[Shreve, 1972]] are likely to be too slow to meet this requirement - the propagation of water-filled fractures appear to be a viable possibility 

- John Evans Glacier:
	- Mostly cold-based in Arctic Canada
	- Observations suggest that hydrologically-driven propagation of features provide a mechanism for surface meltwater to enter the subglacial drainage system [[Boon and Sharp, 2003]]
	- Water levels measured in a supraglacial stream entering the glacier suggest that multiple abrupt drainage events occurred during a week where the crevasse was water-filled or overfilled.
	- Drainage was not sustained - probably owing to the refreezing of surface meltwater exceeding the water inflow.
	- However, eventually, a full passage was connected allowing surface ponds to completely drain within the short timespan of an hour.
	- Boon and Sharp 2003 suggest that the influx of a large volume of surface water, and the warming of englacial ice through its refreezing established a permannet connection to the bed.

Mechanism:

- water is denser than ice, therefore provided that a crevasse remains water-filled, the weight of water can overcome the lithostatic stress in the ice, allowing the crevasse to reach the glacier bed (reprhase)
- [[Alley et al., 2005]], using a magma propagation model by Rubin 1995, conclude that the downward fracture through cold ice requires a large inflow to firstly maintain water pressure and to secondly offset any water loss from refreezing.

## Conclusions

- Once water-driven crevasse propagation is initiated (thus, there is a sufficient inflow of water), the growth rate is determined by the flux of water
- The ice-mechanical properties or the magnitude of the remote tensile stress are less significant factors
