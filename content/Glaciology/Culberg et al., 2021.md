# Extreme melt season ice layers reduce firn permeability across Greenland

- Surface meltwater runoff dominates present-day mass loss from Greenland Ice Sheet (roughly 50/50 between surface meltwater and calving)
	- 55% surface run off
- Porous firn can limit runoff by retaining meltwater if firn is accessible to surface meltwater
	- Firn beneath low-permeability horizons is less accessible
- 2012 extreme melt season formed a near-surface melt layer - ice lab - restricting vertical percolation
	- Using airborne radar along flight lines
	- Data collected between late March and early May
- Frequency of extreme melt seasons relative to the rate at which pore space regenerates is a key determinant of firn's multi-year response to surface melt

## Introduction 

- Uncertainty remains in how supraglacial meltwater is routed to the bed and the evolution of englacial and subglacial systems 
- In high elevation accumulation zone, surface meltwater does not flow directly to the ocean or glacier bed but rather percolates into porous near-surface snow or firn - stored in perennial firn aquifers or refreezes 
- If infiltration and localised refreezing dominates, then all firn pore space must be filled before surface runoff can occur - thereby adding a lag between the onset of surface melting and the start of runoff.
- However, perched impermeable horizons can remove access to otherwise permeable firn, resulting in runoff occuring much more rapidly
- Near-surface ice slabs can grow yearly or even be linked to a single extreme melt event - e.g., 2010, 2012 - which, due to reaching higher elevations, expands the runoff zone up-stream.

## Discussion

- Local surface mass balance regime affects how extreme melt season ice layers form
- Differing degrees of melt layer development across regions with similar mean annual temperatures and melt-to-accumulation ratios implies that melt layer formation is governed by local, short-timescale thermal and hydrologic processes as well as the mean climate state (*mean climate state is more of an envelope?*)
- Extreme melt layers form in nearly all regions of the Greenland Ice Sheet
- Frequency and intensity and length of heatwaves is likely to increase [[IPCC, 2012]] https://doi.org/10.1017/cbo9781139177245, therefore extreme melt layers will play an increasing role in ice sheet hydrology and mass balance.
- Development of ice slabs can promote lateral meltwater flow over local storage
