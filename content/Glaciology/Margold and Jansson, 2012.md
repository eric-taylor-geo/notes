# Evaluation of data sources for mapping glacial meltwater features

Abstract
-	Meltwater systems provide a useful source of information for the reconstruction of ice-retreat patterns during glaciation.
-	Meltwater features such as channels, deltas and fossil shorelines were traditionally mapped using stereo interpretation of aerial photographs.
-	evaluated different sets of satellite images and DEMs for their suitability to map glacial meltwater features (lateral meltwater channels, eskers, deltas, ice-dammed lake drainage channels and fossil shorelines)
Introduction
-	Ice sheets leave traces of their presence on the landscape in the form of glacial landforms, which can be mapped and serve as a basis for palaeoglaciological reconstructions
-	Reconstructing ice-retreat patterns is important for understanding:
-	Coupling between ice dynamics and climate response
-	Ocean circulation
-	Sea-level variations
-	Palaeo-environment
-	Palimpsest Landscapes
-	Validation of numerical models
-	Reconstructions traditionally based upon terminal and retreat moraines, glacial lineations, striae and eskers 
-	Meltwater landforms other than eskers have been largely neglected in regional geomorphological mapping – however the ability to map landforms of the meltwater system is essential for reconstructing decay patterns in regions covered by cold-based ice ([[Kleman, 1994]]) as well as for assessing meltwater fluxes.
-	Kleman , J. 1994 . Preservation of landforms under ice sheets and ice caps . Geomorphology , 9 : 19 – 32 .
-	Meltwater system consists of landforms created by water released from ice sheets and glaciers and requires the presence of meltwater for landform creation. 
-	Water is drained supraglacially, englacially, subglacially or along ice margins and can modify the landscape when in contact with terrain through its erosional impact or by accumulation of transported material.
-	The spatial distribution of landforms created by meltwater enables a retrospective reconstruction of ice configuration. Through:
-	Lateral meltwater channels delineate the positions of ice margins and the gradients of ice-slopes at particular times (Mannerfelt 1949, Dyke 1993)
-	Fossil shorelines and spillways document the existence of former ice-dammed lakes (Jansson, 2003)
-	Eskers are assumed to form close to the ice margin (Hebrand and Åmark 1989; Mäkinen 2003) and have orientations parallel to the ice-flow direction (Hoppe 1961, Brennand 2000)
-	An interpretation of this landform record therefore allows for a detailed reconstruction of ice dynamics during ice retreat – again, requires knowledge of landform formation.

-	Mapping from large-scale topographic maps and aerial photographs is very time consuming and is therefore restricted to local scale. 
-	The onset of satellite imagery and DEMs in palaeoglaciolgical mapping has allowed for larger scale reconstructions of palaeo-ice ([[Boulton and Clark, 1990]])
-	Boulton , G.S. and Clark , C.D. 1990 . A highly mobile Laurentide ice sheet revealed by satellite images of glacial lineations . Nature , 346 : 813 – 817 .
-	However… meltwater features difficult to recognise due to their uneven appearance and size being smaller than the spatial resolution. 
-	But further developments in DEMs with higher resolution have allowed meltwater landforms to be included in palaeoglaciological reconstructions at a regional scale (Clark et al. 2000, Boulton et al. 2001, [[Greenwood et al., 2007]], Evans et al. 2008, [[Stokes et al., 2009]]).
…. Comparison of remote classification in Scotland, Finland, Sweden
-	Finds that success of mapping meltwater morphology from DEMs is primarily a question of the spatial resolution of the data.
-	Possible bias in data:
-	From illumination of shaded relief images generated from DEMs under certain sun angles – Smith and Clark (2005).
-	However, these biases relate more to straight features, whilst features of uneven appearance (e.g., meltwater channels) will be less sensitive to the sun azimuth.
-	Clark (1997): 
-	success of mapping from satellite imagery depends not only on the spatial resolution of the images used but also on the sun angle at the time of image registration
-	Low sun angles cast more distinct shadows and can accentuate the topography in the image
-	Vegetation cover can supress topography – modification of the landscape in the Holocene.
Conclusions
-	In addition to the important aspect of the date and time of satellite imagery acquisition, it is the spatial resolution of the data that determines the quality of the results in mapping glacial meltwater features
-	Aerial photographs are the most reliable source.
-	Satellite with a resolution of at least 15m have some credibility, especially when combined with DEMs of a high spatial resolution.
-	Mapping from satellite data and DEMs has advantages over aerial photographs for it is more efficient, allowing for research across greater regions.
-	Recommends using SPOT 5 images.
