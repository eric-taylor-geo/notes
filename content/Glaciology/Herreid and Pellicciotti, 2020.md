# The state of rock debris covering Earth’s glaciers


**Abstract**

- Using Landsat and RGI – 7.3% of mountain glacier areas are debris covered – half of which is concentrated in three regions:
	- Alaska (38.6%)
	- Southwest Asia (12.6%)
	- Greenland (12.0%)
- Debris is present on 44% of glaciers
- Using their constructed dataset in global models will improve melt estimates for 10.6% of the global glacier domain.

**Main**

- Supraglacial debris inhibits subdebris melt as debris thickness varies from 2cm to several meters thick ([[Østrem, 1959]]; Mattson, 1993; Evatt _et al_., 2015)
- Debris cover has generally been omitted from global-scale glacier models due to its sparse global distribution ([[Hock et al., 2019]])
- The role of debris cover could show that earlier estimates of glacier-sourced eustatic sea-level rise were too high + underestimating the lifespan of glacier-sourced water sources. _Links to Himalayan hydrology and water sources_ [[Glaciology - Human]]
- Multiple studies link warming climate and the spatial expansion of debris cover [9-16]
- Authors highlight three unknowns:
	1. Spatial distribution of debris
	2. Thickness
	3. 3D evolution of debris

- There are limited studies which attempt to quantify global scale debris ([[Shukla et al., 2017]]; [[Scherler et al., 2018]])
- Authors critique these for relying on fully automated techniques and lacking in agreement with techniques.
- Authors, therefore, avoid the big data approach and use a semi-automated method for select satellite images with iterative and manual steps to mitigate error sources – e.g., inaccurate / outdated glacier outlines (_a limitation admitted in Scheler)_, cloud cover, and topographic shading.
- This attempt addresses two of the unknowns:
	1. Spatial distribution
	2. 2D evolution
	- Thus, missing thickness – and henceforth, 3D evolution) – _thickness can be addressed using energy balance models (!)_

**Global Distribution of Debris Cover**

- The colour contrast between rocks, glacier ice, firn, and snow in multispectral satellite imagery allows for robust classification – provided that the glacier map / outline is accurate and that imagery is corrected for season snow and cloud cover.
- 2014 release of the RGI allowed for debris cover to be mapped globally using this method ([[Scherler et al., 2018]])
- A preliminary investigation conducted by the authors  found that the RGI quality was insufficient to map debris cover.
- Authors thus excluded Antarctica and all glaciers with a surface area of <1 km2 (_note that Scherler et al. remarked that glaciers under 1 km2 had the greatest uncertainty and sensitivity to threshold values in RATIO, NSDI etc.)_
- Authors then manually (_damn_) classified true debris cover and glacier-classified land that is not glacier (an RGI false positive).
- RGI false negatives were manually added.
- FP and FN errors added to 11,483 km2 (3.3%) of glacier area.
- Incorporating these areas showed that 7.3% of Earth’s mountain glacier area is covered by rock debris -> _significantly higher than_ _Scherler et al.’s calculation of 4.4%_
- Study finds “systematic undercounting” of debris cover in Scherler _et al_. (2018)
- 44% of glaciers with a surface area >2km2 have some quantity of debris cover
- When only looking at debris covered glaciers – the % of debris-covered area is 14%.
- Further constrained to only the ablation area, this increases to 36%.

**Latitude / Debris Cover relationship:**

- Inversely exponential relationship between distance from the equator and regional percentage of debris-covered glacier area.
- “Warmer climatic zones are more dynamic and conducive to supraglacial debris production”
- However, orographic factors (range age, relief, lithology) may be able to offset a region from this relation – e.g., Alaska.

**Debris-cover state**

- Various debris-cover metrics were calculated
- Common in the literature is that of ‘percentage debris covered’ and the ‘accumulation zone area ratio (AAR)” [24]
- Authors also calculate 4 new metrics:
- Stage
	- (amount of ablation zone covered in debris – stage 1 means that 100% of the ablation zone is covered in debris. Thus, the only way for the 2D debris-cover carrying capacity of the glacier to increase is with the up-glacier migration of the equilibrium line – i.e., the ablation zone increases in size. _This surely can therefore also increase in size from the advance of the glacier, thereby resulting in more of the glacier being below the equilibrium line (assuming the El stays at the same elevation, or moves up-glacier)_
- Debris expansion potential
	- The length of the debris cover-bare ice boundary divided by the perimeter of the debris cover
- Future debris expansion
- Moraine abundance
