# [[Favier et al., 2014]] Retreat of Pine Island Glacier controlled by marine ice-sheet instability


- Steep retrograde of Pine Island Glacier base [[Vaughan et al., 2006]]
- Assessing marine ice-sheet instability two questions must be answered:
	- To what extent are dynamic responses of ice shelves dictated by the bedrock topography rather than the type and amplitude of perturbation
	- Can the grounding line be stabilised on the retrograde slope?
- Confidence in answers is affected by the accuracy of models and estimates of constrained parameters

## Results

- Retreat of PIG is firmly attributed to the acceleration of the glacier in response to sub-ice-shelf melting
- No models produce a steady grounding line on the reverse slope
- Retreat could continue even if the original trigger is removed 
	- However, this could limit retreat and the imbalance that persists once the bottom of the trench is reached
# [[Gudmundsson et al., 2012]] The stability of grounding lines on retrograde slopes

- Understanding of the WAIS is crucial for quantifying estimates of future SLR
- Struggles with modelling - calculated grounding line positions are strongly dependent on grid resolution [[Vieli and Payne, 2005]]
- Shallow-ice approximation models do not allow for the correct description of grounding-line motion

## Model

- models in one horizontal dimension show that rapidly sliding marine-based ice sheets are subject to MISI
- However, this is less clear for two horizontal dimensions - paper seeks to demonstrate that MISI dos not hold unconditionally in 2HD
- Uses two models: three dimensional full stokes model and a vertically integrated model - (Shallow shelf approximation) SSA

## Discussion

- MISI idea rests on ice flux at the grounding line being a monotonically increasing function of ice thickness at the grounding line
- If this holds, then steady-state grounding-line positions of marine-type ice sheets on retrograde slopes are unstable [[Weertman, 1974]]
- [[Schoof, 2007b]] demonstrates that ice flux can be expressed as a function of the ice thickness and the longitudinal stress - ice flux is an increasing function of both of these variables.
- in 1HD, longitudinal stress is also an increasing function of thickness and thus ice flux is always an increasing function of the grounding line thickness.
- Whilst this holds in 1HD, modelling by the authors shows that in 2HD it is possible to have ice configurations where longitudinal stresses decrease with thickness which causes flux to be a decreasing function of cie thickness. In these scenarios, MISI is not expected to operate and it is possible to have a stable grounding-line on a retrograde slope.
- However, whilst possible theoretically, no such stable grounding line has been identified.
- Model also only looks at the stability of ice sheets with respect to the retrograde slope, and does not consider external forcing.

## Conclusions

- study provides specific numerical examples for model geometries where stable grounding lines are located on retrograde slopes.
- Marine ice sheets on such slopes are not inherently unstable.
- Confidence in these findings is robust owing to results agreeing from two different numerical models - full stokes and shallow shelf approximation using vertical integration

# [[Jamieson et al., 2012]] Ice-stream stability on a reverse bed slope

- Present understanding of ice-stream stability is limited by observational records that are too short to resolve muli-decadal to millennial-scale behaviour in order to validate numerical models
- Study presents a dynamic numerical simulation of Antarctic ice-stream retreat since the LGM, constrained by geophysical data - numerical ice-stream model
- Finds that the retreat of the Marguerite Bay Ice Stream was highly nonlinear - retreat was interrupted by stabilisation on a retrograde bed - whereas theory would suggest that rapid unstable retreat under these conditions [[Weertman, 1974]] [[Schoof, 2007b]]
- Transient stabilisations were caused by enhanced lateral drag as the ice stream narrowed - thus understanding ice stream width is crucial for understanding decadal- to centennial-scale ice-stream behaviour and marine ice-sheet vulnerability - time scales relevant to future forecasts for SLR over the next century

# [[Joughin et al., 2014]] Marine Ice Sheet Collapse Potentially Under Way for the Thwaites Glacier Basin, West Antarctica

- Uses numerical model to investigate the sensitivity of the Thwaites Glacier to ocean melt and whether unstable retreat is already underway
## Introduction

- Thwaites Glacier has been identified as being potentially unstable since [[Hughes, 1981]]
- The Amundsen Coast of Antarctica is producing the majority of Antarctica's contribution to SLR [[Rignot, 2008]] [[Shepherd et al., 2012]]
- Thinning owing to the increase presence of warm warming water in the Circumpolar Deep Water on the adjacent continental shelf [[Jacobs et al., 2011]] 
- This plays a role in melting and thinning the ice shelves, buttressing the ice sheet 
- Buttressing effect: ice sheets constrain flow from the interior
- Their melt enhances the flow to the ocean, contributing to feedbacks which increase ice discharge 
- Ice discharge is non-linearly proportional to grounding-line thickness.
- Potential for instability exists when the ice-sheed bed lies below sea level and the ice sheet increases in depth toward the interior.

- Thwaites grounding line lies on coastal sill 600m below sea level and a further 60-80 km inland the coastal sill quickly gives way to a deep marine basin (>1200 bsl)
## Model

- Study uses a basin-scale ice-flow model to evaluate whether unstable collapse is already underway or if the retreat is limited by stabilising factors
- 'prognostic, finite-element, depth-averaged, shallow-shelf model'
- Model initial conditions are the basal shear stresses and ice-shelf rheological factors that best match 1996 observed velocities [[Joughin et al., 2009]]
- Investigate several scenarios to determine sensitivity:
	- direct influence of melt on the grounding-line position with no feedback or response from the glacier - velocity was fixed to its initial value. Found that the grounding-line is relatively insensitive to the direct effect of melting.
		- Retreat is largely driven by the non-steady state fixed velocity imposed at the start of the simulation
	- Adding coupling with the velocity and melt finds that there is much greater sensitivity to melt - the grounding line approaches the deepest parts of the trough for higher-melt simulations
	- Antarctica is projected to receive greater accumulation rates -> 20% linear increase in accumulation rate over the first 100 years
		- In higher melt scenarios - the increased accumulation delays the transition to large losses by less than a decade -> thus, little impact and protection
	- Model simulations not coupled with a GCM
	- Model also does not consider ocean-driven met that extends immediately upstream of the grounding line - this could also accelerate melt 

# [[Ritz et al., 2015]] Potential sea-level rise from Antarctic ice-sheet instability constrained by observations

## Main

- Grounding-line retreat may occur at a rate independent of the original trigger and may continue if the trigger is removed [[Favier et al., 2014]]
- Ice shelf buttressing and certain configurations of bedrock topography can limit MISI [[Schoof, 2007b]] [[Gudmundsson et al., 2012]]

- Uses a numerical ice-sheet model to represent the ocean and atmospheric drivers of MISI and the response of ice shelves
- Three configurations for basal friction and sliding - linear-viscous, nonlinear Weertman, plastic flow.
- Generates 3,000 sampling MISI onset dates and weighs them using Bayesian statistical framework to assess uncertainties in the model.
- Sensitivity of grounding line migration to friction law has been explored previously (2,13,14) - full Bayesian approach allows for the quantification of the probabilisitc contribution to uncertainty in SLR.

## Findings

- Spatial patterns of the probability of ungrounding show how local bed elevation, slope, and friction modulate the response to MISI onset.
- Amundsun Sea embayment (ASE) has the highest probability of ungrounding and SLR contribution - due to its topography (downsloping bedrock below sea level) and low friction
- Thwaites region's ungrounding is rapid - but this is also due to substantial thinning of the Thwaites upstream of the grounding line.
- Peninsula and Marie Byrd Land hardly respond due to their bedrock being largely above sea level.
- Antarctica will contribute 30 cm SLR equivalent by 2100 (95% confidence) - ASE dominates.

- MISI in the ASE could drive large and rapid sea-level rise - but the total Antarctic contribution is moderated by physical constraints.
- Limitations on unknown basal friction law
- Upper-bound estimates from low-resolution models are implausible based on the current understanding of physical mechanisms and potential triggers. 
	- *Therefore, highlighting the importance of higher resolution modelling for future assessments of the stability of the Antarctic Ice Sheet*
# [[Schoof, 2007b]] Ice Sheet Grounding Line Dynamics: steady states, stability, and hysteresis

- marine ice sheet instability hypothesis [[Weertman, 1974]]asserts that ice discharge through the grounding line should increase with ice thickness there. Suppose then that a steady marine ice sheet has its grounding line located on an upward sloping bed. A slight retreat in grounding line position will lead to an increase in ice thickness and hence ice discharge at the grounding line. This represents a positive feedback: the increase in ice discharge should lead to a further shrinkage in the ice sheet and hence grounding line retreat, leading to a further increase in ice discharge and so on
- Based on this hypothesis, grounding lines on retrograde slopes cannot be stable
- This is highly relevant to West Antarctica - bathymetry
- Suggests that the ice sheet is not stable and is unlikey to be in a steady state

- Evidence for a much larger ice sheet preset over the Ross and Ronne-Filcher embayments during the LGG, extending to the shallow Antarctic continental shelf edge [[Stone et al., 2003]]
- Instability provides possible explanations for Heinrich events during the last glacial period [[Clarke et al., 1999]]

- Study based on the boundary layer theory [[Schoof, 2007a]]
- Confirms the hypothesis proposed by [[Weertman, 1974]]
- Viable steady state profiles for two-dimensional ice sheets must have their grounding lines located on prograde beds
- Model is limited by only describing a two-dimensional marine ice sheet - does not include buttressing which could stabilise grounding lines on retrograde bed
	- removal of ice shelf can increase ice discharge - this was observed in the rapid collapse of the Larsen B ice shelf [[Rignot et al., 2004]] *and [[Scambos et al., 2004]]*
- Highlights requirement for further research into addressing the three-dimensional effects in marine ice sheet dynamics - ice shelf buttressing and ice stream formation
- 
## Hysteresis

- Marine ice sheets can undergo hysteresis when material parameters (e.g., ice viscosity, basal slipperiness) or external forcings (accumulation, sea level) are adjusted
- This mechanism is unique to marine ice sheets and requires the presence of an overdeepening.
- This mechanism could explain the dissapearence of a large ice sheet in the Ross embayment since the LGM and could cause Heinrich events [[Clarke et al., 1999]]
- This mechanism also works the other way - ice sheets with prograde profiles could be forced to advance across the overweening if accumulation rates increase, sea levels drop, or when mean ice temperature drops or basal slipperiness decreases.

# [[Sergienko and Wingham, 2019]] Grounding line stability in a regime of low driving and basal stresses

- [[Weertman, 1974]] was the first to provide theoretical descriptions of the stability of marine-based ice sheets - 'stable ice sheet can occur if the bed slopes away from the center of the ice sheet'
- [[Schoof, 2007b]] developed a boundary layer theory for an ice stream undergoing rapid sliding at its grounding line - ice flux at the grounding line is a monotonically increasing function of ice thickness at the grounding line.
- It, therefore, follows through that ice sheets sitting on a retrograde bed is inherently unstable [[Schoof, 2012]] - refered to as the MISI hypothesis

- Studies have considered two distinct types of flow on grounded ice:
	- Those dominated by vertical shear e.g., [[Weertman, 1974]]
	- Those dominated by basal sliding e.g., [[Schoof, 2007b]]
- Assumptions that basal stress is roughly equal to driving stress and at the grounding line, the longitudinal stress must be balanced with hydrostatic pressure [[Schoof, 2007b]]
- Based on these assumptions and balances, [[Schoof, 2007b]] concluded that the ice thickness at the grounding line must be small relative to the ice thickness in the interior - thus the ice thickness gradient must be large near the grounding line
- However, observations display low surface gradients [[MacAyeal et al., 1995]] on present-day West Antarcrtic ice streams - eg., those flowing to the Ross Ice Shelf
- This is perhaps due to the weak beds beneath ice streams [[MacAyeal, 1989]]

## Discussion and conclusions

- Study focuses on the Siple Coast which has a low surface gradient which implies a low driving stress - tanα is low
- Model finds that low surface slopes are a direct result of low or absent basal shear and that the surface slow increases in response to the magnitude of basal shear. 
- Thus, observed low surface slopes on the Siple Coast are indicative of a low basal shear based on this model's findings.
- The study highlights the importance of accurate bed elevation measurements - low amplitude bed undulations can significantly increase form drag, but they cannot currently be resolved due to being much smaller than a single pixel.

- Finds that in low driving and basal stress regime, unconfined marine ice sheets do not conform exactly to the marine ice sheet instability hypothesis 
- Rather, (in)stability of the grounding line is determined by a combination of form drag, basal shear, the accumulation rate, and their gradients.
- Thus, bed shape alone is not a sufficient indicator of marine ice sheet instability.

# [[Edwards et al., 2019]] Revisiting Antarctic ice loss due to marine ice-cliff instability

- Antarctic contribution to SLR has two parts:
	- Increasing snowfall - reduce SLr
	- Ice discharge into the ocean - uncertain
- Marine ice sheets are vulnerable to two hypothesised positive feedback mechanisms which could have caused the past collapse of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet [[Vaughan, 2008]]
- MISI
	- Evidence that MISI is underway in West Antarctica [[Rignot et al., 2014]] [[Favier et al., 2014]] [[Joughin et al., 2014]]
	- Driven by warm circumpolar deep water breaching the continental shelf - though it is unclear whether this is specifically been affected by anthropogenic activity [[Previdi and Polvani, 2014]]
- Marine Ice-Cliff instability (MICI) - self-sustaining retreat of the ice front in regions where the ice is 100 m or more above the ocean surface - triggered by ice shelf collapse [[Bassis and Walker, 2012]] - yield strength of ice
	- Tall cliffs are structurally unstable, and their collapse could expose greater cliffs - providing a positive mechanism for instability - *especially with ice thickness increasing upstream. Though this is specifically ice thickness **above** water*
	- Observational evidence for MICI is indirect and is based on the absence of ice cliffs taller than 100 m and the rapid retreat of the fronts of the Jajobshavn (Greenland) and Crane (Antarctic Peninsula) glaciers -> *[[Needell and Holschuh, 2023]]*

- Global SLR estimates use MICI - however MICI has not been directly observed in the modern era - unclear whether it is required to recreate past SL fluctuations
- Concludes that previous interpretations of MICI projections over-estimate SLR in this century - MICI hypothesis is not constrained enough
