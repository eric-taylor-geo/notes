# Decadal slowdown of a land-terminating sector of the Greenland Ice Sheet despite warming

## Abstract

- Despite a 50% increase in surface meltwater production in 2007-14 compared to 1985-94, annual ice motion was 12% slower.
- GPS and satellite for velocity measurments

## Introduction

- Varied response to fluctuating inputs of surface meltwater to the bed of Greenland Ice Sheet [[Zwally et al., 2002]] [[Sole et al., 2013]]
- Greenland Ice Sheet is losing mass at an accelerated rate [[Shepherd et al., 2012]] [[Enderlin et al., 2014]]
	- Increased surface melting [[Enderlin et al., 2014]] and enhanced ice discharge from marine-terminated glaciers [[Hanna et al., 2013]]
- Acceleration of marine-terminating glaciers is driven primarily by processes at the ice-ocean interface
- However, atmospheric forcing can change ice motion at both land- and marine-terminating glaciers through the delivery of surface melt-water to the ice-sheet bed [[Zwally et al., 2002]] [[Howat et al., 2010]]
- Rapid increases in meltwater increase water pressure at the base, enhancing basal sliding [[Bartholomew et al., 2011]]
- However, the capacity of the subglacial drainage system responds and negative feedback loops lower water pressure and reduce basal sliding as the system becomes channelised, enhancing flux efficiency [[Röthlisberger, 1972]] 

## Results

- Clear regional slowdown 
- Slowdown believed to be as a result of the sustained high production of surface meltwater owing to climate warming in the region.
- Expansion of a channelised subglacial drainage system [[Cowton et al., 2013]]
- 17-33% of slowdown can be explained by reduced internal deformation as a result of ice-thinning 
- The majority of slowdown is due to increases in subglacial drainage effeciency associated with sustained larger melt volumes.
- More research required to determine whether the observed slowdown will migrate up-stream as enhanced surface melt extends to higher elevations
