# Intense atmospheric rivers can weaken ice shelf stability at the Antarctic Peninsula

ARs are narrow long bands of enhanced moisture fluxes originating from the mid-latitudes and sub-tropics

**Rainfall**

ARs bring exceptional amounts of rainfall ([[Vignon et al., 2021]]), which can result in the rapid saturation of firn


Record melt ear along the AP ice shelves in 2019/2020 brought an end to the decreased melt trend that started in the late 1990s.
Thus, the AP ma be entering a new phase of vulnerability from ARs, putting the Larsen C ice shelf at risk.
Indeed, a giant iceberg (A68) calved from the Larsen C ice shelf between July 10-12, 2017, which was attributed to basal melting from subsurface ocean warming ([[Hogg and Gudmundsson, 2017]]).

Foën winds can flow down Antarctic Peninsula onto the Larsen Ice Shelves

Increased water vapour results in increased cloud cover which on the one hand reduces the shortwave solar radiation flux, but increases the longwave radiative flux through clouds' enhancement of the greenhouse effect.



**January 24-26th, 2008 AR**:

- Caused a disintegration and fragmentation of nearly all land-fast ice in the Larsen A and B embayments, generating 6.3 Gt of runoff, which was followed by a calving event 6 days later.
- The calving event resulted in a 11 km retreat of the eastern region of the remnant Larsen B ice shelf.

## AR impacts on processes detrimental to ice-shelf stability

- ARs result in extreme temperature, melt, runoff, and swell events.
- Rainfall events lead to periods of extremely high rates of firn saturation and runoff ([[Vignon et al., 2021]]).
- ARs create anomalously large swells which cause the clearing of sea ice along ice shelf fronts, which increases stress at the calving front, resulting in increased calving.
	- This occured on the Larsen B in 2002 ([[Massom et al., 2018]]).
- Cause a 10% decrease in the sea ice fraction

- AR landfalls prompt a state of high stress on the ice shelves through the combination of surface melt induced melt pond formation, leading to hydrofracturing and wave strain along the ice-shelf front. The tendency of ARs to create widespread melt pond formation makes them possible precursors of ice-shelf collapse via hydrofracturing cascades like what was observed on the Larsen B in 2002


- Has implications for the Larsen C - the Larsen C is the most sensitive to AR-induced temperature changes, having the greatest regional warming, likely owig to the region's topography.
- The southward migration of surface and subsurface melting conditions, coupled with an increase in AR intensity under climate change, could cause the Larsen C to become increasingly vulnerable to AR behaviour and respond similarly to the Larsen A and B ice shelves.

## Conclusions

- Strong ARs were precursors for more than 60% of the major calving events of the Larsen A and Larsen B ice shelves since 2000 and precluded their main collapses
- Relationship for Wilkins ice shelf is less clear:
	- Presence of perennial firn aquifers on the ice shelf may explain the disparity between AR occurences and claving/collapse events for aquifers can delay or reduce runoff ([[Forster et al., 2014]])
	- The topography places Wilkins on the windward side of Antarctic Peninsula mountains, meaning it is less conducive to AR-triggered foën events.
