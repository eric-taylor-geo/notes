# On the reconstruction of palae-ice sheets: recent advances and future challenges

**2.1 Formalising glacial inversion techniques using ice sheet flow-smets**

-	Subglacial processes beneath palaeo-ice sheets created and preserved landforms which are key for reconstructing their past flow patterns and extent.
-	Inverting bed imprint to extract ice sheet properties is known as ‘glacial inversion’ ([[Kleman and Borgstrom, 1996]]; [[Greenwood et al., 2007]])

-	Prevailing paradigm: mapped ice flow indicators (e.g., drumlins) form in radial patterns close to the ice margin and predominantly during deglaciation (E.g., Boulton et al. 1985) -> due to this assumption reconstructions often depict a radial pattern of flow from relatively stationary ice domes.
-	Challenged when the Laurentide Ice Sheet was recognised as a palimpsest landscape of flow patterns from different ages that were not all formed in ice marginal environments ([[Boulton and Clark, 1990]]a; Boultain and Clark, 1990b)
-	B and C (1990a; 1990b) grouped landforms into ‘flow-sets’, noting how they often cross cut each other, allowing the assignment of a relative chronology based on the principles of superimposition.
-	The identification of cross-cutting flow-sets permits detailed reconstructions of changes in ice sheet flow patterns, but flow-sets can also preserve important information with regard to the glaciological conditions that formed and preserved them
-	Kleman and Borgström (1996) highlighted the importance of the basal thermal regime and subglacial hydrology when deciphering the glacio-dynamic context of flow-set formation, and identified several different fan (flow-set) types that could be used to create a time-slice sequence of ice sheet evolution (e.g., those formed during a surge, or those formed during warm-based deglaciation)

-	Reconstructions must consider that flow-sets may pre-date the LGM and may have been protected by cold-based ice – importance of the thermal regime.
-	Clark et al. (2012) compared their reconstruction with numerical modelling of the British-Irish Ice Sheet (Boulton and Hagdorn, 2006, Hubbard et al., 2009) and found that, whilst there were broad similarities in terms of the extent and the position of ice divides, there were marked discrepancies in the timing of maximum extent and retreat phases, which they suggested may be due to the palaeo-climate forcing, interpreted from the Greenland ice core record.
-	Clark et al. (2012) highlighted the requirement for improved understandings of dynamics, in particular – calving and links between interior thinning and marginal retreat
-	Therefore highlighting one of the weaknesses of the glacial inversion method – it is limited by our understanding of the formation of various landforms.
-	Recent advances in geophysical observations of bedforms beneath existing ice sheets are furthering understanding / agreeing with palaeo-glaciological inferences.
-	E.g., King et al. (2009)’s observation of mega-scale glacial lineations beneath the West Antarctic ice sheet agree with Clark’s (1993) inference that bedform elongation is related to ice velocity.
-	Summary: new remote sensing and GIS techniques have led to advances in our ability to reconstruct changing flow patterns of palae-ice sheets.

2.4 Subglacial hydrology of ice sheets and subglacial lakes
-	Glacial geological features are useful for providing information of the ice-sheet thermal regime, and the distribution and drainage of water at the bed
-	Glacial inversion techniques regarding eskers: thought to form time transgressively within 10s of km from the retreating ice margin (Kleman et al., 1997)
-	Clark and Walder (1994) showed that eskers from the LIS were concentrated over the impermeable crystalline bedrock of the Canadian Shield, where subglacial meltwater was more likely to carve R-channels into the base of the ice (due to hard rheology) -> softer basal geologies more likely to drain meltwater, carving shallower channels.
-	[[Storrar et al., 2014]] demonstrate that esker formation increased during the deglaciation of the LIS across the Canadian Shield – coincident with increased rates of ice margin retreat.
-	Mimicks the seasonal evolution of drainage systems in smaller valley glaciers, implying that hydrological evolution can occur over millennial time-scales (e.g., Neinow Haut Glacier D’Arolla paper) - [[Nienow et al., 1998]]
Role of subglacial lakes:
-	Considered to be an active component on the subglacial hydrological system.
-	Fill, drain, exert influence on ice dynamics
-	Subglacial lakes can drain rapidly towards an ice margin (e.g., as a jökulhlaup) – can be on sub-annual timescales (Smith et al., 2009)
-	Use of gravel-boulder outwash fans at the mouth of tunnel valleys in North America have been related to high magnitude subglacial meltwater outburst evens (Culter et al., 2002)
-	Further reading: Shaw, 2002, Shoemaker, 1991;1999)
‘In summary, meltwater landforms are, perhaps, under-used in terms of understanding the source, routing, and storage of meltwater associated with palaeo-ice sheets. Eskers provide important information about subglacial drainage patterns at the ice sheet scale and how it evolves through time, but meltwater channels carved into bedrock are more enigmatic. Numerical ice sheet models have proved an important tool for identifying the potential location of subglacial lakes, but there have been few attempts to integrate a palaeo-glaciological understanding of subglacial hydrology into numerical modelling of ice sheets.’
3.1 Cosmogenic Dating
-	Ideal sample: no exposure to cosmic rays prior to glaciation (has no inheritance) and has full exposure to cosmic rays (no shielding) after deglaciation (Heyman et al., 2011)
-	Unlikely for both of these criteria to be met
-	If exposed prior to glaciation + experienced limited erosion whilst covered: will produce an older age estimate
-	Samples demonstrate that failures to meet these conditions is common (Heyman et al, 2011)
-	Interpretation strategies;
-	Scatted exposure can be interpreted as the result of prior exposure – therefore youngest exposure age is deemed closest to ‘true’
-	Vice versa
-	Average set of exposure ages can be taken on the assumption that the effects of prior and incomplete exposure will cancel eachother out.
-	The different nuclide decay rates of multiple radiogenic isotopes can provide inference into the duration of exposure if nuclides accumulated during a previous period of exposure.
-	For quartz:
-	10Ba: 1.4 Ma Half-life
-	26Al 0.7 Ma Half-life
-	Helps to constrain glacial erosion and quantify the duration of ice coverage
-	NOTE: that this is TOTAL ice coverage over repeated glacial cycles – cannot show an individual coverage event.
