# Formalising an inversion methodology for reconstructing ice-sheet retreat patterns from meltwater channels: application to the British Ice Sheet 

-	Glacial meltwater channels yield important information regarding patterns of palaeo-ice sheet retreat.
-	However, it is important to distinguish between channels formed at different positions in relation to the ice mass (e.g., lateral, subglacial).
-	Authors develop an inversion methodology for extracting ice margin repeat patterns from meltwater channels.

Existing information on meltwater channels
-	Long history of investigation and mapping of glacial meltwater channels in Britain (e.g. Kendall, 1902; Raistrick,1926; Sissons, 1958; Glasseret al., 2004)
-	Sporadic coverage of mapping in Britain - + level of interpretation is inconsistent.
-	Development in understanding from advancing in water flow understanding in subglacial networks ([[Röthlisberger, 1972]])
-	Mapping and interpretation of palaeo channels has inevitably been influenced by the paradigms of the time in which the work was conducted, contributing to inconsistencies in channel interpretation by different authors.
-	Temporal and spatial interpretation affected by the inversion understandings at the time of study.
-	Meltwater channels hold valuable palaeo-glaciological information; to extract this information it is important to distinguish between different channel types
-	Lateral channels used to indicate margins of cold-based ice masses 
-	Subglacial channels approximate former equipotential surfaces and hence ice surface slope.
-	Limited by poor categorisation of channels – e.g., in BRITICE dataset, 53% of channels were not assigned a mode or origin.
Establishing diagnostic criteria for classification of meltwater channels
-	descriptions of meltwater channels report that different types of channel are characterised by distinct morphologies and relationships with topography
-	Subglacial and lateral meltwater channels as these yield the most information for reconstructing patterns of deglaciation, indicating flow directions and palaeomargin positions respectively
-	Lateral channels further subdivided into marginal and submarginal categories (on and below ice surface)

**Application of criteria to meltwater channels database**
-	Channels visually assessed according to four groups of observations:
-	Position relative to topography
-	Orientation elative to contours
-	Channel form (straight / sinuous)
-	Channel relationship to others (isolated or within a sequence / system)
-	Classification using probability categories – “definite”, “probable”, “possible” – used to build possible retreat patterns for the British-Irish ice sheet.
-	(Possible category omitted due to requiring further investigation”
-	Arrangement of marginal meltwater channels indicates successive positions of palaeomargins – frequently used to infer cold ice at the margin where meltwater cannot percolate through to englacial or subglacial conduits – it therefore is deflected along lateral ice margins (Dyke, 1993; Hattstrand, 1998).
-	Orientation of subglacial channels is governed by the disposition of the hydraulic head – approximates the direction of the steepest hydraulic gradient. Therefore, the ice flow direction close to the terminus can be indicated (?) – also indicates warm-based thermal regime to allow meltwater at the ice-bed interface.
-	Paleaomargins are reconstructed based on likely configurations with regard to topography and the position of moraines (Clark et al., 2004)

**Results and Discussion**

Classification
-	Comparison with well-supported channel interpretation shows success in meltwater channel classification meaning that channels can be appropriately classified remotely.
-	Author’s acknowledge that the best method for full analysis would be in situ, however their method still proves useful – allows for remote mapping and classification.
Reconstructed retreat patterns
-	The role of topography in ice-sheet retreat is clearly significant. Successions of lateral channels down valley flanks reflect the thinning of ice lobes during retreat. Notable examples exist on the Lammermuir Hills south of the Firth of Forth (Sissons, 1958), in the Southern Uplands (Price, 1963), and in the Yorkshire Dales (Raistrick, 1931; British Geological Survey, 1985). The varied terrain of upland Britain thus exerted a strong control upon the configuration of retreating ice margins.
-	Formalised inversion procedure permits meltwater channels to be used to derive palaeo-glaciological information.
-	Retreat patterns confirm and extend results from numerous localised studies.
Towards a palaeo-glaciology of the ice sheet
-	Geological and geomorophological records of glaciation provide only snapshots of ice activity in time and space – likely that different meltwater systems will relate to different phase of deglaciation.
-	Opposing retreat patterns are evident in the Peninnes and Vale of Eden – ice known to have flowed in both directions during the evolution of the last ice sheet (Letzer, 1987)
-	Likely that several stages of glacial activity shaped channels – once a channel exists, it is likely to be re-used by subsequent meltwater flows (Rothlisberger and Lang, 1987; Glasser et al., 2004)
-	I would assume this applies to Nye channels – for these are eroded into the glacier bed – R- and H- channels would surely disappear with the retreat of ice and not be reestablished due to them being eroded up into the glacier base.
-	Links to how glacial dynamics are constrained by the previous glacial conditions – memory.
Conclusions
-	Meltwater channels have been demonstrated to provide valuable insight into ice-sheet retreat patterns.
-	However, they can only provide information once they have been correctly classified – this paper provides a methodology for classifying the and therefore their subsequent ‘inversion’ to determine palaeo-ice-sheet retreat patterns.
-	‘The preliminary retreat pattern presented here has, however, demonstrated the value of a geomorphologically driven inversion approach to reconstruction at the scale of the ice sheet, an approach hitherto under-utilised’
