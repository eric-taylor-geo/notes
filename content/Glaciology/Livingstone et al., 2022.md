# Subglacial lakes and their changing role in a warming climate

- We report a global inventory of subglacial lakes (773 in total): 675 from Antarctica (59 newly identified here), 64 from Greenland, 6 from Iceland, 2 beneath the Devon Ice Cap and 26 from valley glaciers.
- 80% of subglacial lakes are stable, implying either closed systems or approximately balanced inflow and outflow; the remaining lakes are active and display one of five distinct activity patterns.

## Subglacial lakes and ice dynamics

- Lake drainage affects ice flow by altering basal water pressure and thus basal traction.
- The size of the effect depends on whether, and to what extent, lake discharge exceeds the hydrological capacity of the existing subglacial drainage system
- If discharge exceeds the hydrological capacity of the existing drainage system, then discharge will cause a transient increase in basal water pressure, enhancing basal sliding.
- The net long-term impact of subglacial lakes on ice velocity depends on the balance of reduced motion (compared to ice motion in the absence of lakes) during lake filling, enhanced motion during lake drainage, and reduced motion following the development of efficient downstream drainage, which might in some cases go below long-term average value.
	- Fricker et al. ([[Fricker et al., 2016|2016]]) find a long-term reduction in ice motion due to filling and draining
	- Siegfried et al. ([[Siegfried et al., 2016|2016]]) find an increase.

## Future evolution of subglacial lakes

- Climate warming will enhance meltwater input via:
	- Increased surface melt input into the subglacial hydrological system
	- Increased ice flow increases frictional melting
	- Loss of buttressing ice shelves increases ice flow further.
- Ice recession produces steeper mean surface slopes which creates higher hydrological gradients at the bed which reduces the potential for hydrological minima.
- Such recession after the collapse of Crane Glacier in 2002 caused a subglacial lake drainage ([[Scambos et al., 2011]])
- As melt intensifies and expands further inland, more subglacial lakes may become coupled to surface melt, increasing the number of active lakes and the potential for catastrophic drainage events.

### Future evolution of impacts of subglacial lake drainage on ice dynamics

- Increased lake activity is likely to enhance the hydrological connectivity between lakes and their surroundings.
- Increased discharge of water over the grounding line, increasing plume-driven frontal ablation (*e.g., [[Gourmelen et al., 2025]]*)
- Lake drainage events are likely to cause transient increases in ice flow followed by a longer term decrease as a more extensive and long-lived efficient subglacial drainage system develops.
