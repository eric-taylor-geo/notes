# Reconstructing the advance and retreat dynamics of the central sector of the last Cordilleran Ice Sheet

Introduction
-	Cordilleran Ice Sheet (CIS) formed part of the NA Ice Sheet Complex during the LGM – contained an ice volume similar to that of the present day GIS
-	Numerical modelling indicates that northern British Colombia was the nucleation centre for ice sheet growth during MIS4 and 2 (Seguinot et al., 2016)
-	Few empirical constraints are available denoting the nature of ice build up and retreat in this region (Clague and Ward, 2011)
-	Cross-cutting striae provide evidence of ice flow direction progression, including complete ice flow reversals at some locations
2.1 Glacial inversion
-	Invert the spatial distribution and characteristics of glacial landforms to decipher the configuration of the ice sheet over time ([[Kleman and Borgstrom, 1996]]; [[Greenwood et al., 2007]])

-	Glacial meltwater record is key for delineating former ice marginal positions – many of the ice margins being mapped based on the orientation and direction of successive lateral and submarginal meltwater channels that dip into valleys.

-	Cross-cutting relationships used to assign a relative age to flowsets based on the principle of superposition.

2.1.2 Deglacial Dynamics
-	Use the distribution of CIS outlet glacier moraines and meltwater landforms to delineate CIS ice marginal positions during deglaciation.
-	CIS outlet glacier moraines: 
-	sharp-crested, straight or arcuate-shaped ridges of sediment that were deposited along active ice margins. These moraines are inferred to relate to CIS outlet glacier marginal positions during deglaciation based on their morphology, orientation and location proximal to other ice marginal landforms, such as kame terraces and lateral and submarginal meltwater channels (Dulfer and Margold, 2021).
-	Lateral and submarginal meltwater channels: 
-	channels formed by water flowing along the ice margin, and consequently, delineating the glacier margin at the time of formation
-	Sequences of parallel lateral meltwater channels, with many channels bending down into the valleys, can be used to determine ice surface lowering and marginal retreat through time ([[Greenwood et al., 2007]])
-	Kame terraces:
-	flat-to gently-sloping accumulations of glaciofluvial sediment deposited along lateral glacier margins, often in association with lateral and submarginal meltwater channels. Kame terraces record ice margin position and approximate ice surface elevation at the time of deposition (Brown, 1931; Borsellino et al., 2017; Shulmeister et al., 2018).
-	Perched deltas:
-	flat-topped accumulations of sediment with steeply-dipping fronts formed by the deposition of sediment into ice-marginal lakes. Perched deltas mark former glacial-lake levels that can be used to determine the ice margin position at the time of formation (Mannerfelt, 1945; Plouffe, 2000; Perkins and Brennand, 2015; Stroeven et al., 2016).
-	Eskers:
-	ridges of glaciofluvial sediment deposited by meltwater flowing through, beneath, or above ice. Eskers are assumed to form time-transgressively, close to the ice margin and are aligned roughly normal to the margin (Hebrand and Åmark, 1989; Brennand, 2000; [[Livingstone et al., 2015]]; Stroeven et al., 2016; Hewitt and Creyts, 2019).
-	Landform record of the CIS is dominated by glacial meltwater landforms rather than recessional moraines – Dulfer and Margold (2021) record only 97 CIS outlet glacier moraine ridges as opposed to ~18,000 lateral and submarginal meltwater channels.
-	Therefore, reconstruction is heavily reliant on meltwater landforms.

-	10Be cosmogenic nuclide and radiocarbon dating
-	Cosmogenic nuclide data offer a direct age on the time of emplacement of boulders and other lithic material by the ice sheet
3. Results
3.1 Glacial flowsets
-	Variations in flowset orientation and flowset cross-cutting relationships indicate ice flow direction shifted numerous times throughout the last glacial cycle.
Stage 1:
-	Small, discrete flowsets primarily made up of drumlins and crag-and-tails  - lineations are weathered and most are superimposed by younger flowsets
-	Independent advance of glaciers whilst the CIS is retreating. Advance into high-altitude ice free areas during cooling.
Stage 2:
-	Most stage 2 flowsets consist of lineations eroded into the bedrock on high mountain peaks (1500-1800 m asl)
-	Variable ice flow direction but overall patterns shows eastward flow.
-	Outlet glaciers respond to cooling and later advance over late glacial moraines, thereby creating cross-cutting lateral meltwater channels.
Stage 3:
-	Most numerous flowset.
-	Morphology is highly variale and ice-flow parallel lineations occur over a range of elevations (800 – 1900 m asl)

3.2 Ice retreat record
-	Meltwater channels frequently occur at high elevations (up to 1800 m asl) across the Cassiar and Omeneca Mountains – often occur as narrow incisions in mountain ridges.
5. Discussion
5.1 Deglaciation Style
-	To the east, channels eroded into mountain ridges by meltwater across the Cassiar and Omineca mountains indicate high elevation areas became deglaciated ahead of valleys, causing emergence of numerous nunataks around the periphery of the ice sheet during deglaciation.
-	However, suites of ice contact landforms, such as abundant lateral and submarginal meltwater channels, kame terraces, and perched deltas indicate CIS outlet glaciers actively retreated through the mountains of northern British Columbia, similar to the southern sectors of the CIS (Margold et al., 2013b; Perkins and Brennand, 2015; Sacco et al., 2017).
6. Future work
-	Challenges:
-	Understanding the ice sheet build-up stage and ice extent during MIS 3,4, and 6
-	Quantifying the rate of ice sheet thinning during deglaciation
-	Further quantitative dating of both the deglaciation of the central sector / last remnant of the CIS and of the Late Glacial readvance moraines.
7. Conclusions
-	Using distribution of CIS outlet moraines and meltwater landforms – the treat pattern is mapped -> proceeded westwards towards the Coast and Skeena mountains.
-	Some moraines are incised by lateral and submarginal meltwater channels formed along the CIS margins.
-	Relationship suggests that CIS outlet glaciers advanced in the Late Glacial, allowing the mapping of the maximum extent of the Eastern margin of the CIS during late stages of deglaciation – chronology suggest that this advance occurred during the Younger Dryas stadial – but more chronological controls required to confirm this.
