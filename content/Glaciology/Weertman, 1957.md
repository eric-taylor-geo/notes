# On the Sliding of Glaciers

Weertman (1957) proposes two mechanisms for ice to move past bumps in the glacier bed: regelation and enhanced creep.

# Regelation Flow:

Ice on the stoss side of bumps in the basal topography is under greater overburden pressure which lowers the pressure melting point. Dependent upon the thermal regime of the glacier, this can cause ice to melt on the stoss side and flow around the bump, driven by the pressure gradient over the bump. The lee-side has lower overburden pressure and the water refreezes.

The regelation of water releases latent heat on the lee-side, creating a thermal gradient across the bump. The transfer of latent heat back through the bump continues the process of melting on the stoss-side. The temperature gradient is shallow, making the transfer of latent heat back through the bump difficult. Thus, regelation flow works best for small bumps where heat transfer is most efficient.

### Enhanced Creep

The small increase in pressure on the stoss-side of a bump leads to locally enhanced stress. Because ice flow is raised to the power of 3 ([[Glen, 1955]]), a small change in the stress on the stoss side increases the velocity significantly. Thus, ice flows faster around the bump.

Larger bumps will result in greater disruptions to ice flow, causing increased stress and therefore, increased creep. Thus, enhanced creep works best for larger bumps.
