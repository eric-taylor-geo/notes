## Discussion

**Why do modelled and observed debris thicknesses increase towards glacier temini?**

- Analytical, numerical, and observed debris thickness patterns all increase down-glacier.
- Analytical model demonstrates that the glacier surface acts as a conveyor belt, advecting debris towards the terminus.
- Velocites on a glacier tend to decline monotonically towards 0 down-glacier - thus, more debris is transported into a fixed cell than is removed. Thus, debris thickens.
- Processes can result in the reduction of debris thickness - debris can fall into moulins and crevasses or become compacted.
	- The magnitude of compaction is outweighed by factors contributing to debris-thickness. 
	- At the extreme end, compaction could lead to a 40% reduction in thickness down-glacier e.g., [[Anderson and Anderson, 2016]]
- Areas with faster surface velocities will have thinner debris cover

**Why do debris patterns have a characteristic convex-concave form?**

- Convex-up debris patterns occur where surface velocities are large, velocity gradiets are small, and emergence rates are high.
	- Expect to occur close to the ELA where maximum ablation rates typically occur on debris-covered glaciers
- Concave-up debris patterns occur as emergence rates and surface velocities decline towards 0 - as you move down-glacier.
- Concave-up debris thickness patterns are therefore the product of monotonic decline of surface velocities towards the terminus

#### 5.5.1. Secondary effect on debris convexity: nonuniform glacier geometry

- Here, nonuniform glacier geometry refers to variability in glacier bed slope and width. Glaciers with multiple accumulation basins will tend to form ice-stream interaction medial moraines (e.g., Anderson, 2000; Gibson et al., 2017). These medial moraines result from the concentration of debris into septa. Velocity dominance still leads to convex- concave-up debris thickness patterns on glaciers with medial moraines (see the Ayutor-2, Baltoro, Kennicott, Koxkar, and Pasterze glaciers). Coalescing debris-covered tributaries may lead to across-glacier debris thickness variations (e.g., Baltoro glacier; Minora et al., 2015; [[Gibson et al., 2017]]), but individual flow lines will still evolve toward a convex-concave shape.
