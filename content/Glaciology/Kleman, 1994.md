# Preservation of landforms under ice sheets and ice caps

**Introduction**

-	Global climatic signals and ice sheet volumes can be extracted from deep-sea and ice-sheet cores 
-	d18O record
-	However, the evolution of individual palaeo-ice sheets cannot be traced using such data.
-	Understanding local glacial geology and morphology in formally glaciated areas is crucial for reconstructing changing configurations of palaeo-ice.
-	Much knowledge is gathered from the spatial analyses of glacial landform patterns.
-	Palimpsest of the landscape: question of to what extent small-scale landforms can survive prolonged ice-sheet coverage and to what extent they become altered (and under what conditions if so)
-	If we fail to recognise that landforms can survive successive waves of glacial activity, researchers are at risk of drawing incorrect conclusions and perceiving an array of landforms as deriving from the same glacial activity.


-	Basal sliding unable to occur under cold-base conditions (Boulton, 1972)
-	History:
-	Sugden (1968) attributed preservation of landforms to frozen bed 
-	Evidence for negligible erosion in northern Finland presented by Kaitanen (1969)
-	Korpela (1969): shallowly buried insterstadial organic deposits numerous in northern Finland.
-	Kujansuu (1975): both interstadial deposits and land forms (eskers) survived through the Late Weichselian glaciation in the area.
-	Schytt (1974) postulates that the Scandinavian ice sheet over the area may have been cold-based and non-eroding.
-	Further work has identified landforms that have survived ice-sheet overriding – thus challenging the originally standing assumption of ‘glacial erasure’ during successive glacial activity
-	In most cases: preservation largely explained by continuously cold-based conditions (but some evidence for wet-based conditions at times (>e.g. Kleman et al., 1992)
2. Evidence for preservation
2.1 Direct observation at glacier and ice cap margins
-	Goldthwait (1960) found undistributed patterned ground in a tunnel dug into the margin of a cold-based Greenland glacier. 
-	Ice Thickness of 40m and actively flowing without basal sliding.
-	Radiocarbon ages indicated that the site had been covered by ice for <400 years.
-	Primary example used in textbook literature
2.2 Morphostratigraphic evidence
-	Most supporting evidence for preservation is offered by landforms that are stratigraphically overlain by deposit that predate the last period of ice-sheet coverage at the site,
-	Mangerud et al. (1987): describe a distinct high-elevation marine terrace in western Svalbard overlain by basal till containing straited clasts.
-	Shell samples yield ages of ~36 ka. Terrace was orrun by an ice sheet.
-	Perhaps the strongest evidence of complete preservation of pre-last stadial landscapes stems from northeastern Sweden
-	Lagerback and Robertsson (1987) describe two overlapping regional landform system systems. The younger showing patches of S-N to SSW-NNE trending drumlines and the older one showing continuous NW-SE trending drumlins.
-	Older landscape was interpreted to have been preserved underneath two later, continuously cold-based ice sheets.
-	Although the number of Weichselian events is up to debate – this study (and subsequent ones) indicate that pre-Late Weichselian landforms were preserved.
-	Additionally, evidence that older channels were overrun by wet-based ice for a period of time.
2.3 Morphological Evidence
-	Ellesmere Island (England, 1986): documents wo lateral channels crossing an otherwise unaltered alluvial fan – little doubt that the subaerial nature of the fan and inconsequent nature of the channels prove later existence of ice at the location. However, little is known about the duration of the involved glacial events. England (1986) attributes this to cold-based conditions.
-	Other examples
2.4 Conditions under which preservation occurs
-	Most work attribute preservation to continuously cold-based conditions. 
-	However,[[ Boulton and Clark, 1990]] attribute drumlin preservation to ‘under ice-divide preservation” – makes sense – little lateral movement at division zones -> therefore little erosion.
-	Kleman (1994) believes that this explanation is insufficient and that some part of preservation must be as a result of cold-based conditions.
-	Because flow traces are present in sharply delineated sets which indicates strong contrasts between flow trace creation and no-change conditions. Under wet-based conditions and the influence of a migrating ice divide, we would expect the delineation to be much more blurred between sets as opposed to clear jumps.
-	Whilst it is not inconceivable that landforms can survive long periods under wet-based ice-divides, it would be unusual for the ice-divide to remain in place upon retreat – this would only occur if the glacier retreated symmetrically.
-	“To sum up: Full preservation of minor or fragile forms (boulder fields, paleo-surfaces, sharp-crested eskers, etc.) probably requires continuous frozen-bed conditions. Large-scale landforms such as large drumlins can probably be morphologically discernible ( something very different from full preservation) after a substantial time of wet based ice-overriding.” (p. 27)
3. The basal and marginal geomorphic systems
-	Consensus that landforms such as flutings and drumlins are indicators of web-based sliding.
-	Landforms commonly used in reconstructions of glacial dynamics can be described as belonging either to the wet-bed system of to the meltwater system (includes landforms such as eskers, channels, and ice dammed lake traces)
-	However, in contrast, there is not a set criteria for he classification of cold-based conditions -> therefore, seems justifiable to define a ‘dry-bed system- which compliments the wet-bed system and marginal meltwater system.
-	Three morphologic systems:
-	Dry bed system: no new landforms are created with the exception of the occasional glaciotectonic features. Frozen bed leads to a hiatus in landform development.
-	Wet bed system: basal sliding occurs. Flow orientated lineations are continuously produced, destroyed, and reorientated. Flow-aligned landforms are fossilised at the cessation of wet-based conditions (i.e., by deglaciation of the (re)freezing of the bed)
-	Marginal meltwater system: a coherent system of linear features (eskers and /or ice-directed drainage channels) is produced. Despite its small areal coverage, the meltwater system carries important information – forming a spatially coherent system it reflects the decay system and may be the only system doing so at points in time.
-	(Forth system is the supraglacial drift depositional system but appears to be of little importance for palimpsestual lineation landscapes.
-	‘Viewing rules’ can be deduce: interested in age, location, and type of information.
-	Simplest way to illustrate these relationships is to ‘invert’ the system: “to describe instead what happes to the geomorphologist’s ‘line of sight’ as it penetrates further and further back in time at specific points in space. What we seek is the intersection between line of sight and the timespace domains of landform formation” (p. 28)
-	In this sense, a dry-bed is ‘transparent’ – it (assuming preservation is true) preserves the landform allowing us to see back in time until we meet time of the landform’s initial development.
-	Wet-bed systems at ice-divide zones are opaque to semi-transparent.
