# Mountain glacier-to-rock glacier transition

## Introduction

- Paraglacial processes are becoming ever more importance in deglacierising mountain regions.
- Supraglacial debris coverage and thickness on glaciers may increase due to increased delivery in response to deglacial unloading and depressing following the exposure of glacially steepened rock walls following down wastage and/or retreat [[Hambrey et al., 2008]] [[Scherler et al., 2011]] [[Rowan et al., 2015]] *oh doesn't it feel so good when all the citations start linking !!!*
- Debris-covered glaciers will transform to rock glaciers [[Shroder et al., 2000]] [[Jones et al., 2018]] [[Knight et al., 2019]]
	- Implications on the preservation of rock glaciers as a future water supply due to their increased climate resilience [[Anderson et al., 2018]]
- Lots of gaps in understanding on whether glaciers will transition to rock glaciers

- Rock glaciers are lobate or tongue-shaped assemblages of ice-supersaturated debris and/or pure ice.
- Despite their semantic connection, there is a significant difference between rock glaciers and debris-covered glaciers

**Debris covered glaciers**

- Partially or wholly covered with a thin debris mantle
- Characterised by a topographically complex, spatially-chaotic mosaic of surficial features 
	- Hommocks, depressions, supraglacial melt ponds, frequent ice exposures (ice cliffs)
- Moves as a result of internal deformation. basal sliding, and soft bed deformation

**Rock glaciers**

- Moves as a result of internal deformation which predominantly occurs in a shear zone at depth
