# Positive degree-day sums in the Alps: a direct link between glacier melt and international climate policy

- Calculated degree-day factors indicate that future alpine glaciers will not be safe under further temperature increases permitted by international policy (Paris Agreement)

**Introduction:**

- Increasing awareness since the mid-1980s of increasing glacier melt associated with rising temperatures has stimulated the development of models to predict future glacier melt before it occurred / clearly visible (Hansen et al., 1981)
- Modelling of glaciers predict a sensitivity of mass balance between -0.2 to -2 m w.e. a-1 K-1
	- Model examples: (Ambach and Kuhn, 1985; Oerlemans and Hoogendoorn, 1989; Braithwaite and Zhang, 2000).
	- Smaller sensitivity for dry, continental environments and higher sensitivity for wet, maritime environments.
- Model predictions from the 1980s/90s can now be compared with observed data (possibility? Test model output with actual data to determine the accuracy of the model?
- Authors study mass balance variations in the Alps and explain them in terms of temperature changes.
- Mass balance changes appear to be associated with increases in summer temperatures since 1981.
- Alpine glaciers in study probably will not survive into future decades in their current form (and even more unlikely if temperatures rise further)
- Degree-day factor is “the temperature sensitivity of melt, reflecting the temperature sensitivities of the different terms in the energy balance with the largest contributions from sensible heat flux and incoming longwave radiation” (p. 901)
- “A positive degree-day sum is the sum of air temperatures above the melting point during a period (Cogley et al., 2011)” (p. 901)
	- Some past glacier-climate studies assume melt of snow and ice is proportional to the positive degree-day sum (e.g., Zingg, 1952; [[Braithwaite, 1985]], 1995, 2011; Hock, 2003; de Woul and Hock, 2005).
- Braithwait (1985) cite Finsterwalder and Schunk (1887) as the first mention of the degree-day principle in glaciology.
	-  However, authors cite von Sonklar (1858) as the first to recognise the principle in his understanding that summer melting must balance with winter snow accumulation at the snowline
- [[Reveillet et al., 2017]]: “a classical degree-day model is sufficient to stimulate the long-term glacier-wide mass balance”
	- Link to coursework: Degree day model is simplified and does not take various glacier dynamics into account (e.g., sliding). Therefore, analysing model outputs such as length and velocity may be inaccurate as these dynamics would be affected by processes not accounted for in this model. But, as per Reveillet et al (2017), analysing mass balance may provide a better overall picture.
- Paper hypotheses:
$$
\frac{∆B}{∆T}\ =\frac{∆B}{∆ADD} * \frac{∆ADD}{∆T}
$$
-  B: annual balance (m w.e. a-1)
- _ADD_: annual degree day sum (ºC d a-1)
- Most researchers calculate degree-day sums from observed data by summing the positive values of daily mean temperatures.
	- However, [[Arnold and MacKay, 1962]] point out the shortcomings of this

**3. Calculating ‘observed’ degree-day sums**

- Despite nobody *observing* degree-day sums directly, the adjective is used to denote degree-day sums derived directly from ‘observed’ data rather than from models (e.g., Braithwaite, 1985)
- Degree-day sums which are calculated using daily mean temperatures (e.g., [[Braithwaite, 1985]]) are limited for mean daily temperatures below zero may still have air temperatures above 0 for parts of the day ([[Arnold and MacKay, 1962]]).
- This limitation can be reduced by defining the daily degree-day sums using several temperature measurements per day: (M times per day) – positive degree day sum

**4. Degree-day sums at climate stations**

- Authors calculate monthly degree-day sums by adding up daily degree day sums at weather stations for all months with at least 25 days of data (using method 4)
- Authors extrapolate temperatures from stations using a vertical lapse rate of -0.006 degrees per meter.
- The median altitude was chosen for the annual balance at the median altitude is roughly equal to the annual balance for the whole glacier (Xie et al., 1996; Braithwaite, 2015).
- Median altitude is also available for over 1,000,000 glaciers in the Randolph Glacier Inventory

**9. Implications for climate policy**

-	Although global temperatures have not yet exceeded the limit allowed by the Paris Agreement glaciers all over the world show greatly increased mass loss under the climate of the early 21st century (Zemp and others, 2015, 2019; Marzeion and others, 2018, Thomson and others, 2021).
-	For the glaciers of the Alps, the regional temperature rise is greater than the global temperature rise, leading to very negative balances
-	Glaciers are therefore not ‘safe’ even under current global climate let alone that permitted by the Paris Agreement

**10. Summary and conclusions**

-	Tested new ways of calculating positive degree-day sums from observed temperatures that take into account diurnal temperature variatons.
-	Annual degree-day sums at high-altitude climate stations are highly correlated with summer temperature as first suggested in 1866 by von Sonklar
