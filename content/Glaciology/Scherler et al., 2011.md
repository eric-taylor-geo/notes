#  Spatially variable response of Himalayan glaciers to climate change affected by debris cover

- Snow and meltwater is important for human uses in the Himalayan region - contributing to drinking water, agriculture, and hydropower [[Barnett et al., 2005]]; [[Bookhagan and Burbank, 2010]]
- Climate change is expected to increase mountain-river discharge in the short term as ice melts, however discharge will be reduced in the long term [[Rees and Collins, 2006]]
- Thus, knowledge of the impacts of climate change on the cryosphere is crucial for predicting future water supplies [[Immerzeel et al., 2010]]
- Debris-cover influences the terminus dynamics and can modify a glacier's response to climate change
- Studies in the Himalaya have found several debris covered glaciers with stagnant glacier reaches that extend several km up-glacier from their snout [15],[16]
- The growth of meltwater ponds and surface lowering indicates that glaciers are shrinking - however, despite this, their fronts remain stable [17] -> perhaps due to debris.
	- This has also been observed in other regions [18], [19]

- Authors analyse 286 glaciers in the Himalaya
![[Pasted image 20240906224508.png]]
- Varying rates of retreat of glacier snouts between 2000 and 2008 using satellite imagery - varies between -80 and +40m per year
- Highest concentration of retreating glaciers (and rates) were int he western and northern Himalaya, and the West Kunlun Shan - where the proportion of debris-covered glaciers is also relatively low

- Authors assess the impact of debris cover on terminus dynamics by measuring surface velocity along the central flowline and determining the fraction of stagnant ice as a function of distance upstream from the snout
![[Pasted image 20240906224913.png]]
- The distribution of stagnant glaciers varires greatly in the Himalaya
- Debris is sourced from valley sides and thus, greater hillslope angles will increase the flux of rocky debris to glacier surfaces [20]
- Thus, the formation of debris-covered glaciers are linked to steep accumulation areas. >25%
- Yet, stagnant ice implies low gravitational driving stresses meaning that stagnant regions are often confined to regions with shallow gradients. <8%

- For example, in the Karakoram, accumulation areas are steep and there are frequent shallow termini - thus we would expect there to be stagnant debris-covered snouts based upon the logic above.
- However, in this region, stagnant glaciers are almost absent and thus, the advancing or stable glaciers must be due to different mass-balance regimes associated with their specific climatic setting.
	- Westerly jet stream over central Asia which transports moisture during winter has shifted and strengthened in the last few decades [21]
	- Tree-ring records show an increase in winter precipitation [22]
	- Summer temperatures have decreased perhaps due to increased precipitation and clouds [23]
- Thus, long term mass balancing is crucial - but is largely unavailable

- Debris cover, beyond a few centimeters in thickness, attenuates heat transfer from the atmosphere and slows a glacier's response to climate warming [[Mattson et al., 1993]], [[Østrem, 1959]], [[Ogilvie, 1904]]
- Debris over also influences the impact of natural or anthropgenic forcings relating to radiative heat transfer. For example, variations in insolation, atmospheric dust, or soot deposition [28] will have minor effects on mass balance if debris cover is already present.
- And on the contrary, they are more likely to have more significant effects on clean ice.

- Debris-cover must be considered in future predictions of water availability and global SLR.
