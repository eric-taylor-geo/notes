# The Extraordinary March 2022 East Antarctica “Heat” Wave. Part II: Impacts on the Antarctic Ice Sheet


## Conger Ice Shelf 

- Conger ice shelf collapse was coincident with the heat wave, raising questions on whether the two events were connected.
- Previous ARs have been connected to ice shelf collapses on the Antarctic Peninsula ([[Wille et al., 2022]]), but have not yet been linked to collapses in East Antarctica - though their associated cycles have been found to trigger calving events.
- Sea ice was at a satellite era record low in February 2022 ([[Turner et al, 2022]]), and by March 2022, Conger Ice Shelf's ice cliffs were sea-ice free, allowing swells to destabilise the ice shelf front ([[Wille et al., 2022]])

- Event timeline: (using Sentinel-1)
- 5th March 2022 - Calving event on the landward margin of the ice shelf
- 7th March 2022 - More calving events. Ice shelf loses its pinning point on Bowman Island.
- 12th March 2022 - Icebergs blown westward
- 14th March 2022 - Ice shelf still intact
- 15th March 2022 - AR landfall 
- 16th March 2022 - Entire collapse of the ice shelf
- 19th March 2022 - End of AR event

- Sentinel-1 backscatter only detects surface melt after the collapse on 17th March, thus the collapse was not due to hydrofracture, which differs from the collapse of Larsen B in 2002 ([[Walker et al., 2024]], [[Scambos et al., 2003]]).
- Consequently, the structural precursor for the ice shelf collapse appears to be the dual calving events between 5-7th March, during which the shelf became unpinned from Bowman Island.
- During this period, a stationary and intense cyclone occurred at the mouth of the ice shelf, driving strong surface winds.
- The associated wave swell, coupled with high winds, likely dislodged the recently unbuttressed ice shelf, leading to its abrupt collapse.

___

- Although this AR event and associated temperature anomalies were extraordinarily higher than anything previously observed over Antarctica, the autumn occurrence and impact over the coldest part of the continent led to heavy snowfall and a highly net-positive SMB event. In fact, this event helped make 2022 a record-breaking snowfall year for the entire AIS that resulted in an overall mitigation of global sea level rise by around ∼0.82 mm (296 Gt) ([[Wang et al., 2023]]; [[Clem et al., 2023]])
- Sea ice was at a satellite era record low in February 2022 ([[Turner et al, 2022]]), thus the impact on sea ice was minimal. Had the event occured at a different time,  the implications ons ea ice may have been stronger.
- Had the event occured over Thwaites Glacier region during peak summer, the resutls could potentially have been damaging for glacier stability.
- Quick melt and refreeze produces ice lenses -> link with Culberg

- Thus, the impacts of atmospheric rivers heavily depend on the timing and location of where they make landfall.
