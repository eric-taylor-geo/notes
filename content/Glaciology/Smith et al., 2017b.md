# Sub-ice-shelf sediments record history of twentieth-century retreat of Pine Island Glacier

- Grounding line positions in the pre-satellite era are poorly constrained
- Short observation record limits our understanding of whether current dynamics are in response to current or past forcing
- Although the ocean has been identified as a key driver of recent ice-sheet changes, the longer-term context—and, specifically, when the present imbalance was initiated—remains uncertain

- The El Niño record includes a major central Pacific warm event in the early 1940s, and analysis of ice cores shows that this was a time of exceptional warmth in West Antarctica.
- Earliest satellite imagery from 1973 shows a bump in the ice surface which was interpreted as the last point of grounding on the highest part of an ocean ridge - it disappeared several years later, suggesting that thinning was underway ([[Jenkins et al., 2010]])

- Sediment cores show two types of layering:
1. Stratified, gravelly, and sandy mid, which is produced by the melt-out of material, current transport, and sediment mass flows proximal to the grounded ice
2. Finer sediment, with a lack of coarse clasts, which indicates deposition that is far enough from the grounding line not to be affected by mass flows.

- Dating using Pb210 isotopes within sediments allows for a chronology of retreat
![[Pasted image 20250519123723.png]]
- Opening of an ocean cavity behind the ridge in response to warm water influx following a strong 1940s El Niño, which led to warming over West Antarctica
- The opening of an ocean cavity suggests that at least some part of the ridge became ungrounded at this time.
- By the 1970s, the ice sheet had become fully unground off the ridge, now lying on a retrograde bed. The grounding line has since continued to retreat.
- Even with conditions returning in ensuing decades, retreat continued.

**Conclusion:**

- First quantitative support for the idea that the changes we observe currently in the Amundsen Sea were triggered by a climatic anomaly in the late 1930s to 1940s, a link that has, until now, remained largely speculative.
- Despite a return to pre-1940s climatic conditions in the ensuing decades, thinning and glacier retreat have not stopped and are unlikely to be reversible without a major change in marine or glaciological conditions.
- Thus, a period of warming in the Antarctic shelf waters triggered a substantial change in the ice sheet, via the mechanism that we see today—that is, ocean-driven thinning and retreat of ice shelves leads to inland glacier acceleration and ice-sheet thinning
- Findings also suggest that ice-sheet retreat can continue even when the forcing reverts to its earlier state.
