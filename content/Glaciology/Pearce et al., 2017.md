# Glacier Reconstruction - Book Section

**Introduction**

-	Reconstructions are important for they provide information about palaeo-glacier changes over time scales much longer than the observational record permits.
-	Objectives are to reconstruct glacial dimensions and dynamics at a particular point in time , analyse rates of ice-mass fluctuations, understand past climates (Ballantyne, 2007; Clark et al., 2012)
-	This provides a better understanding of glacier and ice sheet response to past environmental changes, which, in turn, enables improved predictions of their behaviour in the future.
-	Geomorphological information about palaeo ice-mass dimensions and dynamics serve as key constraints for numerical ice sheet models (Stokes and Tarasov, 2010).
-	Mountain-scale glacier reconstructions are often produced in order to estimate former equilibirus line altitudes (ELAs) ([[Benn and Evans, 2010]])
-	Empirical data demonstrates that the ELA is determined by the relationship between air temperature and preciptation (Ohmura et al., 1992)
-	Therefore, if palaeo-temperatures are known independently, ELA estimates can be used to predict former preciptation totals.
Ice sheet reconstruction
-	Past ice extent, configuration, dynamics etc. can be reconstructed using a glacial inversion model that allows inferences to made from the existing geomorphological record.
-	This technique relies upon the understanding of the genesis of glacial landforms -> however, these are often debated and assumptions are made (difficult to observe subglacial landform formation)
-	For example, if we assume that drumlins are formed parallel to ice flow directions, they can be grouped into spatially coherent patterns known as ‘flow-sets’ (e.g., [[Boulton and Clark, 1990]]; Clark, 1994; Stokes et al., 2009).
-	Grouping is based of proximity, parallel concordance, and morphological similarly (Clark, 1999)
-	Similar criteria for subglacial ribs which form obliquely to ice flow direction (Hughes et al., 2014)
-	Ice streams are indicated by highly convergent flow patterns and elongated subglacial bedforms (Spagnolo et al., 2014; Margold et al., 2015)
-	Moraines are used to mark palaeo-ice margins and their orientation can be used to infer retreat.
-	Dating of sediments or boulders can constrain the relative chronology (Greenwood and Clark, 2009b; Clark et al., 2012)
-	Landforms created by glacial meltwater are also used to reconstruct ice sheets.
-	Eskers record patterns of channelised meltwater drainage, and their orientation is determined by the hydraulic potential gradient imposed by the ice sheet (Shreve, 1972; Storrar et al., 2014)
-	They likely form time-transgressively, close to the ice margin, during deglaciation (Hooke and Fastook, 2007; [[Livingstone et al., 2015]]).
-	Eskers can therefore be used to reconstruct ice margin retreat paths (Greenwood and Clark, 2009b)
-	Meltwater channels require classification as either ‘subglacial’ or ‘lateral’ before use in ice sheet reconstruction ([[Greenwood et al., 2007]])
-	Subglacial meltwater channels form in relation to the hydraulic potential gradient imposed by the ice sheet (Sugden et al., 1991), but, unlike eskers, are not restricted to areas near the ice margin
-	Lateral meltwater channels form as water flows around the margin of ice-masses, and are common where cold-based ice prevents englacial or subglacial water flow.
-	Lateral meltwater channels often form parallel sequences, indicating successive ice-margin positions during thinning ([[Greenwood et al., 2007]])
