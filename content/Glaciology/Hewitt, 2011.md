#  Modelling distributed and channelized subglacial drainage: the spacing of channels

-	The nature of subglacial drainage systems is important due to its effect on ice flow.
-	Water pressure in a drainage system effects ice velocity.
-	Water pressure is dependent upon the flux of meltwater to the glacier bed and the effective ness of the drainage system ([[Röthlisberger, 1972]])
-	Study models the interactions between channelised and distributed systems. 
-	Predict that cannels will grow headwards – observed on alpine glaciers (Hubbard and Nienow, 1997) -> also seen in reading in Nienow (1998)
-	Models that attempt to distinguish between channelised and distributed flow are forced to address the question of when a transition occurs, and also what the spacing is between channels.
Channelized flow
-	Most subglacial channels are incised into the ice, governed by the balance between creep closure and melting – R-channels ([[Röthlisberger, 1972]]; Nye, 1976)
-	Efficient at transporting water and operate at low water pressure, thereby drawing in surrounding water and forming arborescent networks, predominantly aligned in the down-glacier direction.
-	Assumptions made in the model instantaneous local energy transfer from dissipation to melting conduit walls.
Distributed flow

-	Water transport networks remains spread out over a large part of the glacier bed.
-	Various methods:
-	Flows through linked cavities which form on the lee side of bumps in the glacier bed ([[Kamb, 1987]])
-	Canals eroded into underlying sediment (Walder and Flower, 1994)
-	Thin patchy films (Alley, 1989)
-	Porous till or underlying aquifers
-	Due to various types of distributed network, all of which are highly dependent upon local conditions, the model describes the systems in a generalised sense – creating a microporous sheet.
Presence of eskers
-	The deposition of eskers indicates a certain level of channel stability over multiple years of ice retreat (though suggestion that eskers could form instantaneously during large floods (Shaw, 1994)). 
-	This suggests that only the largest, most dominant arteries in a drainage network will result in esker formation upon glacier retreat.
