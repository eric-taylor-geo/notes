# Channelized, distributed, and disconnected: spatial structure and temporal evolution of the subglacial drainage under a valley glacier in the Yukon 

**Introduction**

-	Speed and ice transport rates influenced by basal processes – modulate basal sliding rates.
-	Contribution rates to measured surface speeds:
-	Jakobshavn Isbræ (largest Greenland outlet glacier): 44-90% (Luthi et al., 2002; Ryser et al., 2014)
-	Antarctic ice streams: 69% (Engelhardt and Kamb, 1998)
-	Mountain glaciers: ~50% (e.g., Blake et al., 1994; Harper et al., 1998)
-	Seasonal variation as a result of changes in the subglacial drainage system which is associated with the seasonal input of surface meltwater which is routed to the glacial base via moulins and crevasses ([[Nanni et al., 2021]])
-	Changes in the subglacial drainage system have limited understanding and are the least observed glaciological phenomena.
-	The distribution of bedrock heterogeneities and the topography also modulate basal sliding (Alley et al., 1986; Alley, 1989). However, despite these varying significantly across different glaciers, they are unlikely to cause variations in sliding speeds at seasonal or shorter timescales at any given glacier.
-	Many recent subglacial drainage models involve a subglacial system that spans the entire glacial bed – they have success in reproducing observed variations in glacier velocities and the transition for a cavity-like drainage system to a channel-like drainage system in the summer as water discharge increases from meltwater input.
-	However, these models are unable to reproduce certain direct borehole observations (Flowers, 2015) -> Rada and Schoof (2018) show that borehole observations which do not follow model predications can be as a result of existing in hydraulically isolated portions of the glacier bed.
-	Other inconsistences:
-	Large and sustained water pressure gradients over short distances
-	Development of widespread areas of high water pressure during winter
-	Boreholes displaying persisting water pressures that exceed the overburden pressure from ice
-	Displaying mutually anti-correlated diurnal water pressure variations.
-	Water pockets: isolated water volume embedded into the ice – can range from large subglacial conduits to centimetre scale patches of water.
-	Large expanses of (multiple) water pockets decrease the significance of distributed / channelised networks on glacial flow.
