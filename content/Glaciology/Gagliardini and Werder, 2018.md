# Influence of increasing surface melt over decadal timescales on land-terminating Greenland-type outlet glaciers

## Abstract

- Surface melt on the GrIS has increased both in intensity and spatial extent over the past few decades
- Some melt probably is routed to the glacier bed, enhancing glacier sliding speeds, advecting more ice to the ablation area.
- Contributing to increasing rates of thinning and retreat of land-terminating glaciers in Greenland.
- Changes in ice flow conditions will influence crevassing and the routes of water to the glacier bed.
- Results of paper's model conclude that crevasse distribution is likely to extend further upstream, allowing water to reach the bed at higher elevations, increasing the advection of ice into the ablation area.

## Results

- Expansion of crevassed area up-elevation, activating moulins.
- Effects of surface melt influence ice flow over a large area - thus changes in basal conditions in the crevassed area can and do influence upstream ie velocities.
- This mechanism expands the crevassed area.
- Moulins are only activated in the crevassed area - thus no surface meltwater reaches the bed upstream of the crevassed area.

## Discussion

- Crevassed areas of land-terminating glaciers will move further inland as the area affected by surface melt increases.
- The effect of increased surface melt on glacier dynamics is varied and dependent upon the efficiency of the sub-glacial hydrological system - efficient (channelised) or inefficient (distributed)
- Model shows that drainage systems will remain inefficient for the most part - the area occupied by channels increases at a much lower rate than the area of the bed accessed by surface water.
- Increased discharge through the inefficient system lowers the effective pressure - enhancing flow velocity 

- Findings contrast with observation-based study by [[Poinar et al., 2015]] who argue that crevassing will **NOT** extend inland.
- Findings agree with observations of [[Yang and Smith, 2016]]
- [[Christoffersen et al., 2018]] [[Hoffman et al., 2018]] suggest that drainage of supraglacial lakes is responsible for inland migration of crevassing - however, author's model does not even take supraglacial lakes into account and yet inland migration is still observed suggesting that migration can occur without drainages.

**Assumptions / Missing Processes:**

- Assumes that moulins open instantaneously - unrealistic. Small crevasses may also not survive a single winter - model does not account for crevasse closing thus the rate at which crevasses propagate upstream is likely to be overestimated
- Model does not consider supraglacial lakes and their drainage - these are a primary control of moulin formation [[Christoffersen et al., 2018]] [[Hoffman et al., 2018]]
- Model does not consider the retention of surface meltwater within firn (or I assume, refreezing as internal accumulation)
	- 45% of meltwater produced at higher elevations may be stored in firn through capillary forces or by refreezing [Steger et al., 2017]
- Water reaching cold based beds may simply refreeze and not contribute to subglacial hydrological systems.
- Increased basal sliding will increase frictional heat, moving any transition zones between temperate and cold bases up-stream (is up-stream the term for these types of glacier to replace up-glacier ?)
- However, these processes are unlikely to counteract the upstream propagation of crevasse areas - thus model results are still robust.

## Conclusions

- Land-terminating glaciers represent 10% of the total ice volume above floatation of the Greenland Ice Sheet (personal citation tf)
- This upstream migration of crevasses will allow new meltwater to reach the bed farther inland and hence ice flow to be influenced by basal hydrology over larger areas. As a consequence, the mean annual velocity at these higher elevations will increase and more ice will be transported to the frontal regions where the surface melt is highest, therefore, increasing the total mass loss of these land-terminating glaciers
