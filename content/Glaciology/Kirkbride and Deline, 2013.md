# The formation of supraglacial debris covers by primary dispersal from transverse englacial debris bands

- Important to understand debris emergence as glaciers shrink [[Deline, 2005]] [[Scherler et al., 2011]]
- "Because many debris covers are increasing by emergence of englacially-transported debris from ice which is at least several decades old, a glaciological explanation of debris-cover variation growth is required in addition to the geomorphological explanation based on a recent increase in extra-glacial debris input"
- Empirical evidence from Glacier d'Eestelette, France

Paper has two main aims:

1. What are the glaciological determinants of the thickness and rate of spreading of supraglacial debris delivered from englacial transport
2. What glaciological factors determine the sensitivity of glaciers to developing and maintaining debris covers?
## Primary and Secondary Debris Dispersal on Glacier Surfaces

- **Primary dispersal:** supraglacial dispersal pof debris across melting ice surfaces to the migration of englacial debris septa
	- Delivers debris to the glacier surface based on patterns which are predetermined by glaciological controls on the glacier
- **Secondary dispersal**: gravitational processes that distribute debris locally down slopes of ice-cored relief features formed by differential ablation
	- Thus, secondary dispersal modifies primary pattern
- Theoretical treatments of debris-cover formation through melt-out as uniform over the glacier area - [[Konovalov, 2000]]
	- *Though I think [[Anderson and Anderson, 2018]] provide a more in depth study*
	- *Hmm actually not really - mostly grounded as well in observation - through they do say that moraine bands will also develop into the convex-concave up pattern*
- Empircal studies [[Boulton, 1970]] [[Goodsell et al., 2005]] find that englacial debris is contained in discrete septa (debris-rich bands) from a variety of origins
	- From [[Anderson and Anderson, 2018]]"
		- " Here, nonuniform glacier geometry refers to variability in glacier bed slope and width. Glaciers with multiple accumulation basins will tend to form ice-stream interaction medial moraines (e.g., Anderson, 2000; Gibson et al., 2017). These medial moraines result from the concentration of debris into septa. Velocity dominance still leads to convex- concave-up debris thickness patterns on glaciers with medial moraines (see the Ayutor-2, Baltoro, Kennicott, Koxkar, and Pasterze glaciers). Coalescing debris-covered tributaries may lead to across-glacier debris thickness variations (e.g., Baltoro glacier; Minora et al., 2015; [[Gibson et al., 2017]]), but individual flow lines will still evolve toward a convex-concave shape."
- The reduced ablation below medial morain reduces surface lowering causing morain relief to increase downstream through differential melting of the glacier surface
- Lateral debris motion down the moraine slopes causes medial moraine to widen, increasing the spatial extent of retarded ablation
- Upon the coalescence of multiple adjacent moraines, complete debris cover forms [[Anderson, 2000]]
