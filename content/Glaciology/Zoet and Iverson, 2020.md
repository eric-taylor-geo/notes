# A slip law for glaciers on deformable beds

-	The slip relation most widely applied in glacier-flow models is derived from considering ice resting on an irregular rock surface (i.e., hard bed). In this scenario, movement of ice past rock bumps by regelation and by creeping flow causes slip resistance to increase with slip velocity—the equivalent of viscous drag at the bed
-	In modeling studies in which effects of both viscous and Coulomb slip laws are compared, ice-loss and sea level–rise projections are acutely sensitive to the form of the slip law, with viscous and Coulomb relations yielding distinct glacier responses
-	Most shear deformation of the till occurs in a zone close to the ice-bed interface. 
-	Approximately 35% of ice motion is caused by till deformation
-	Most measurements beneath modern glaciers, which indicate that basal motion occurs primarily at the surfaces of till beds or by till shear to only shallow depths (Kuffey Textbook)
-	Propose a slip law which takes into account deformation – again like many other models, this law agrees with some observations of glacier surface velocity.
