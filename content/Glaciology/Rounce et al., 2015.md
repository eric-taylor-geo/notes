**Energy Balance Model**

- Uses a state-state surface energy balance model for a debris-covered glacier:

$$
R_n(T_s) + H(T_S) + LE(T_S) + P(T_S) + Q_C(T_S) = 0
$$
$R_n$: net radiation flux
$H$: heat flux supplied by rain
$Q_c$ ground heat flux
- See [[Rounce and McKinney, 2014]] for full description of net radiation and sensible heat fluxes
- Latent heat flux is difficult to determine without knowledge of debris moisture contents or the relative humidity at the debris surface
- Paper estimates latent heat flux using [[Nicholson and Benn, 2006]]
- Paper estimates heat flux by precipitation using [[Reid and Brock, 2010]]
- Conductive heat flux at the surface and debris-ice interface ere estiamted using [[Reid and Brock, 2010]]
- Surface temperature was computing using [[Reid and Brock, 2010]] - iterative approach using Newton-Raphson
- In the case of snow, a snowmelt model was used [[Fujita and Sakai, 2014]]

## Modelled Results

**Model Calibration**

- Three methods were used to estimate latent heat flux: Rain, RH100, Dry
- The performance of each model was assessed using the total sum of squares and R2 correlation coefficients
- There is good agreement between modeled and measured temperature

**Modeled ablation rates**

- 'Differences in melt and the sensitivity to thermal conductivity highlight the importance of properly estimating/measuring the thermal conductivity of the debris cover'
## Conclusions

- "All three models performed well, as a result of the calibration procedure, which allowed variations in the lack of latent heat flux to be compensated for by adjusting the debris properties. However, the LERain and LERH100 models yielded more reasonable values of latent heat fluxes. **This suggests that in a data-scarce region either the LERain or LERH100 model may be used if relative humidity or precipitation data are available**"
- Ablation rates are most sensitive to variations in thermal conductivity, followed closely by albedo and surface roughness
