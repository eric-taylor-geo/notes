#  Subglacial drainage processes at a High Arctic polythermal valley glacier

-	Dye-tracer experiments over two summer melt seasons at polythermal John Evans Glacier, Canada.
-	Dye was injected into several moulins and traced to a single subglacial outflow.
-	Hydrographs of tracer concentration suggest a distributed system in late June.
-	Development and maintenance of a channelised network requires sustained high rates of surface melting.
-	Warm summer (2000) saw drainage become channelled and maintained over the summer season.
-	Increased opening of moulins over the melt season.
-	Saw the evolution into a channel, incised upwards into ice by 26th June
-	Channel formation initially driven by rapid drainage of supraglacial stored meltwater into 5 moulins.
-	Upon the draining of supraglacial stores, channels were maintained by melt-induced surface run off.
-	Colder summer (2001) saw drainage remain mostly distributed.
-	Mean drainage-system cross sectional area can be calculated via discharge and water velocity (A = Q/u) -> Neinow et al. (1998)
-	Mean cross sectional area was higher in 2001 than 2000, indicating limited channel development.
Implications:
-	Study finds rapid formation of channels in a polythermal glacier which contradicts Rabus and Echelmeyer’s (1997) suggestion that polythermal glaciers will have poorly developed subglacial drainage systems.
-	Once the system becomes hydraulically efficient (channelised), surface velocity responses to supraglacial hydrological forcing (melt) is impeded -> consistent with other studies
-	Zwally et al. (2002) found that channel formation on the Greenland ice sheet was restricted due to such a large overburden pressure -> therefore the initial surface velocity increase experienced at the onset of rapid melt may have been prolonged, accelerating icesheet movement over a longer part of the melt season.
-	The speed at which channels can form affects the duration of rapid glacier flow – rapid formation limits this period.
-	Therefore, coupling between subglacial hydrology and glacier dynamics may play a smaller role in High Arctic glacier responses to climate forcing than those of the Greenland ice sheet (Zwally et al., 2002)
-	The level of arborescence is depending upon the volume of melt (Hart et al., 2022) (separate reading)
