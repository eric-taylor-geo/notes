# Persistent, extensive channelized drainage modeled beneath Thwaites Glacier, West Antarctica

- [[Smith et al., 2017a]] suggest that channels beneath Thwaites Glacier are ephemeral, only forming during subglacial lake drainage events.
- However, this study's models find that channels are persistent and extensive beneath Thwaites Glacier, extending 100-200 km inland.
- Distributed-only networks result in unrealistically high water pressures within 100 km of the terminus. Thus, a channelised network is necessary to explain the observed radar speckle content.
- This is a surprising result because geometric and hydrological conditions at Thwaites Glacier are unfavourable to the development of subglacial channels, given the lack of a sufficient surface meltwater input to reach a critical discharge rate to form channels ([[Schoof, 2010]]).
- The presence of sufficient discharge could be due to Thwaites' large catchment size, funnel-like geometry, and high basal melt rates ([[Joughin et al., 2009]])
- High basal friction melt rates in the terminal 100 km could also result in increased channelisation close to the grounding line ([[Joughin et al., 2009]])

- This result also contrasts with Smith et al. ([[Smith et al., 2017a|2017]]) because an extensive channelised system may not allow the isolation of subglacial lakes.
- Thus, bringing together the evidence for channelisation, but also ephemeral channel formation to permit lake activity, implies the presence of hydraulically disconnected portions of the bed  - which is known to occur beneath alpine and Greenland glaciers ([[Rada Giacaman and Schoof, 2023]]).
- Without the presence of a seasonal melt cycle forcing a reconfiguration of the hydrological system ([[Schoof, 2010]]), disconnected regions can remain disconnected year-round, permitting disconnected water to pool into lakes, which drain when they periodically exceed their hydropotential seals ([[Fowler, 1999]]; [[Smith et al., 2017a]]).
- Such drainage events could act as similar catalysts for drainage network reconfigurations as the seasonal melt cycles of alpine and Greenland glaciers.
- *Indeed, Siegfried et al. ([[Siegfried et al., 2016|2016]]) noted two draining events of Lake Mercer beneath Mercer Ice Stream in late 2012 and late 2014 had differing impacts on ice dynamics, suggestive of the change in the subglacial hydrological system between drainage events*

- Modelled channels at the grounding line show that they coincide with the areas of the highest melt, suggesting that subglacial discharge plumes could amplify local submarine melting through turbulent heating and the entrainment of warm water, such as mCDW.
- An already efficient channelised network could accommodate the additional flux from lake drainage events, which may explain why Smith et al. ([[Smith et al., 2017a|2017]]) only observed a minor increase (<10%) in Thwaites Glacier's velocity during drainage - though this would require the previously disconnected lake to efficiently connect to the main channelised network efficiently.
