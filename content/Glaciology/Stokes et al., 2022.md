# Response of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet to past and future climate change

- East Antarctic Ice Sheet contains 52 meters of sea level equivalent but is typically seen as being less vulnerable to climate change than West Antarctic Ice Sheet or Greenland Ice Sheet.
- Recent mass losses in regions of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet call for the need to re-evaluate its sensitivity to climate change.
- Ice loss from Antarctica has exceeded mass gains, resulting in a contribution to SLR.
- Largest imbalance is in the West Antarctic Ice Sheet (holds 5.3m (order of magnitude lower than East Antarctic Ice Sheet)) and has lost 2,000 GT of ice between 1992 and 2017 - 6mm contribution to global mean sea level [[Shepherd et al., 2018]]
- Imbalance is attributed to the intrusion of warm modified Circumpolar Deep Water which is promoting melting on the underside of floating ice shelves, resulting in thinning, grounding line retreat, and increased ice discharge [[Pritchard et al., 2009]] [[Pritchard et al., 2012]]
- MISI could occur on retrograde beds [[Schoof, 2007b]]

- Despite the acknowledgement of the West Antarctic Ice Sheet's vulnerability since the 1970s, the vulnerability of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet has been generally viewed as being less sensitive to ocean-climate warming.
	- View particularly due to the fact that large parts of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet have persisted for millions of years [[Sugden et al., 1995]]
- However recent observations suggest that sensitivity may be higher than previously thought - yet this is often shrouded in significant uncertainties 
- Mass loss from some marine-based catchments e.g., Wilkes Land - attributed to CDW intrusion  - thinning, MISI, etc.
- Terrestrial regions of the East Antarctic Ice Sheet are gaining mass throough increased accumlatiom (e.g., Dronning Maud Land) - however there is large inter-annual variability
- The observational time series of accumulation and ice discharge is too short to distinguish whether recent trends are significant (and promoted by anthropogenic activity) or whether they represent natural variability in the ocean-climate system [[King et al., 2020]] https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2020GL087493

**Past response to warm periods**

- Bed topography affected previous periods of retreat [[Livingstone et al., 2012]], which may explain regional asynchronicity
- Geomorphological evidence on the sea floor of the Mertz Trough, Prydz Channel and western Ross Sea, for example, indicates marked accelerations in grounding-line retreat across overdeepened basins [[Livingstone et al., 2012]]
- Retreat slowed by elevated bed tropography
- Isostatic rebound in the Weddell and Ross seas may have also excerted a stabilising effect [[Kingslake et al., 2018]]
- Past retreats triggered by a combination of ocean, atmosphere, and sea-level changes [[Noble et al., 2020]] - controlled timing and spatial pattern
- During the most recent deglaciation, oceanic warming became an increasingly important control on marine-based retreat - modified CDW intrusion
- Palaeodata of grounding line retreat provides context for constraining modern day observations. -> essay link here

**Recent ocean conditions and ice dynamics**

- Evidence and modelling from past warm periods suggest that three Eastern basins have enhanced sensitivty:
	- Recovery Subglacial Basin
	- Wilkes Subglacial basin
	- Aurora Subglacial Basin
- Terrestrial regions respond mainly to atmospheric forcing
- Marine-based sectors respond to both -> both link into MISI
- Therefore, understanding oceanic conditions and bathymetry are important to understand the East Antarctic Ice Sheet's dynamics

- Strong easterly winds around most of East Antarctica drive onshore Ekman transport of cold, fresh Antarctic Surface Water.
- Piles up cold, fresh water over the continental shelf, which can induce downwelling circulation (Antarctic Slope Current) which restricts the intrusion of CDW

- Study highlights three types - determined by the strength of the easterlies
	- Fresh (most of Antarctica)
	- Dense (e.g., Ross Sea, Adélie Coast, Pryd Bay)
	- Warm (e.g., Wilkes Land) recent warming close to Shirase Glacier, Dronning Maud Land, has been detected, enabled by weaker polar easterlies [[Hirano et al., 2020]]

- Observational period is short (few decaedes) and few measurements are available 
- Makes detecting long-term shelf warming difficult
- Though warm water has been detected around several main outlet glaciers [[Greenbaum et al., 2015]]  (https://www.nature.com/articles/ngeo2388), coinciding with high basal melt rates -> inducing thinning and reduced buttressing, thereby promoting discharge [[Gudmundsson et al., 2019]] (https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2019GL085027)
- Wilkes Land overlies the Aurora Subglacial Basin and has been referred to as East Antarctica's 'weak underbelly' [[Miles et al., 2016]] https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.1501350 [[Pelle et al., 2020]] https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2019GL086821
- Many studies remain unstudied yet are proximal to warm intrusions of CDW [[Ribeiro et al., 2021]] https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2020JC016998
- Recovery Subglacial Basin is drained by several large glaciers with retrograde slopes (Recovery, Baily, Slessor) [[Diez et al., 2018]] https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/2017GL076601
- -> therefore highlights their theoretical vulnerability to future ocean warming [[Golledge et al, 2017]] https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/2016GL072422 and MISI hypothesis. 
	- However, no present changes in ice dynamics [[Gardner et al., 2018]] https://tc.copernicus.org/articles/12/521/2018/
- Summary:
	- Most EA outlet glaciers have no significant change in velocity or discharge over recent decades [[Gardner et al., 2018]]
	- This includes those draining Wilkes and Recovery Subglacial Basins
	- Some draining ASB in Wilkes Land -> akin to WAIS - potential for this to continue or increase

## Surface Mass Balance

- Uncertainties in mass balance for the EAIS arise from uncertainties in mass input rather than ice discharge. Because surface melt does not runoff, it plays little contribution to mass balance (for the time being)
- Current mean surface mass balance is +1,246 Gt per year (1980-2018) [[Wang et al., 2021]] https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1029/2020GL090961
- SMB dominated by snowfall -> rainfall, sublimation, runoff at least an order of magnitude smaller [[Lenaerts et al., 2019]]  https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1029/2018RG000622
- Despite East Antarctica being very dry (0.05-0.14m water equivalent) [[Lenaerts et al., 2019]], its shear size generates a large surface mass balance
- The absence of in situ measurements, especially in the interior, means that assessments of SMB rely on atmospheric datasets, analyses, and regional climate models. Without in situ measurements, reanalyses are poorly constrained -> large uncertainties
- Despite uncertainties, studies using different reanalysis products broadly agree in the magnitude of inter-annual variability in snowfall (but means are different) as well as recent trends.

**Ponding**

- Role of firn air content -> high firn air content allows the reabsorption of surface melt [[Kuipers Munneke et al., 2014]] https://doi.org/10.3189%2F2014JoG13J183
- Recent ponding of meltwater on EA ice shelves -> risk of ice-shelf collapse through hydrofracturing [[Stokes et al., 2019]] https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-019-50343-5
- The presence of surface meltwater features (streams, lakes, slush) in the grounding zone of numerous EA ice shelves is indicative of insufficient porosity for drainage into the firn.
- Firn air content can be approimated using a liquid-to-solid ratio defined by the amount of surface melt (and rain technically, albeit rare) divided by snowfall 
	- Rainfall: [[Vignon et al., 2021]] https://doi.org/10.1029%2F2020GL092281
- Ratio is <25% on most EA ice shelves, meaning that snowfall is 4x larger than liquid water fluxes; therefore, the firn is porous and able to accommodate surface melt.
- Exceptions:
	- Amery Ice Shelf - closer to 80% (peninsula levels) and eastern Dronning Maud Land are around 40% -> these ice shelves have lots of supraglacial lakes but their physical confinement protects them from widespread hydrofracture :))
- Presently, less than 1% of vulnerable ice-shelf areas support lakes :)) [[Lai et al., 2020]] https://doi.org/10.1038%2Fs41586-020-2627-8
